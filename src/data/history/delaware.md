# Delaware — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
Delaware is a state-monopoly gambling jurisdiction with the **Delaware Lottery** as the central administrative authority across most verticals. Legal verticals: the **Delaware Lottery** (Powerball, Mega Millions, scratch, daily numbers; oversight by the **Delaware Lottery Office** in the Department of Finance); **video lottery / casino gaming** at three racinos — **Delaware Park Casino**, **Bally's Dover Casino & Resort** (formerly Dover Downs), and **Harrington Raceway & Casino** — operating under the Delaware Lottery's licensing umbrella; **table games** at all three since 2010; **pari-mutuel horse racing** (thoroughbred at Delaware Park, harness at Dover and Harrington); **retail sports betting** (since June 5, 2018 — the **first state after Nevada** to offer full single-game sports wagering post-PASPA) and **online sports betting** (since December 27, 2023 via Rush Street Interactive / BetRivers); **online casino (iGaming)** since October 31, 2013 — one of the first three U.S. states to authorize regulated online casino; and **charitable bingo, raffles, instant bingo, and Texas Hold'em charitable tournaments**. There are no commercial casinos outside the three racino umbrella and no tribal gaming (no federally recognized resident tribes operating Class III). The **Delaware Lottery Office** and the **Delaware Division of Gaming Enforcement (DGE)** are the principal regulators.[^1][^2]

## How Delaware got here
Colonial Delaware (proprietary under Pennsylvania until 1776) followed the Mid-Atlantic pattern of public lotteries for civic purposes. The **1897 Delaware Constitution** — still the governing document — included Article II § 17 prohibiting most gambling but permitting lotteries "under State control." Critically, when most U.S. states banned lotteries following the Louisiana Lottery scandal, **Delaware and Louisiana were the only two states without a constitutional lottery prohibition by 1890** — a posture that would prove decisive in the late 20th century. The 1933 Horse Racing Act authorized pari-mutuel wagering under the Delaware Racing Commission; Delaware Park opened June 26, 1937, Dover Downs in 1969, Harrington's pari-mutuel meet in 1946, and Brandywine Raceway operated harness racing 1953-1989. Delaware has never had legal greyhound racing.[^3][^4]

The **Delaware Lottery** was established by the General Assembly on May 31, 1974 (Governor Sherman W. Tribbitt). The first game launched in March 1975 — **"LOTTO Superfecta,"** a weekly game keyed to live horse races at Brandywine and Harrington — a unique racing-linked lottery format. Critically, the Lottery's 1974 statutory mandate was broad enough to include **sports lottery games**. In **1976**, the Lottery briefly offered **NFL "Scoreboard" parlay cards**; wagering was discontinued after the first year, but the **statutory authorization remained in place** — a fact that proved decisive when Congress passed PASPA in 1992. Delaware joined Powerball in 1991 and Mega Millions on January 31, 2010.[^5]

Casino-style gaming arrived via the **1994 VLT Act** (Horse Racing Redevelopment Act, signed by Governor Thomas Carper), which authorized **video lottery terminals** at the three racetracks. VLT slots launched in December 1995 at Delaware Park and Dover Downs and early 1996 at Harrington. **Table games** were authorized in 2009 and launched in May 2010 at all three racinos. **iGaming** was authorized by the **Delaware Gaming Competitiveness Act of 2012** (HB 333, signed June 28, 2012); operations went live October 31, 2013 in partnership with 888 Holdings. Delaware's PASPA grandfathering meant the Lottery resumed parlay-card wagering in September 2009, though *Office of the Commissioner of Baseball v. Markell* (3d Cir. 2009) limited Delaware to the pre-existing parlay format. When *Murphy v. NCAA* fell on **May 14, 2018**, Delaware moved instantly: **full single-game retail sports betting launched June 5, 2018**, just three weeks later. **Online sports betting and iGaming** were re-procured in December 2023 with **Rush Street Interactive (BetRivers)** replacing 888 Holdings as the platform vendor.[^4][^6][^7]

## Regulatory framework today
The **Delaware Lottery Office** ([delottery.com](https://www.delottery.com/)), within the Department of Finance, is the licensing authority for the state lottery, video lottery (slots), table games, sports lottery (sports betting), and iGaming. The **Delaware Division of Gaming Enforcement** ([dge.delaware.gov](https://dge.delaware.gov/)) provides investigations, compliance, and licensee oversight. The **Delaware Thoroughbred Racing Commission** and the **Delaware Harness Racing Commission** regulate pari-mutuel racing. **Charitable bingo, raffles, and Texas Hold'em charitable tournaments** are administered by the **Delaware Board of Charitable Gaming** under the Department of Finance ([finance.delaware.gov/charitable-gaming](https://finance.delaware.gov/charitable-gaming/)). Problem-gambling treatment is coordinated by the **Delaware Division of Substance Abuse and Mental Health (DSAMH)**, and the **Delaware Council on Gambling Problems** (DCGP) — the NCPG state affiliate — operates the helpline **1-888-850-8888**.[^8]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
Delaware administers a centralized statewide Voluntary Self-Exclusion program through the **Delaware Lottery Office**. The program is one of the older and more clearly documented VSE rosters in the United States, dating from the 1995 launch of the VLT casino sector and updated with each subsequent vertical expansion (table games 2010, iGaming 2013, sports betting 2018, online sports betting 2023).

**Where to enroll**: enrollment is **in-person only** at the Delaware Lottery Office: **1575 McKee Road, Suite 102, Dover, DE 19904-1903**. The required application form is obtained by calling **(302) 744-1669** or in person at the Lottery Office. Self-Exclusion Appointments run Monday-Friday, 8:00 AM-4:00 PM (excluding state holidays). The application **must be submitted in person at the Lottery Office** — there is no fully online enrollment portal for the Delaware list, a meaningful access barrier compared to states with web-based enrollment like Colorado or Connecticut. The program is described on the Lottery's site at **[delottery.com/More/iGaming/Self-Exclusion-Program](https://www.delottery.com/More/iGaming/Self-Exclusion-Program)** and **[delottery.com/More/Table-Games/Self-Exclusion-Program](https://www.delottery.com/More/Table-Games/Self-Exclusion-Program)**.[^9]

**Duration options**: registrants choose **one year, five years, or lifetime**. The lifetime option is **permanent and cannot be removed**. The one-year and five-year options bind the registrant for **at least** the chosen term.[^9]

**Scope**: the Delaware Lottery's Self-Exclusion list is **comprehensive across all licensed verticals**: all gaming activities at the three licensed video-lottery agents (**Delaware Park, Bally's Dover, and Harrington Raceway & Casino**) — slots, table games, and retail sportsbooks — **plus** the iGaming websites and the **Lottery's iGaming/Sports Mobile app**. A single enrollment covers all three properties and all licensed online platforms; Delaware's small operator footprint and Lottery-monopoly structure permit this single-list architecture in a way that more fragmented states cannot match. The list does **not** automatically cover **Delaware Lottery retail ticket purchases** at convenience stores (a known gap), and does not cover out-of-state mobile sportsbooks accessed from Delaware.[^9]

**Removal**: at the end of the chosen one-year or five-year term the registrant remains on the list until they take affirmative removal action; the list does **not** auto-expire and does **not** auto-renew. The **lifetime** selection cannot be removed at any time. Re-entry after the term requires Lottery Office processing.[^9]

**Penalties for breach**: a self-excluded person who **knowingly enters a gaming area** is **guilty of a Class A misdemeanor** under Delaware law — among the more punitive enforcement provisions of any U.S. state VSE program. Any winnings won by a self-excluded person are forfeited, and the player cannot recover losses. Licensees that knowingly permit play face Lottery Office administrative sanction and Division of Gaming Enforcement investigation.[^9]

**Multi-state participation**: Delaware is a founding signatory to the **Multi-State Internet Gaming Agreement (MSIGA)** along with Nevada, New Jersey, Michigan, Pennsylvania, and West Virginia — primarily for online poker liquidity pooling rather than for cross-state self-exclusion. The self-exclusion list itself is administered by Delaware and is not interoperable with MSIGA partner-state lists at this writing. Exact 2026 enrollment counts are **[unconfirmed]** in publicly published Delaware Lottery reports; the program has operated continuously since 1995 with iterative expansion. DCGP helpline **1-888-850-8888** is the entry point for counselor-assisted enrollment.[^8][^9]
