Pennsylvania's gambling history holds the most extreme positions a U.S. state can take. William Penn's "Frame of Government" of 1682 criminalized cards, dice, and lotteries. Yet colonial Pennsylvania ran lotteries to fund Philadelphia's defenses, Christ Church's steeple, and the 1751 founding of Pennsylvania Hospital, with Benjamin Franklin as the chief promoter. The Commonwealth banned lotteries by statute in 1833, and the 1873 Constitution made the prohibition explicit. That ban held for 138 years. A 1971 constitutional amendment finally lifted it. The Pennsylvania Lottery opened March 7, 1972 under a unique rule: net proceeds are dedicated entirely to programs for older Pennsylvanians — prescription assistance, transit, property-tax rebates, and aging services. Pari-mutuel horse racing had been authorized earlier, in 1959.

Modern legal gambling came in a single transformative act. The Race Horse Development and Gaming Act of 2004, signed by Governor Ed Rendell on July 5, 2004, created the Pennsylvania Gaming Control Board and authorized 14 casino licenses. The first slot-only casino opened in November 2006. Table games followed in 2010. Act 42 of 2017 — the broadest single gaming-expansion law in U.S. history at the time — authorized sports wagering, online casino gaming, daily fantasy sports, Category 4 satellite "mini-casinos," and video gaming terminals at qualifying truck stops. After Murphy v. NCAA, Pennsylvania launched retail sports betting in November 2018 and mobile in May 2019. Online casino gaming launched July 15, 2019, and Pennsylvania became the second-largest iGaming market in the United States.
