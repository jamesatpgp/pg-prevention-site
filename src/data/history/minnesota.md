Minnesota's 1858 Constitution prohibited any lottery — a ban that held for 130 years. St. Paul's "O'Connor System," in place from about 1900 to 1935, formally accommodated visiting criminals running gambling and other vice in exchange for not committing crimes against St. Paul residents. The system collapsed after the 1934 Hamm and Bremer kidnappings exposed the underlying corruption. Slot machines proliferated in Twin Cities and Duluth resorts before the federal Johnson Act cleared them after 1951. Voters approved pari-mutuel racing by constitutional amendment in November 1982. Canterbury Downs opened on June 26, 1985, closed in the early 1990s, and reopened as Canterbury Park in 1995 after the legislature authorized racetrack card clubs. Running Aces opened in 2008.

The lottery prohibition was repealed by 1988 amendment; the Minnesota State Lottery launched April 17, 1990. Tribal gaming is Minnesota's defining vertical. After the 1987 Cabazon decision and IGRA's 1988 passage, Minnesota was the first state to enter Class III tribal compacts: Governor Rudy Perpich signed the first agreements in 1989, and all eleven federally recognized Minnesota tribes were compacted by 1991. Grand Casino Mille Lacs opened in 1991; Mystic Lake opened May 24, 1992, and remains one of the largest tribal casinos in the United States. The compacts have no expiration date, which makes Minnesota tribes unusually resistant to state expansion efforts. Minnesota authorized electronic pull-tabs and linked bingo in 2012 to fund the state's contribution to U.S. Bank Stadium. Sports betting remains illegal as of 2026.
