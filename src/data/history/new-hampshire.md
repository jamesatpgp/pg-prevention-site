New Hampshire ran the first modern state lottery in America. Governor John W. King and Representative Larry Pickett championed the New Hampshire Sweepstakes Act in 1963 as a way to fund education without imposing a sales or income tax — preserving the state's "no broad-based tax" identity. Voters approved in March 1964; the first ticket sold March 12, 1964, and the first drawing was held September 12, 1964. Federal authorities initially threatened mail and broadcast enforcement, so the law was structured as a horse-race sweepstake tied to Rockingham Park to avoid the technical "lottery" term and limited to in-person ticket sales. Within seven years, New York, New Jersey, Massachusetts, Connecticut, and many others followed New Hampshire's lead.

Pari-mutuel horse racing had been authorized earlier, in 1933, reviving Rockingham Park (originally opened in 1906 and shut down for unauthorized wagering). New Hampshire joined Maine and Vermont in 1985 to launch the Tri-State Megabucks compact, the first multi-state lottery game in U.S. history. Greyhound racing was prohibited effective January 1, 2010. Charitable gaming evolved into a quasi-casino sector: roughly 15 to 20 commercial-style halls offer blackjack, poker, roulette, and craps, with net proceeds going to nonprofits. There is no Las Vegas-style commercial casino. The Sports Betting Act of 2019 awarded the statewide mobile sports betting contract exclusively to DraftKings. Mobile sports betting launched December 30, 2019; retail followed in 2020.
