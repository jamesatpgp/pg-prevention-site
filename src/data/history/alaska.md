# Alaska — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
Alaska is a low-volume, charitable-gaming-centered jurisdiction. There is **no state lottery, no pari-mutuel horse or greyhound racing, no commercial casinos, no legal sports betting, and no licensed online gaming**. The only commercial-scale legal activity is the **charitable gaming sector** — bingo, pull-tabs, raffles, lotteries, and Calcutta pools — regulated by the **Alaska Department of Revenue, Tax Division — Charitable Gaming Group** under Alaska Statutes Title 5, Chapter 15. Two **tribal Class II** electronic-bingo halls operate under IGRA: Klawock Cooperative Association (Prince of Wales Island) and the Native Village of Eklutna's Chin'an Gaming Hall (outside Anchorage); the Eklutna facility remains in legal dispute and the Department of the Interior in September 2025 withdrew Solicitor's Opinion M-37079, the legal predicate for Eklutna's tribal-lands status.[^1][^2][^3]

## How Alaska got here
Russian colonial Alaska (1733-1867) and the U.S. District of Alaska (1867-1912) hosted no organized commercial gambling. Gold-rush mining camps at Nome (1898), Skagway, Wrangell, and Fairbanks (1902) saw saloon faro and dice play typical of frontier boomtowns, but the most consequential rush — the Klondike of 1896-1899 — channeled commercial gambling north into Dawson City in Canada's Yukon. Alaska's territorial criminal codes adopted standard federal-territorial anti-gambling postures, and the Alaska Constitutional Convention (1955-1956) produced a constitution that took effect with statehood on January 3, 1959. **Article XII § 15** prohibits lotteries except as authorized by the Legislature; the legislature has never authorized a statewide lottery, and Alaska does not participate in Powerball or Mega Millions. Bills HB 246 (2020) and SB 110 (2022) attempted to create a state lottery and to authorize gambling on state ferries; neither passed.[^4][^5]

The state's gambling regime instead pivoted around **charitable gaming**: the Legislature enacted the first authorizing statutes in 1960; the current statutory framework is at AS Title 5, Chapter 15, with the Alaska Gaming Reform Act taking effect May 26, 2016. Permitted activities — **bingo, pull-tabs, raffles, lotteries, and Calcutta pools** — generate most of Alaska's regulated gambling revenue, with pull-tabs the dominant category. Qualified permittees include not only standard civic/religious/fraternal entities but uniquely Alaskan ones: **dog mushers' associations, outboard motor associations, fishing-derby associations, and nonprofit trade groups** — a reflection of the state's sporting culture. Tribal Class II gaming developed slowly: Klawock's electronic-bingo facility on Prince of Wales Island has operated for over a decade; the Native Village of Eklutna opened the Chin'an Gaming Hall near Anchorage in 2024, with federal court rulings against the Tribe in September 2021 (*State of Alaska v. Native Village of Eklutna*) and renewed federal legal uncertainty in 2025. Class III gaming — true slot machines and banked card games — has never been authorized anywhere in Alaska. Alaska has not legalized sports betting following *Murphy v. NCAA* (2018); DFS operates without specific authorization.[^1][^3][^6]

## Regulatory framework today
The **Alaska Department of Revenue, Tax Division — Charitable Gaming Group** is the principal civilian regulator, licensing permittees and operators and collecting pull-tab tax ([tax.alaska.gov/programs/programs/index.aspx?60010](https://tax.alaska.gov/programs/programs/index.aspx?60010)). The **Department of Commerce, Community, and Economic Development — Division of Community and Regional Affairs** provides a secondary resource page on local-government gaming permits ([commerce.alaska.gov](https://www.commerce.alaska.gov/web/dcra/LocalGovernmentResourceDesk/FinancialManagement/Gaming.aspx)). Tribal gaming is governed by federal **NIGC** under IGRA. There is no Alaska Gaming Commission, no Alaska Lottery, and no Racing Commission. Problem-gambling treatment is administered through the **Alaska Department of Health, Division of Behavioral Health** as part of general substance-use services; Alaska has no NCPG state-council affiliate.[^1][^7]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
**Alaska has no statewide statutory voluntary self-exclusion program.** Because the state has no lottery, no commercial casino, no racing, and no licensed online gaming vertical, no state agency maintains an exclusion list, and there is no enrollment portal on any Alaska .gov site. The **Alaska Department of Revenue, Tax Division — Charitable Gaming Group** does not administer a self-exclusion list for bingo halls, pull-tab operators, or charitable raffles; the charitable-gaming framework relies on per-event purchase limits, permittee record-keeping, and operator discretion rather than on a regulator-maintained exclusion roster. Players who wish to be barred from a specific charitable bingo hall must do so through the operator's own internal policy, and these arrangements are not standardized.

Tribal-property self-exclusion at the **Klawock Cooperative Association** electronic-bingo facility and the **Native Village of Eklutna's Chin'an Gaming Hall** is administered by each tribe's gaming commission and security operation under tribal ordinance. Klawock and Eklutna do not publish their self-exclusion enrollment numbers, duration menus, or removal/petition procedures on public-facing tribal websites, so duration options, scope, penalties for breach, and the removal process are **[unconfirmed]** absent direct contact with each tribal gaming commission. Neither tribe participates in any publicly listed multi-state tribal self-exclusion database, and Alaska itself is not a party to the Multi-State Internet Gaming Agreement (MSIGA) or the Nevada-New Jersey iGaming arrangements (which would be irrelevant in any case because Alaska has no licensed iGaming).

For Alaska residents who self-exclude from out-of-state online sportsbooks or casinos, that exclusion is administered by the regulator of the licensing state (typically a state where the operator is also licensed); it has no effect inside Alaska because no licensed online operator runs in-state. **The practical 2026 picture**: residents seeking self-exclusion route through the **NCPG national helpline (1-800-GAMBLER)** and into clinical referral via the Division of Behavioral Health rather than into any regulator-administered list. This is an honest gap in Alaska's responsible-gambling infrastructure and stands out among the 50 states.[^7][^8]
