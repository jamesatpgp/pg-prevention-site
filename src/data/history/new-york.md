# New York — Gambling History

## The present

New York in 2026 is the largest sports-betting market in the United States and one of the largest overall gambling markets. The state offers: the New York Lottery (the country's second-oldest modern lottery, the largest by sales); pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing at NYRA's three flagship tracks (Belmont, Saratoga, Aqueduct) plus harness tracks; Class III tribal casinos run by the Oneida, Seneca, and St. Regis Mohawk nations; nine "racinos" operating video lottery terminals; four upstate commercial casinos opened 2017–2018; nine licensed mobile sports-betting operators; retail sportsbooks at commercial and tribal casinos; bingo and other charitable games; and pari-mutuel OTB outlets in surviving regional corporations. Downstate commercial casino licensing is under active selection in 2026. iGaming has not been authorized as of 2026. The dominant regulator is the **New York State Gaming Commission**, which absorbed predecessor racing, wagering, and lottery agencies in 2013.¹ ²

## How it got here

New York's gambling history pivots on **November 6, 1894**, when voters ratified a sweeping constitutional ban on "lotteries or the sale of lottery tickets, pool-selling, book-making, or any other kind of gambling." That clause, codified at Article I, § 9, was a reaction to lottery-contractor scandals and bookmaking corruption at the racetracks; it remains the constitutional baseline today, modified only by enumerated exceptions added by successive amendments.³

Before the ban, gambling had been embedded in New York public finance. The colonial legislature and the early Republic authorized lotteries to fund King's College (Columbia, 1746), Union College (1805), roads, bridges, and prisons. Horse racing predates the Republic: the Newmarket Course on Long Island's Hempstead Plain (1665) is generally cited as North America's first organized racetrack; Saratoga Race Course (1863) is the oldest continuously operating major sports venue in the country.

The Hart-Agnew Act of 1908 briefly criminalized oral as well as written bookmaking, shutting most New York racing. The 1939 amendment to Article I, § 9 reopened pari-mutuel wagering on horse races; the Pari-Mutuel Revenue Law (1940) created the State Racing Commission. The 1957 amendment permitted charitable bingo; 1966 voters approved a state lottery and the New York Lottery began sales June 1, 1967 — the second modern U.S. lottery. The 1970 OTB law created the country's first legal off-track betting system; NYC OTB filed bankruptcy and shut down December 7, 2010 after the legislature declined a rescue.⁴

Tribal gaming opened with Oneida's Turning Stone Casino (Verona, July 20, 1993). The 2001 VLT statute authorized racinos; Saratoga Gaming and Raceway opened January 28, 2004. Voters approved the 2013 commercial-casino constitutional amendment (Proposal 1), and the Upstate New York Gaming Economic Development Act of 2013 authorized seven Las Vegas-style casinos — four upstate (del Lago, Rivers Schenectady, Tioga Downs, Resorts World Catskills, all open by February 2018) and three downstate licenses still being awarded. After *Murphy v. NCAA*, 138 S. Ct. 1461 (2018), retail sports betting launched July 16, 2019, at Rivers Schenectady; mobile sports betting launched January 8, 2022, under the FY2022 budget bill with a 51% gross-revenue tax rate and a minimum of nine operators.⁵ Within twelve months New York was the largest U.S. sports-betting market by handle.

## Regulatory framework today

- **New York State Gaming Commission** — successor to the Racing & Wagering Board and Lottery, the single regulator for the Lottery, VLTs, commercial casinos, sports wagering (retail and mobile), pari-mutuel racing, interactive fantasy sports, charitable gaming, and the **statewide voluntary self-exclusion list**. https://gaming.ny.gov/
- **Office of Addiction Services and Supports (OASAS)** — funder of problem-gambling treatment and the regional Problem Gambling Resource Centers. https://oasas.ny.gov/gambling
- **Tribal gaming commissions** of the Oneida, Seneca, and St. Regis Mohawk Nations regulate Class III tribal casinos under their respective compacts; the NIGC publishes filings.⁶

## Voluntary self-exclusion

New York runs a **single Voluntary Self-Exclusion (VSE) list administered by the Gaming Commission** with statewide cross-vertical scope — one of the broadest in the country.⁷

**Scope.** Self-excluded individuals are barred from on- and off-track pari-mutuel wagering, internet and account wagering, **retail and mobile sports wagering**, the four upstate commercial casinos (and any subsequently licensed downstate casinos), the nine VLT facilities, New York Lottery traditional draw and instant games, Quick Draw, interactive fantasy sports, and charitable gaming. The list does not apply to the three tribal casino operators (Oneida, Seneca, St. Regis Mohawk), which run their own internal exclusion lists honored on tribal land — a meaningful coverage gap.

**Duration.** One, three, five years, or lifetime. (Three years is unusual; most states offer only 1/5/lifetime.) Terms run from the Commission's processing date; removal is not automatic at the end of a term.

**How to enroll.** Two paths as of 2026. (1) In person at any licensed gaming facility — every commercial casino, racino, OTB, and NYRA track stocks the Commission's VSE form and will help submit it. (2) By U.S. mail to the Commission's headquarters in Schenectady, with a notarized form and a recent photograph. The notarization-and-mail process has long been criticized as a barrier; **Senate Bill S8636 (2025) is pending in 2026** to authorize fully electronic enrollment. Once processed, the Commission distributes the list to every licensed operator, who must freeze accounts and block account creation.

**Removal.** Affirmative request only — written, notarized, with photograph — submitted after the minimum term has elapsed. The Commission notifies operators.

**Penalties for breach.** Winnings are surrendered to the state, accounts are closed, and the person may be ejected and trespassed. Operators face Commission enforcement action for failing to detect or honor enrollments.

**Support.** Since 2024 the **New York Council on Problem Gambling** has operated a free **Voluntary Self-Exclusion Support Program** offering one-on-one help with the application, free notary services, and follow-up support — a recognition that the paper-and-notary process is a real-world barrier. HOPEline (1-877-846-7369) staffs 24/7 call/text/chat referral.

## Problem gambling response

OASAS administers state problem-gambling funding under a mature dedicated-revenue model: 1% of VLT and commercial casino gross gaming revenue (Tax Law § 1612(b) and Pari-Mutuel Wagering & Breeding Law § 1352(2)), 1% of mobile sports-betting gross gaming revenue (Laws of 2021, ch. 59, Part Y), plus additional appropriations. Combined dedications produce the largest dollar-volume problem-gambling funding stream in the country (mobile sports betting alone yields roughly $6+ million annually at full ramp).⁸ OASAS funds seven regional Problem Gambling Resource Centers (Western NY, Finger Lakes, Central NY, Capital, Mid-Hudson, NYC, Long Island) that handle screening, referral, peer support, and clinician training. The **New York Council on Problem Gambling (NYCPG)** is the NCPG state affiliate, operates HOPEline, and runs the VSE Support Program.
