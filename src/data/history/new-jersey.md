New Jersey's gambling history has a colonial spine and a gangster middle. The Province authorized at least 17 public lotteries between 1737 and 1776 to fund Princeton College, churches, bridges, and the militia. The 1844 Constitution banned legislative lottery authorization — a ban that held for 125 years. Atlantic City was incorporated in 1854 as a Quaker temperance resort but ran illegal back-room casinos through the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Mayor Enoch "Nucky" Johnson tolerated gambling, prostitution, and bootleg liquor from 1911 to 1941. In May 1929, the Atlantic City Conference at the President Hotel brought together Lansky, Luciano, Capone, Costello, and Torrio, helping form the National Crime Syndicate.

Modern legal gambling came through the ballot box. Pari-mutuel racing was legalized by 1939 amendment, and Monmouth Park reopened in 1946. Voters lifted the lottery ban by 1969 amendment; tickets went on sale December 16, 1970. Voters rejected a 1974 statewide casino plan but approved a 1976 Atlantic-City-only proposal. The Casino Control Act, signed in June 1977, made New Jersey the second U.S. state to authorize commercial casinos — 45 years after Nevada. The 2013 Internet Gaming Act made New Jersey the first state with comprehensive online casino gaming; it launched in November 2013. After the Supreme Court struck down PASPA in May 2018, Monmouth Park took the first legal sports bet outside Nevada on June 14, 2018.
