# Massachusetts — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

Massachusetts operates three commercial destination casinos (Plainridge Park Casino in Plainville, MGM Springfield, and Encore Boston Harbor in Everett), a state lottery that is among the highest per-capita revenue producers in the country, statewide retail and mobile sports wagering, and charitable "beano" and raffles run by qualified nonprofits. The Massachusetts Gaming Commission (MGC) — a five-member independent body created by the 2011 Expanded Gaming Act — sits at the center of the regulatory architecture and is widely regarded as one of the most public-health-forward gaming regulators in the United States.¹ Live thoroughbred racing has ended (Suffolk Downs ran its final card in 2019) and greyhound racing was abolished by ballot question in 2008, so the pari-mutuel sector is now confined to simulcasting.

## A compressed history

Massachusetts has spent four centuries oscillating between Puritan prohibition and pragmatic revenue. The Bay Colony's General Court ordered all "cards, dice, or tables" destroyed in 1630, yet by the eighteenth century the same legislature was chartering public lotteries to fund Faneuil Hall, Harvard's Stoughton Hall, and Revolutionary-War supplies.² Reformers prevailed in the 1830s — private lotteries were banned in 1833 and all outstanding grants repealed in 1834 — and Chapter 271 of the General Laws ("Crimes Against Public Policy") kept gambling broadly illegal for the next century.³

The first modern crack came in 1934, when Chapter 374 of the Acts of 1934 created the Massachusetts Racing Commission and authorized pari-mutuel wagering. Suffolk Downs opened July 10, 1935 and hosted Seabiscuit's 1937 Massachusetts Handicap victory. Charitable "beano" (bingo) was legalized in 1971, raffles in 1969. The state lottery launched March 22, 1972 under Chapter 813 of the Acts of 1971; its instant-ticket innovation in 1974 was among the first in the country and net proceeds still flow to municipalities as Unrestricted General Government Aid.⁴

Casino legalization came late and deliberately. The Expanded Gaming Act (Chapter 194 of the Acts of 2011), signed by Governor Deval Patrick on November 22, 2011, authorized three Category 1 destination resorts (one each in three regions) and one Category 2 slots license. Plainridge Park opened June 24, 2015; MGM Springfield on August 24, 2018; Encore Boston Harbor on June 23, 2019. The Region C southeastern license was never issued — the Mashpee Wampanoag Tribe's federal land-in-trust decision was rescinded in September 2018, halting the First Light Casino project in Taunton.⁵ The Aquinnah Wampanoag operate a small Class II electronic gaming facility on Martha's Vineyard, opened in 2020 following the First Circuit's 2017 ruling in *Aquinnah v. Massachusetts*.

Sports wagering arrived under Chapter 173 of the Acts of 2022, signed by Governor Charlie Baker on August 10, 2022. Retail books opened at the three casinos on January 31, 2023; mobile betting went live March 10, 2023 with operators including DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars, Fanatics, and ESPN BET. The statute taxes retail GGR at 15% and mobile at 20% and prohibits wagering on Massachusetts collegiate teams except in tournament play.

Defunct forms include greyhound racing (prohibited January 1, 2010 by 2008 Ballot Question 3), Suffolk Downs thoroughbred racing (ended 2019), Foxboro/Bay State harness (closed 1997), and Hinsdale Raceway (closed 1985).

## Regulatory framework today

The Massachusetts Gaming Commission (https://massgaming.com) licenses and regulates the three commercial casinos and statewide sports wagering under M.G.L. c. 23K and c. 23N. The Massachusetts State Lottery Commission (https://www.masslottery.com), housed in the Treasurer's office under M.G.L. c. 10 §§ 22–39, runs draw and instant games and the Charitable Gaming Division that licenses beano halls and raffles. Pari-mutuel and simulcast wagering remain under c. 128A within MGC jurisdiction. The Department of Public Health receives the statutorily dedicated 5% problem-gambling allocation through the Public Health Trust Fund, and the Massachusetts Council on Gaming and Health (MACGH, https://macgh.org) operates the 1-800-426-1234 helpline and manages PlayWell program administration on the Commission's behalf.⁶

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Massachusetts runs a single statewide Voluntary Self-Exclusion (VSE) program administered directly by the Massachusetts Gaming Commission under 205 CMR 133. On March 27, 2026, the MGC ended its licensing agreement with the British Columbia Lottery Corporation and rebranded the long-running GameSense responsible-gaming program as **PlayWell**, an in-house initiative the Commission now owns and manages outright.⁷ VSE enrollment and intake continue uninterrupted under the new brand, with administration delegated to MACGH.

Enrollment is offered three ways: (1) walk-in at any **PlayWell Info Center** inside Plainridge Park Casino, MGM Springfield, or Encore Boston Harbor during operating hours, no appointment required; (2) remote video enrollment for individuals unable or unwilling to come on-property — a smartphone or computer with email and video capability is required; and (3) by contacting vse@massgaming.gov or 1-800-GAM-1234.⁸ Participants choose one of three exclusion terms: **1 year, 5 years, or lifetime**, with the additional choice of excluding from **casino gaming, sports wagering, or both**.⁹

The program is broad but not universal. It bars participants from the gaming floors, hotels, restaurants, events, marketing lists, and check-cashing privileges of all three commercial casinos and from all licensed Massachusetts mobile and retail sports wagering operators (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars, Fanatics, ESPN BET, and others). It does **not** cover the Massachusetts State Lottery (a separate lottery self-exclusion exists for iLottery products), the Aquinnah Wampanoag's Martha's Vineyard Class II facility (tribal sovereign program), or simulcast wagering at Plainridge's racebook. The state has no online casino product to cover.

Removal requires an affirmative application at the end of the chosen term — exclusion does not lapse automatically. A 1-year or 5-year enrollee must complete the term, then submit a removal request through MGC and complete a re-entry process; lifetime enrollment is irrevocable. Anyone who breaches the agreement and attempts to gamble forfeits winnings (which are deposited into the Public Health Trust Fund) and may be removed and charged with criminal trespass.

Massachusetts is **not** yet a participant in the multi-state National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP) at nvsep.org, though NVSEP has publicly named Massachusetts as the next jurisdiction confirmed to onboard.¹⁰ The complementary **PlayMyWay** budget tool — billed as a U.S. first — operates separately: players can set daily, weekly, and monthly spend limits by inserting their rewards card into any slot machine or PlayMyWay kiosk at the three casinos, with real-time tracking against the limit.¹¹

The MGC does not publish current VSE enrollment counts on a fixed schedule; cumulative enrollment was reported in the low thousands through 2024 SEIGMA reporting.
