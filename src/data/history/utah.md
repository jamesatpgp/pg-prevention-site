Utah is, with Hawaii, one of only two U.S. states with no legal gambling of any kind. There is no lottery, no casino, no charitable bingo, no pari-mutuel wagering, no sports betting, and no online wagering. Article VI § 27 of the 1895 Utah Constitution — "The Legislature shall not authorize any game of chance, lottery or gift enterprise under any pretense or for any purpose" — has never been amended. Utah's anti-gambling tradition predates statehood by half a century. The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, led by Brigham Young, established settlements in the Salt Lake Valley beginning in 1847 and explicitly framed gambling as incompatible with church standards. Utah was admitted as the 45th state in 1896, and the constitution's anti-gambling clause was approved without serious opposition.

The closest Utah ever came to legal gambling was a brief 1925-1927 pari-mutuel horse-racing experiment, suspended by the legislature. A 1958 ballot initiative to re-legalize horse-race betting was rejected by voters. Bills in 2020 and 2025 to authorize pari-mutuel wagering by local option both died in committee. Utah's eight federally recognized tribes cannot operate Class III gaming because IGRA's "permitted by state law" gateway is closed. The Skull Valley Band attempted a Class II bingo development in the late 1990s that never opened at scale. In 2026, Utah's Attorney General took the lead among state AGs challenging federally regulated prediction markets (Kalshi and Polymarket), arguing that sports-related "event contracts" sold to Utah residents are illegal gambling under Utah law.
