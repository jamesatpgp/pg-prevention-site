Lexington raced horses on Race Street as early as 1789, three years before Kentucky statehood. The Bluegrass region produced America's most important thoroughbred bloodlines, and the Kentucky Derby and Kentucky Oaks, both first run at Churchill Downs in 1875, became cultural institutions. The 1891 Kentucky Constitution banned lotteries — a reaction to the Louisiana Lottery scandal — but state courts upheld pari-mutuel betting on horse races as a permitted exception. The 1906 State Racing Commission formalized pari-mutuel as the exclusive legal form of track betting. From the 1930s through 1961, Newport and Covington — across the Ohio River from Cincinnati — hosted open mob-run casinos and were nicknamed the "Sin City of the Midwest." A 1961 reform campaign finally shut them down.

Voters approved a constitutional amendment authorizing a state lottery in 1988, and the Kentucky Lottery launched April 4, 1989. Net proceeds support merit and need-based college scholarships. Kentucky launched online iLottery in 2016. Charitable gaming was authorized by 1992 amendment. The most distinctive modern development is historical horse racing (HHR), introduced in 2010 when the Kentucky Horse Racing Commission redefined "pari-mutuel" to include slot-style machines tied to anonymized historical race data. After the Kentucky Supreme Court questioned the practice in 2020, the General Assembly amended the statute in 2021 to put HHR on firm footing. Sports wagering was authorized by House Bill 551 of 2023; retail launched September 7, 2023, and mobile September 28, 2023. Kentucky has no commercial casinos and no federally recognized tribes.
