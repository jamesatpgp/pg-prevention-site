# Utah — Gambling History

## Present-day posture

Utah in 2026 is, with Hawaii, one of only two U.S. states with **no legal gambling of any kind**. There is no state lottery, no commercial casinos, no card rooms, no charitable bingo, no charitable raffles for prizes, no pari-mutuel wagering, no sports betting, no daily fantasy sports, no historical horse racing, no online wagering, and no tribal Class III gaming. Article VI, Section 27 of the 1895 Utah Constitution — "The Legislature shall not authorize any game of chance, lottery or gift enterprise under any pretense or for any purpose" — has never been amended. Utah Code Ann. § 76-10-1101 et seq. defines and criminalizes gambling broadly enough that most private wagering on poker among acquaintances technically constitutes a misdemeanor.¹

## Compressed history

Utah's anti-gambling tradition predates statehood by half a century. The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, led by Brigham Young after Joseph Smith's 1844 death, established settlements in the Salt Lake Valley beginning in 1847 and explicitly framed both alcohol and gambling as incompatible with church standards; Young's 1866 sermon condemning gambling establishments in Utah Territory is a frequently cited primary statement of the territorial-era position. Non-Mormon gambling associated with Park City mining and Union Pacific railroad construction was suppressed by territorial authorities. Utah was admitted as the 45th state on January 4, 1896 after pledging permanent abolition of polygamy in its enabling act; the constitution's anti-gambling clause was approved without serious opposition.²

The closest Utah ever came to legal gambling was a brief 1925-1927 pari-mutuel horse-racing experiment, suspended by the legislature amid concerns about corruption among racing commissioners.³ A 1958 ballot initiative (Initiative A) to re-legalize horse-race betting was rejected by voters. Senator David Hinkins's 2020 SB 156 — and a 2025 reintroduction under the same number — would have authorized pari-mutuel wagering subject to county-by-county local-option referendums; both bills died in committee. The Utah Horse Racing Commission, housed within the Department of Agriculture and Food, persists to regulate non-wagering quarter-horse and thoroughbred meets at fairgrounds. Utah's eight federally recognized tribes (Navajo Nation, Ute Indian Tribe of the Uintah and Ouray Reservation, Ute Mountain Ute, Confederated Tribes of the Goshute, Skull Valley Band of Goshute, Paiute Indian Tribe of Utah, Northwestern Band of the Shoshone Nation, San Juan Southern Paiute) cannot operate Class III gaming because IGRA's "permitted by state law" gateway is closed; the Skull Valley Band attempted a Class II bingo development in the late 1990s that never opened at scale.⁴

In a 2026 development that drew national attention, Utah's Attorney General has taken the lead among state AGs challenging federally regulated prediction markets (Kalshi and Polymarket) on the ground that sports-related "event contracts" sold to Utah residents are illegal gambling under Utah law regardless of CFTC registration of the underlying instruments — one of the highest-profile federal-vs.-state gambling fights of the post-PASPA era.⁵

## Regulatory framework today

There is **no Utah gambling regulator** because there is no legal gambling to regulate. Adjacent functions:

- The **Utah Horse Racing Commission** (within the Department of Agriculture and Food, https://ag.utah.gov/animal-industry/utah-horse-racing-commission/) regulates non-wagering horse racing at fairgrounds.
- The **Utah Attorney General** prosecutes illegal gambling and leads the multistate prediction-market litigation.
- The **Utah Division of Substance Use and Mental Health (DSAMH)**, within the Department of Health and Human Services (https://sumh.utah.gov/), recognizes gambling disorder as a behavioral-health concern; problem-gambling treatment is delivered through general substance-use-disorder counseling channels because Utah has no gambling-tax revenue to fund a dedicated program.⁶

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Utah has **no state-administered voluntary self-exclusion program because no licensed gambling venue exists in the state to exclude an individual from**. Utah is structurally unique: where other states (including Tennessee) operate a VSE list because there is *something* commercial to be excluded from, Utah has nothing to enroll into. The VSE concept in Utah accordingly defaults to alternative pathways:⁷

1. **National Council on Problem Gambling — National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP)** at https://www.nvsep.org/, which Utah residents may use to self-exclude from online operators in states that have signed onto NVSEP (currently a small set, but expanding). Utah's Attorney General position that online sports betting offered to Utah residents constitutes illegal gambling means that Utahns enrolling in NVSEP are effectively double-blocking activity that is already illegal.

2. **Out-of-state casino exclusion lists** — Utah residents who travel to gamble in Nevada, Idaho, Colorado, Wyoming, or Arizona may enroll directly in those state programs (Nevada Gaming Control Board, Idaho Lottery, Colorado Limited Gaming Control Commission, Wyoming Gaming Commission, Arizona Department of Gaming). Cross-state coordination is informal at best — a Utah resident excluded in Nevada is not automatically excluded in Idaho.

3. **Operator-level self-exclusion** — A Utah resident who has an online sports-betting or DFS account on an operator that offers Utah-blocking (i.e., DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, etc., none of which formally operate in Utah but whose accounts can be opened by a Utah resident traveling to a legal state) may use the operator's in-app responsible-gaming controls to self-exclude on the operator's licensee-specific list across all states.

4. **Gamblers Anonymous Utah, Utah 988, Utah 211** — Utah's substantive problem-gambling support infrastructure is overwhelmingly peer-support and crisis-line oriented rather than venue-exclusion oriented. The National Council on Problem Gambling national helpline (1-800-GAMBLER / now 1-800-522-4700) is the principal referral.⁸

Because Utah collects no gambling tax revenue, DSAMH has no statutory funding stream specifically dedicated to problem-gambling services; treatment is provided through general behavioral-health appropriations. Provider capacity is small, and Utahns with significant gambling problems often access treatment for activity that occurred entirely out of state or online. As of 2026 there is no proposal pending in the legislature to create a state-administered VSE list because the constitutional prohibition makes the question moot.⁹
