# Minnesota — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

Minnesota is overwhelmingly a **tribal gaming state**: eleven federally recognized tribes operate Class III casinos under indefinite-term compacts that have no expiration date. There is no commercial casino industry. The state-run Minnesota State Lottery operates draw and instant games; two licensed pari-mutuel tracks (Canterbury Park in Shakopee and Running Aces in Columbus) host live thoroughbred/quarter-horse and standardbred meets along with card rooms; and the country's largest per-capita charitable gambling sector — pull-tabs, electronic pull-tabs, bingo, raffles, tipboards, and paddlewheels — clears more than $2.5 billion in gross receipts annually.¹ **Sports betting remains illegal in Minnesota** as of May 2026; multiple bills have died in the legislature over tribal-exclusivity disputes with the two racetracks.

## A compressed history

The Dakota and Ojibwe peoples played plum-stone, hand-game (moccasin), and lacrosse with significant property at stake long before European contact, traditions that persisted through reservation life.² Minnesota's 1858 Constitution at Article X § 8 prohibited any lottery — a ban that held for 130 years. St. Paul's "O'Connor System" (c. 1900–1935), under Police Chief John O'Connor, formally accommodated visiting criminals running gambling and other vice in exchange for not committing crimes against St. Paul residents; the system collapsed after the 1934 Hamm and Bremer kidnappings exposed the underlying corruption.³ Slot machines proliferated in Twin Cities and Duluth resorts through the 1940s before the 1951 Johnson Act cleared them.

Pari-mutuel racing arrived by constitutional amendment in November 1982; the Minnesota Racing Commission was created under Chapter 240. Canterbury Downs opened June 26, 1985, closed in the early 1990s, and reopened as Canterbury Park in 1995 after the legislature authorized racetrack card clubs (Chapter 633 of the Laws of 1994, Minn. Stat. § 240.30). Running Aces opened April 11, 2008 as a standardbred facility.

The lottery prohibition was repealed by 1988 constitutional amendment; the State Lottery Act (Chapter 290 of the Laws of 1989, codified at Minn. Stat. Chapter 349A) launched April 17, 1990. Net proceeds are constitutionally divided among the Environment and Natural Resources Trust Fund, the General Fund, and in-lieu-of-sales-tax allocations.

Tribal gaming is Minnesota's defining vertical. Following the U.S. Supreme Court's 1987 *Cabazon* decision and IGRA's 1988 passage, **Minnesota was the first state to enter Class III tribal compacts** — Governor Rudy Perpich signed the first agreements in 1989, and all eleven federally recognized Minnesota tribes were compacted by 1991. Grand Casino Mille Lacs (Mille Lacs Band of Ojibwe) opened in 1991; Mystic Lake (Shakopee Mdewakanton Sioux Community) opened May 24, 1992 and remains one of the largest tribal casinos in the United States. Other major properties include Treasure Island (Prairie Island), Black Bear (Fond du Lac), Grand Portage, Northern Lights (Leech Lake), Shooting Star (White Earth), Jackpot Junction (Lower Sioux), and Fortune Bay (Bois Forte). The compacts' indefinite term and narrow renegotiation triggers have made Minnesota tribes unusually resistant to state expansion efforts.⁴

Charitable gambling expanded dramatically with the 2012 authorization of electronic pull-tabs and linked bingo (Chapter 299 of the Laws of 2012), originally to fund the state's $498 million contribution to U.S. Bank Stadium.

Defunct or never-legalized forms include greyhound racing (never authorized), riverboat gambling (never authorized despite Mississippi River proximity), the O'Connor System (ended ~1935), and the mid-century roadhouse slot operations.

## Regulatory framework today

The Minnesota Racing Commission (https://mn.gov/mrc) licenses Canterbury Park and Running Aces and oversees the racetrack card rooms. The Minnesota Gambling Control Board (https://mn.gov/gcb), created in 1985, regulates charitable lawful gambling under Minn. Stat. Chapter 349 — pull-tabs, electronic pull-tabs, bingo, raffles, tipboards, and paddlewheels. The Minnesota State Lottery (https://www.mnlottery.com) operates under Minn. Stat. Chapter 349A. Tribal Class III gaming is federally overseen by the National Indian Gaming Commission (https://www.nigc.gov) under tribal-state compacts. The Minnesota Department of Human Services Problem Gambling Program (https://mn.gov/dhs) receives statutory funding under Minn. Stat. § 245.98, and the Northstar Problem Gambling Alliance (https://www.northstarpg.org), founded 1985, runs the 1-800-333-HOPE helpline.

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Minnesota's self-exclusion landscape mirrors its fractured regulatory structure: there is **no single statewide self-exclusion program**. Excludable individuals must enroll separately in each of three parallel systems.

**1. Tribal casino self-exclusion.** Each of the eleven federally recognized tribes operates its own voluntary exclusion program under its compact. The Minnesota Indian Gaming Association (MIGA) maintains an information clearinghouse, but enrollment is property-by-property — a person seeking exclusion from all eleven tribes' casinos must contact each tribal gaming authority separately. Duration options, removal procedures, and breach penalties vary by tribe. Several tribes offer 6-month, 1-year, 5-year, and lifetime options; others offer only lifetime. Enrollment is generally in person at the tribal gaming commission's offices or at the property's security office. Re-entry by an excluded person is criminal trespass under tribal law and may trigger forfeiture of winnings. This decentralized model has been a sustained critique of the Minnesota system.

**2. Racetrack and card room self-exclusion.** Canterbury Park and Running Aces each administer their own voluntary self-exclusion programs under Minnesota Racing Commission rules. Both tracks accept 6-month, 1-year, 5-year, and lifetime exclusion. Enrollment is in person at the track's security or responsible-gaming office. Exclusion at one track does not automatically exclude at the other; an individual must enroll separately at each venue. Card rooms (poker and house-banked card games at both facilities) and simulcast wagering are covered by the same enrollment.

**3. Lottery self-exclusion.** The Minnesota State Lottery operates a self-exclusion mechanism for its retailer-network products and for its limited online subscription products under Lottery administrative rules. The lottery program does not cross-share with tribal or racetrack exclusion lists.

Minnesota is **not** a participant in the multi-state National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP at nvsep.org) and, having no legal sports betting or online casino, has no shared-liquidity online poker exposure or interstate digital exclusion footprint. There is no statewide statutory penalty regime for breach beyond the tribal trespass authority of each tribe and the MRC rules for the two tracks. The Northstar Problem Gambling Alliance provides referral support and treatment navigation but does not administer the exclusion lists themselves.

Aggregate Minnesota enrollment counts are not centrally published. NPGA estimates suggest several thousand individuals are enrolled across the tribal, racetrack, and lottery systems collectively [enrollment figures not centrally aggregated].
