# Louisiana — Gambling History

Louisiana in 2026 hosts one of the most varied gambling markets in the country: fifteen authorized riverboat casinos (many now land-based after the 2018+ conversion program), the single land-based Caesars New Orleans (formerly Harrah's), four racinos at the state's pari-mutuel tracks, parish-option video poker at truck stops, bars, and OTBs (43 of 64 parishes), three IGRA tribal casinos, the Louisiana Lottery (since 1991), pari-mutuel thoroughbred and quarter-horse racing, charitable bingo and electronic video bingo, and parish-option retail and mobile sports wagering (since 2021/2022). The Louisiana Gaming Control Board (LGCB) sets policy and oversees licensees with day-to-day enforcement by the Louisiana State Police, Gaming Enforcement Division; the Louisiana State Racing Commission regulates the tracks; the Louisiana Lottery Corporation operates the lottery; the Louisiana Department of Revenue Office of Charitable Gaming licenses charitable forms.¹

Louisiana's gambling culture predates the United States. Under French and Spanish rule, the colony of La Louisiane hosted bouillotte (poker's ancestor), faro, écarté, and craps; antebellum New Orleans had thirty-plus licensed gaming houses by the 1820s, and steamboat gambling between New Orleans, Natchez, Memphis, and St. Louis produced the iconic Mississippi-river card sharp. The Fair Grounds (founded 1852, renamed 1872) remains the second-oldest continuously running U.S. racetrack.

The pivot point in 19th-century American gambling was the **Louisiana State Lottery Company (1868-1894)**. In 1868 the Radical Republican legislature granted Charles T. Howard's company an exclusive 25-year monopoly in exchange for $40,000 annually to the treasury. Within years it was the largest lottery in the United States, with national mail-order sales producing tens of millions of dollars annually — and breathtaking corruption: the company bribed legislators, judges, governors, and federal postal officials, with drawings nominally supervised by Confederate generals P.G.T. Beauregard and Jubal Early as a public-relations veneer. Federal action followed. The Anti-Lottery Act of 1890 banned the use of the U.S. mail for lottery tickets, and 1895 legislation (later 18 U.S.C. §1301) banned interstate transportation; the U.S. Supreme Court upheld federal authority in *Champion v. Ames*, 188 U.S. 321 (1903) — a landmark Commerce Clause case. Louisiana voters decisively rejected a 1892 re-charter amendment, and the company effectively ended in 1894 (briefly relocating to Honduras as a "foreign" lottery before dissolving). **No U.S. state authorized a legal lottery from 1894 until New Hampshire revived the form in 1964** — a 70-year national prohibition directly traceable to the Louisiana Lottery scandal.²

Modern Louisiana gambling reopened in stages. Pari-mutuel horse-race wagering was authorized in 1908; the Fair Grounds hosts the Louisiana Derby. Evangeline Downs (Opelousas, 1966), Delta Downs (Vinton, 1973), and Louisiana Downs (Bossier City, 1974) followed. Frank Costello's Jefferson Parish slot empire operated openly through the 1930s-1951 under political protection (Huey Long-era tolerance), closed by Kefauver-era reform. Voters approved a modern lottery by 69% in October 1990; the Louisiana Lottery Corporation began ticket sales Sept. 6, 1991. The Riverboat Economic Development and Gaming Control Act (1991) authorized fifteen riverboat licenses (Shreveport-Bossier, Lake Charles, New Orleans, Baton Rouge zones); the cruising requirement ended in 2001 and inland conversion was authorized from 2018. The single land-based Harrah's New Orleans (now Caesars) opened a temporary site in 1995 and a permanent $381 million casino Oct. 28, 1999; federal prosecutors indicted Gov. Edwin Edwards in 1998 on charges of extorting bribes for riverboat licenses, with conviction in 2000 and roughly eight years in federal prison. Video poker at qualifying truck stops, bars/restaurants, hotels, and OTBs was authorized in 1991, with parish-option referenda determining local availability. Racinos at all four pari-mutuel tracks were added beginning with Delta Downs in 1997. Three IGRA tribes operate casinos: the Coushatta Tribe (Coushatta Casino Resort, Kinder, 1995), the Chitimacha Tribe (Cypress Bayou Casino Hotel, Charenton, 1994), and the Tunica-Biloxi Tribe (Paragon Casino Resort, Marksville, 1994). The Jena Band of Choctaw has compact authorization but no operating casino.³ Sports wagering required parish referenda: on Nov. 3, 2020, 55 of 64 parishes approved. Acts 440 and 443 (2021) launched retail in October 2021 and mobile Jan. 28, 2022, limited to approving parishes.

## Regulatory framework today

The Louisiana Gaming Control Board (https://lgcb.dps.louisiana.gov/) sets policy under La. R.S. Title 27; the Louisiana State Police Gaming Enforcement Division (https://lsp.org/about/leadershipsections/bureau-of-investigations/gaming-enforcement-division/) conducts investigations and runs day-to-day enforcement. The Louisiana State Racing Commission regulates pari-mutuel racing under La. R.S. Title 4. The Louisiana Lottery Corporation (https://louisianalottery.com/) operates the lottery under La. R.S. Title 47:9000 et seq. The Office of Charitable Gaming, within the Louisiana Department of Revenue (https://www.ocg.louisiana.gov/), licenses charitable forms under La. R.S. 33:4861.1 et seq. The Louisiana Office of Behavioral Health (LDH) administers the state Compulsive and Problem Gaming Program under La. R.S. 27:601.27.1.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

The Louisiana Gaming Control Board administers the **statewide Self-Exclusion Program** under La. R.S. 27 (Uniform Compulsive and Problem Gambling Program at La. R.S. 27.1) covering all LGCB-regulated gaming. Louisiana's program is broad in scope and offers multiple enrollment paths — including a mail-in option that is rare among state programs.

**Enrollment.** A person seeking exclusion may complete and sign the Request Form in any of four ways: (1) **in person at a Louisiana State Police, Gaming Enforcement Division** location, in the presence of a Division agent; (2) **in person at any LGCB-licensed casino or racetrack**, with compliance staff witnessing; (3) **online** through the LGCB website at https://lgcb.dps.louisiana.gov/voluntary-exclusions/; or (4) **by mail** to LGCB, 8585 Archives Avenue, Suite 100, Baton Rouge, LA 70809. A government-issued photo ID is required.⁴

**Duration.** One year, five years, or lifetime. Louisiana's three-tier menu provides flexibility that two-tier states do not. Five-year and lifetime enrollments are common; the LGCB does not impose a waiting period between durations.

**Scope.** The list covers **all LGCB-regulated gaming establishments**: the fifteen riverboat licensees, Caesars New Orleans, the four racinos, all video poker establishments (truck stops, qualified bars/restaurants, hotels, OTBs), and **retail and mobile sports wagering platforms**. The Uniform Program at La. R.S. 27.1 was specifically designed to apply across casino, video-poker, racino, and sports-wagering venues with a single list. Pari-mutuel-only wagers without slot/HHR component are subject to track responsible-gaming policies but not the consolidated SE list at the same enforcement level. Charitable bingo, lottery retailer sales, and tribal casinos are not within the LGCB list — the three IGRA tribes operate independent tribal exclusion programs.

**Removal process.** One-year and five-year enrollees are removed automatically at term end. Lifetime enrollees may petition for removal under La. R.S. 27 procedures; removal is discretionary with the LGCB and historically restrictive. A clinical certification typically accompanies any lifetime-removal petition.

**Penalties for breach.** While on the list, enrollees cannot engage in gaming, receive comps, hold slot/players-club memberships, receive credit from any casino, cash checks at a casino, or collect winnings — and recovery of losses is barred. The State seizes any winnings found on or paid to an enrolled person; criminal trespass charges may follow at retail venues. The LGCB may discipline operators that willfully permit excluded persons to gamble in person or via a sports-wagering platform.⁵

**Multi-state participation.** Louisiana is not an MSIGA member (no online casino). The LGCB list is not formally reciprocal with adjoining state lists. The three IGRA tribes' programs are independent. National sports-wagering operators apply enrollments on other state lists at the account level under their internal compliance.

**State of the program in 2026.** Louisiana's program is comparatively well-funded among Southern states because gaming revenue is statutorily dedicated to the Compulsive and Problem Gaming Program at the Louisiana Office of Behavioral Health. The state historically operated an inpatient gambling-addiction program at the Center of Recovery (CORE) in Shreveport in the 1990s-2000s — one of the first state-supported residential programs in the U.S. — and continues to support a Louisiana Problem Gambling Treatment Network of outpatient and intensive-outpatient providers. The state helpline is 1-877-770-STOP. The LGCB problem-gambling page is at https://lgcb.dps.louisiana.gov/problem-gambling/.⁶
