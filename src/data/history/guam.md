Guam was ceded to the United States by the Treaty of Paris in 1898 and organized as an unincorporated territory. Chamorro cultural practice during the Spanish colonial period (1668-1898) included cockfighting, card play, and small-stakes village gambling tied to fiestas. U.S. naval administration from 1898 to 1950 and post-war civil administration emphasized military priorities and adopted criminal-code prohibitions on commercial gambling. Those prohibitions survive in modern Guam law at Title 9, Chapter 64 of the Guam Code Annotated. The defining feature of modern Guam gambling policy is decisive voter rejection of casino legalization at every referendum.

In 1977, voters rejected by roughly three-quarters a 1976 package authorizing a government-owned casino and slot machines at Guam International Airport. Casino legalization initiatives failed again in 1996, 2004, and November 2012 (Proposal A, rejected roughly 21,200 to 13,300). On January 5, 2008, voters rejected slot machines at greyhound racetracks by 63 percent. Opposition has united political and religious leadership, giving Guam the longest unbroken record of voter rejection of casino gambling among U.S. jurisdictions where the question has reached the ballot. Guam Greyhound Park in Tamuning operated for decades under Governor Ricardo Bordallo's 1977-78 executive orders but has been closed for years. Cockfighting, a long-licensed Chamorro cultural practice, was preempted by the 2018 Farm Bill effective December 2019. There is no government lottery, no commercial casino, no operating racetrack, and no licensed sports-wagering market.
