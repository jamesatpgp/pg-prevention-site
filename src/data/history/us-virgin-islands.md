The Danish West Indies — St. Thomas, St. John, and St. Croix — passed from Denmark to the United States in 1917. Through the Danish colonial period the islands had no chartered lottery or licensed casino. Horse racing existed informally and became culturally important, with carnival-season racing dating from the early twentieth century. Cockfighting was preempted by the 2018 Farm Bill amendments to the federal Animal Welfare Act. The Virgin Islands Lottery is one of the oldest U.S. lottery operations, established in the mid-1930s. It is an autonomous government entity whose proceeds support education, healthcare, retirement, and veterans services in the territory.

The Casino and Resort Control Act of 1995 legalized casino gaming on St. Croix only — a deliberate policy choice protecting the tourism brand of St. Thomas and St. John. The Virgin Islands Casino Control Commission was created as the licensing and enforcement body. Two casinos operate on St. Croix today: Divi Carina Bay Resort & Casino on the east end and Caravelle Casino & Hotel in Christiansted. Horse racing was modernized under the Virgin Islands Horse Racing Industry Assistance Acts of 2011 and 2016. Hurricanes Irma and Maria in September 2017 closed Clinton E. Phipps Racetrack on St. Thomas for seven years; racing resumed May 3, 2024. In April 2024, the Casino Control Commission issued an initial racino license to VIGL Operations on St. Croix; the license expired April 19, 2025 when construction did not proceed.
