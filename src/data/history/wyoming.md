Wyoming was admitted as the 44th state on July 10, 1890. Cheyenne in the late 1860s and early 1870s rivaled Dodge City and Deadwood for open-saloon gambling along the Union Pacific main line. The 1889 constitution included Article III § 20: "The legislature shall not authorize any game of chance, lottery, or gift enterprise, under any pretense or for any purpose" — language nearly identical to Utah's, dispositive of Wyoming gambling policy for 75 years. The 1965 constitutional amendment first permitted the legislature to authorize pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing. The Wyoming Pari-Mutuel Commission was created by HB 130 of 1967 and operated under that name until reconstituted as the Wyoming Gaming Commission in 2020.

WyoLotto was authorized by 2013 legislation; first ticket sales were August 24, 2014. Wyoming and North Dakota are uniquely the only U.S. lottery jurisdictions that prohibit instant scratch games. Lottery net proceeds go to counties and municipalities rather than to education. House Bill 25 of 2013 redefined "simulcast" to authorize Historic Horse Racing terminals; HHR went live in December 2013. Tribal gaming developed after a decade-long federal-court fight. In 2005 the state was compelled to allow the Northern Arapaho Tribe a casino, and Wind River Hotel and Casino opened in Riverton that year. The Eastern Shoshone subsequently negotiated a compact. Sports betting was legalized by HB 133 in April 2021; the online market launched September 1, 2021 with DraftKings and BetMGM.
