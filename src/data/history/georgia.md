Georgia was founded in 1733 as the last of the original thirteen colonies, under James Oglethorpe's charter that explicitly prohibited organized gambling. The 1877 Georgia Constitution prohibited lotteries and the sale of lottery tickets — a clause still controlling Georgia gambling policy today. The ban has barred a state lottery without constitutional amendment, foreclosed pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing, and prevented commercial casino gaming. Later constitutional revisions have not changed the underlying anti-lottery posture; only narrow exceptions have been carved out. Illegal numbers games operated in Atlanta, Savannah, and other urban centers through the mid-20th century, sometimes linked to the Dixie Mafia. The first major legal exception came in 1976, when a constitutional amendment legalized charitable bingo.

The dominant exception came in November 1992. Voters approved Governor Zell Miller's signature initiative — a constitutional amendment authorizing a state-run lottery dedicated to education. The Georgia Lottery Corporation sold its first ticket on June 29, 1993. Lottery proceeds fund the HOPE Scholarship (Helping Outstanding Pupils Educationally) and the universal Pre-K Program. The third major exception is the Coin-Operated Amusement Machine (COAM) sector. HB 487, signed by Governor Nathan Deal in April 2013, gave the Georgia Lottery regulatory authority over skill-based redemption machines. Class B COAMs at convenience stores and gas stations award non-cash prizes such as lottery tickets or store merchandise — never cash. Efforts to authorize commercial casinos in Atlanta and Savannah (2017-2019) and sports betting (2022-2025) have all failed.
