Hawaii is one of only two U.S. states — Utah being the other — that has authorized no form of state-regulated commercial gambling. As of 2026 there is no state lottery, no commercial or tribal casino, no pari-mutuel racing, no sports wagering, and no charitable bingo or raffles. Wagering long predates Western contact. Native Hawaiians bet on footraces, boxing, holua sled racing, surfing, and the board game konane, especially during the four-month Makahiki festival. Protestant missionaries arriving after 1820 pressed for prohibition, and in August 1825 the chiefs at Honolulu issued a kapu against gambling, drunkenness, theft, and Sabbath-breaking — among the earliest formal gambling bans in the islands.

Through the Kingdom, Republic, and Territorial periods, gambling remained a misdemeanor or, for organized operators, a more serious offense. Statehood in 1959 carried that prohibition forward intact. The 1973 penal code consolidated the gambling offenses in Hawaii Revised Statutes Chapter 712. A narrow "social gambling" exception permits home games among players on equal terms, but it is an affirmative defense rather than an authorization. Act 111 of 2022 elevated promoting gambling in the first degree to a class B felony. Casino-legalization bills have been introduced repeatedly without passage, including HB 1308 in the 2025 session. Hawaii's outbound gambling tourism is among the highest in the country: Las Vegas is widely known as "the Ninth Island," with pre-pandemic Hawaii-to-Las Vegas air traffic peaking at roughly 335,000 passengers in 2019.
