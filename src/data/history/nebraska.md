Frontier-era Omaha, North Platte, and Sidney developed extensive saloon and gambling cultures along the Union Pacific corridor. The 1875 Nebraska Constitution at Article III § 24 barred any lottery authorization — a ban that persisted in part until 1992. Tom Dennison's Omaha political machine, running from 1903 to 1933, protected extensive illegal gambling in the city's Third Ward (the "Burnt District") in exchange for political support, ending after Dennison's 1933 federal indictment. Voters approved pari-mutuel racing and bingo by constitutional amendment in 1934, and the State Racing Commission was created the following year. Ak-Sar-Ben, owned by a civic organization, hosted the Cornhusker Handicap from 1920 until the track closed in 1995. Greyhound racing was never legalized.

The state lottery was authorized by Initiative 415 in November 1992, replacing the 1875 prohibition. The first ticket sold September 11, 1993. Net proceeds are allocated to education, the environmental trust fund, the State Fair, and a Compulsive Gamblers Assistance Fund. Commercial casino expansion came in November 2020, when voters approved three companion ballot measures: Initiative 429 authorizing games of chance at licensed racetrack enclosures, plus companion statutory and tax measures. Casinos opened in phases at existing tracks: WarHorse Lincoln in September 2022, Grand Island Casino in 2022, and WarHorse Omaha in 2023. Retail sports betting launched at WarHorse Lincoln in June 2023. Mobile sports betting requires a further constitutional amendment, which has not yet passed.
