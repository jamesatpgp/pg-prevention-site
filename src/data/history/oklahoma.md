# Oklahoma — Gambling History

## The present

Oklahoma in 2026 has **the largest tribal gaming sector in the United States by number of facilities**: 38 federally recognized tribes operate more than 130 Class II and Class III casinos under IGRA-authorized compacts. Legal forms also include the Oklahoma Education Lottery; pari-mutuel wagering on Thoroughbreds, Quarter Horses, and Appaloosas at three racetracks (Remington Park, Will Rogers Downs, Fair Meadows), all of which operate electronic gaming devices under State Question 712; charitable bingo and instant bingo licensed by the ABLE Commission; and licensed daily fantasy contests. There is no state-regulated commercial sports betting and no iGaming — though both have been debated repeatedly. Regulation is split among the Oklahoma Horse Racing Commission, the Oklahoma Lottery Commission, the ABLE Commission, and 38 tribal gaming commissions, with the Office of the Governor as compact counterparty.¹ ²

## How it got here

Oklahoma's pre-statehood gambling traditions are inseparable from Indian Territory: Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, Muscogee (Creek), Seminole, and Plains Nations carried stick, dice, and ball-play wagering traditions through removal and into the territorial period. Anglo-American commercial gambling arrived with the 1889 Land Run, the railroads, and cattle-trail towns. At statehood in 1907 the Progressive-era constitution prohibited both alcohol (until 1959) and lotteries: Article X, § 36 read "The Legislature shall have no power to authorize lotteries or gift enterprises for any purpose, and shall pass laws to prohibit the sale of lottery tickets in this State."³

Match-race horse-race wagering at county fairgrounds was widely tolerated through the early 20th century but operated outside any pari-mutuel framework. Voters approved **State Question 553 in November 1982**, authorizing pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing. The Oklahoma Horse Racing Act of 1983 (Okla. Stat. tit. 3A §§ 200-205.16) created the Horse Racing Commission. Remington Park (Oklahoma City) opened September 1, 1988; Will Rogers Downs and Fair Meadows followed in their current forms by the mid-2000s.⁴

The most consequential 21st-century events were the **November 2, 2004 ballot propositions**. State Questions 705 and 706 created the Oklahoma Education Lottery (first ticket October 12, 2005; first draw game January 23, 2006); State Question 712 authorized the **Tribal Model Gaming Compact** (Okla. Stat. tit. 3A § 281) and authorized the three pari-mutuel racetracks to operate **electronic gaming devices** (Class III slot machines) under the model framework. The compact authorized electronic devices and certain non-house-banked games, with exclusivity fees paid to the State based on net win.

**2019–2020 compact dispute.** Governor Kevin Stitt asserted the model compacts expired by their terms January 1, 2020 and sought higher exclusivity rates. The tribes contended the compacts auto-renewed. *Cherokee Nation et al. v. Stitt*, Case No. 19-cv-01198 (W.D. Okla. July 28, 2020), ruled the compacts had automatically renewed for an additional 15-year term; the State did not appeal. In parallel, Governor Stitt signed compacts with the Comanche Nation and Otoe-Missouria Tribe authorizing event wagering (sports betting) and house-banked games; the Oklahoma Supreme Court invalidated these in *Treat v. Stitt*, 2020 OK 64, holding the governor lacked unilateral authority beyond the model framework.⁵

**Major operators today** include the Chickasaw Nation (WinStar World Casino & Resort in Thackerville — by some measures the largest casino in the world by gaming-floor area), the Cherokee Nation (Hard Rock Hotel & Casino Tulsa and Cherokee Casino properties), the Choctaw Nation (Durant), the Muscogee (Creek) Nation (River Spirit Tulsa), the Osage Nation (Osage Casinos), and the Citizen Potawatomi Nation (FireLake). State-regulated commercial **sports betting** has not been authorized — repeated bills (HB 1027 of 2023, HB 1101 of 2024, others in the 2025 session) have not passed. **Daily fantasy** operates under the Oklahoma Fantasy Contests Act (Laws of 2017, SB 1252; tit. 3A §§ 280-280.14) licensed by ABLE.

## Regulatory framework today

- **Oklahoma Horse Racing Commission** — racetrack and electronic-gaming-device licensing under SQ 712. https://www.ohrc.org/
- **Oklahoma Lottery Commission** — state Lottery. https://www.lottery.ok.gov/
- **Oklahoma Alcoholic Beverage Laws Enforcement (ABLE) Commission** — charitable bingo, daily fantasy contests. https://oklahoma.gov/able.html
- **Tribal gaming commissions** of 38 federally recognized tribes regulate Class II/III gaming on tribal land; NIGC publishes compact filings and the gaming-tribe roster. https://www.nigc.gov/
- **Oklahoma Department of Mental Health and Substance Abuse Services (ODMHSAS)** — Compulsive Gambling Treatment Program and helpline funding. https://oklahoma.gov/odmhsas.html

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Oklahoma's self-exclusion is **tribal-led and statewide via a single voluntary form coordinated by the Oklahoma Association on Problem Gambling and Gaming (OAPGG)** — a structure unusual among U.S. states because there is no centralized government regulator with authority over all gambling.

**Administering body.** The **OAPGG Statewide Self-Exclusion** program coordinates the master list across participating tribal gaming commissions. **17 of Oklahoma's tribal gaming commissions and 94 casinos** participate as of 2026, meaning a single enrollment covers the vast majority of Oklahoma tribal gaming square footage.⁶ Non-participating tribes maintain their own internal exclusion lists.

**Where to enroll.** A person can:

1. Complete the OAPGG online form and schedule an **online notary session with OAPGG staff** (the modernized 2024-2026 process), or
2. Call OAPGG at (405) 801-3329 to schedule an **in-person appointment** at the OAPGG office.

A participant who only wants to be excluded from a single casino may enroll directly with that tribal gaming commission's internal program; tribes are required by compact to offer such a program.

**Duration.** OAPGG offers terms of **1, 5, or 10 years** — note that 10 years is the OAPGG ceiling, unusual among states. Some individual tribes also offer lifetime through their internal programs.

**Scope.** All 94 participating casinos across 17 tribal gaming commissions. Coverage **does not include**: (a) the Oklahoma Lottery and its retail products, (b) the three pari-mutuel racetracks' electronic gaming devices unless the track is also a tribal facility, (c) charitable bingo, and (d) fantasy contests. The **Oklahoma Lottery** runs its own separate Self-Exclusion Program (SEP) for Lottery players who hold a Players Club account — see https://www.lottery.ok.gov/play-responsibly/self-exclusion.

**Removal.** Removal must be affirmatively requested at the end of the chosen term; lifetime through tribal programs is final.

**Penalties for breach.** A self-excluded person discovered at a participating casino may face **criminal trespass charges**, all winnings are forfeited, and the participant loses any pre-existing player-club benefits.

**Multi-state.** None. Tribal corporate policies (e.g., Hard Rock, Caesars where operated by tribes) may extend voluntarily.

**Help.** Oklahoma Problem Gambling Helpline **1-800-522-4700** (24/7, the NCPG national line with Oklahoma routing).

## Problem gambling response

ODMHSAS administers the **Oklahoma Compulsive Gambling Treatment Program** under tit. 3A § 729; the Lottery dedicates a portion of operating budget for treatment, and tribal compacts include problem-gambling funding contributions. The **Oklahoma Association on Problem Gambling and Gaming (OAPGG)** is the NCPG state affiliate, coordinates Statewide Self-Exclusion, runs the **Smart Play OK** consumer site, distributes posters and brochures to tribal partners, and helps recruit treatment providers. NCPG State of the States surveys have flagged Oklahoma's per-capita problem-gambling spending as modest relative to gaming revenue, though absolute funding has grown with sector growth.⁷
