The most distinctive thread in Arkansas gambling history is the Hot Springs illegal-casino era. Tolerated casino gambling ran continuously in Hot Springs from the late 1860s through the late 1960s, peaking between 1927 and 1947. Owney "The Killer" Madden — formerly of New York's Cotton Club and the Murder, Inc. era — relocated to Hot Springs in 1935 and ran bookmaking and wire-service operations until his death in 1965. Statewide enforcement crackdowns under Governor Winthrop Rockefeller in 1967 finally closed the Hot Springs casinos. Arkansas was the only place in the country where this kind of open-but-illegal casino tradition ran continuously for a century. The Arkansas Constitution of 1874 had formally banned lotteries; horse-race betting was banned by Progressive reform in 1907.

Modern legal gambling came mostly through the ballot box. Pari-mutuel horse racing was formally re-authorized by Act 46 of 1935, anchored at Oaklawn Jockey Club in Hot Springs (founded 1904). Greyhound racing at Southland Park in West Memphis was authorized by 1957 statute and ran until December 31, 2022. The Arkansas Scholarship Lottery was authorized by Constitutional Amendment 87 in November 2008; tickets went on sale September 28, 2009. Constitutional Amendment 100 in November 2018 authorized commercial casinos at Oaklawn and Southland by name and two new casinos in Jefferson and Pope counties. Saracen Casino Resort opened in October 2020 in Pine Bluff. The Pope County license remains contested in litigation. Retail sports betting launched July 1, 2019; mobile sports betting launched March 5, 2022.
