# Kansas — Gambling History

Kansas in 2026 operates a structurally unusual gambling regime: the Kansas Lottery technically "owns" all gaming at the four state-owned casinos (one in each of the Northeast, Southeast, South Central, and Southwest gaming zones), while private "managers" build and run the facilities. Add to that four federally recognized tribal casinos in northeast Kansas under IGRA compacts, the Kansas Lottery and Powerball/Mega Millions, retail and mobile sports wagering (since Sept. 1, 2022), and charitable bingo and raffles. There is no commercial pari-mutuel racing in operation; the Woodlands closed in 2008 and racinos contemplated under the 2007 Expanded Lottery Act never opened. The Kansas Racing and Gaming Commission (KRGC) regulates the state-owned casinos and sports wagering; the Kansas Lottery operates the lottery; the Kansas Department of Revenue licenses charitable gaming.¹

The cattle-trail era (1860s-1880s) made Wichita, Abilene, Ellsworth, and Dodge City famous for faro, monte, poker, keno, and dice — Wyatt Earp and Bat Masterson worked as both peace officers and faro dealers. The 1859 Wyandotte Constitution included a strict lottery ban, and Methodist-Baptist temperance reformers, Carry Nation's saloon-smashing, and the Populist movement pushed Kansas into one of the most restrictive anti-gambling and anti-alcohol regimes in the country. The 1881 alcohol-prohibition amendment marked the same reform energy that locked gambling out for over a century.² Bingo was authorized by 1974 constitutional amendment; pari-mutuel wagering was approved by voters in November 1986 and the legislature enacted the Kansas Parimutuel Racing Act the next year (K.S.A. 74-8801 et seq.). The Woodlands (Kansas City, Kansas), Camptown Greyhound Park (Frontenac), and Wichita Greyhound Park opened, plus smaller summer-meet venues at Eureka Downs and Anthony Downs; the racing industry struggled commercially and the Woodlands closed in 2008. Raffles by qualified charities were authorized by constitutional amendment in 2014.

The modern Kansas Lottery was authorized by November 1986 amendment and Kansas Lottery Act 1987 (Kansas joined Powerball at its 1988 founding). Tribal casinos opened in 1996-1998: Casino White Cloud (Iowa Tribe of Kansas and Nebraska), Golden Eagle Casino (Kickapoo Tribe in Kansas), Sac and Fox Casino (Sac and Fox Nation of Missouri in Kansas and Nebraska), and Prairie Band Casino and Resort (Prairie Band Potawatomi Nation, branded for a period as Harrah's Prairie Band).³ The Kansas Expanded Lottery Act of 2007 (Senate Bill 66, signed by Gov. Kathleen Sebelius) created the state-owned casino structure to thread the constitutional needle: the state owns the devices and table-games rights, with private managers under contract. Boot Hill Casino & Resort (Dodge City, Dec. 15, 2009), Kansas Star Casino (Mulvane, Dec. 2011), Hollywood Casino at Kansas Speedway (Kansas City KS, Feb. 2012), and Kansas Crossing Casino (Pittsburg, March 2017) followed. The state-owned model produces an effective state tax of approximately 27% of casino gross gaming revenue plus local and problem-gambling allocations.⁴ Senate Bill 84 (2022), signed by Gov. Laura Kelly, legalized sports wagering at the four state-owned casinos and via mobile platforms; launch was Sept. 1, 2022. In 2025, the Kansas Attorney General opined that the state could legally negotiate a Class III compact with the Wyandotte Nation, opening a path for a potential additional tribal casino in Kansas City, Kansas.

## Regulatory framework today

The Kansas Racing and Gaming Commission (https://krgc.kansas.gov/) regulates the state-owned casinos and sports wagering. The Kansas Lottery (https://www.kslottery.com/) operates the lottery and "owns" the casino gaming devices/rights under K.S.A. Ch. 74, Art. 87/98. The Kansas Department of Revenue licenses charitable gaming. The Kansas Department for Aging and Disability Services (KDADS), Behavioral Health Services, administers the Problem Gambling and Addictions Grant Fund (K.S.A. 79-4805 et seq.).

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

The Kansas Racing and Gaming Commission administers two related programs: a statewide **Voluntary Exclusion Program (VEP)** for the state-owned casinos and a **Sports Wagering Exclusion Program** for retail and mobile sportsbooks.

**Enrollment for casino VEP.** A person may enroll at any of the four state-owned casinos by asking for a KRGC agent at the property — no appointment required. Enrollment is also available at the KRGC Topeka office by appointment (call 785-296-5800). Online application is published at https://krgc.kansas.gov/problem-gambling/how-to-apply/ for sports wagering and casino programs, with state/government-issued photo ID required. The KRGC operates the formal program pages at https://krgc.kansas.gov/problem-gambling/voluntary-exclusion-program/ and https://krgc.kansas.gov/problem-gambling/sports-wagering-exclusion-program/.⁵

**Enrollment for sports wagering.** The KRGC accepts sports-wagering exclusion applications by email (Garrett.Rogers@ks.gov) with a photo of a government-issued ID. The state's mobile sportsbooks (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars, ESPN BET, Fanatics) are required to enforce the KRGC sports-wagering exclusion list against account creation and wagers.

**Duration.** Casino VEP: two years or lifetime (the older statutory pairing). The combined online application also permits one-to-five-year or permanent durations for the sports-wagering exclusion program. Sources differ slightly on duration menu by year of update; the KRGC pages should be relied on for the operative menu.

**Scope.** The VEP covers the four state-owned casinos. The Sports Wagering Exclusion Program covers all retail and mobile sportsbooks tethered to those casinos. The four tribal casinos (Prairie Band Potawatomi, Kickapoo, Iowa, Sac and Fox) operate their own tribal exclusion programs and are not subject to the KRGC list, although they coordinate informally with KRGC; the Kansas Lottery operates a separate retailer-side responsible-play framework but no consolidated list.

**Removal process.** Two-year enrollees are removed automatically at term end. Lifetime/permanent enrollment is irrevocable.

**Penalties for breach.** Enrollees who enter the gaming floor of a state-owned casino or who place a wager with a Kansas-licensed sportsbook are subject to criminal trespass charges, forfeiture of winnings to the State (which feeds the Problem Gambling and Addictions Grant Fund), and loss of player-club privileges. Operators that knowingly permit excluded wagers face KRGC administrative discipline.

**Multi-state participation.** Kansas is not a member of MSIGA. The KRGC does not formally share its list across state borders. Tribal exclusion lists in northeast Kansas are not consolidated with the state list, leaving a recognized gap between commercial and tribal venues.

**State of the program in 2026.** Kansas's program is comparatively well-funded for its population because the 2007 Expanded Lottery Act built a dedicated statutory funding stream (Problem Gambling and Addictions Grant Fund, administered by KDADS Behavioral Health Services) into the initial legislation rather than relying on general-fund appropriations — a design that insulates problem-gambling services from budget volatility. The 2022 sports-wagering act extended messaging and SE list requirements to mobile sportsbooks. Helpline: 1-800-522-4700.⁶
