# North Carolina — Gambling History

## The present

North Carolina in 2026 occupies an unusual middle position: no commercial casinos, no pari-mutuel racing, but a robust state lottery, a young and very large mobile sports-betting market, and two operating tribal casinos plus a Catawba facility under construction. Legal forms are the North Carolina Education Lottery; in-person and statewide mobile sports wagering licensed by the North Carolina State Lottery Commission (launched March 11, 2024); Class III tribal gaming at three federally recognized facilities; charitable bingo and raffles; and the distinctly North Carolina "beach bingo" tradition. There is no iGaming, no DFS-specific licensing regime, and no commercial casino. The Lottery Commission is also the home of the new Sports Wagering Division.¹ ²

## How it got here

Colonial North Carolina was generally hostile to commercial gambling, and the General Assembly's 1834 lottery ban (Laws of 1834-35, ch. 14) was followed by an even harder line in the 1868 Reconstruction Constitution and the 1876 revision: a flat constitutional ban on lotteries that survived without amendment for 129 years.³ N.C. Gen. Stat. Chapter 14, Article 37 ("Lotteries, Gaming, Bingo, and Raffles") and the 1937 Slot Machine Law (Laws of 1937, ch. 196) reinforced criminal prohibition.

Charitable bingo arrived in 1979 (Laws of 1979, ch. 893); "beach bingo" — low-stakes (prizes $10 or less) games conducted without a license — was carved out at N.C. Gen. Stat. § 14-309.14, a uniquely North Carolina coastal artifact. The Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians opened Harrah's Cherokee on November 13, 1997 as a Class II/video-only facility; the 2012 compact added Class III table games (launched September 28, 2012). Harrah's Cherokee Valley River opened September 28, 2015; Catawba Two Kings Casino Resort opened in temporary form July 1, 2021 in Kings Mountain on land taken into trust in North Carolina in 2020 after extensive federal litigation.⁴

The constitutional turn came in November 2006, when voters had already lived with the August 31, 2005 enactment of the North Carolina State Lottery Act (S.L. 2005-344). The first scratch ticket sold March 30, 2006; the first draw game launched May 30, 2006. By statute (N.C. Gen. Stat. § 18C-164), net Lottery proceeds fund NC Pre-K, K-12 class-size reduction, school construction, university and community-college need-based scholarships. Cumulative transfers to the Education Lottery Fund exceeded $9 billion by FY2024.⁵

After *Murphy v. NCAA*, 138 S. Ct. 1461 (2018), the 2019 legislature authorized in-person tribal sports betting (S.L. 2019-201); the first sportsbook opened at Harrah's Cherokee on March 18, 2021. Statewide mobile sports wagering followed via House Bill 347 (S.L. 2023-42), signed June 14, 2023; mobile launched **March 11, 2024** with eight licensed operators. First-week handle exceeded $1.5 billion. The statutory rate is 18% of gross wagering revenue with revenue split among the NC Major Events Fund, university athletics, and the DHHS gambling-treatment fund.⁶ Pari-mutuel and ADW horse-racing authority granted by HB 347 has not been activated as of 2026.

## Regulatory framework today

- **North Carolina State Lottery Commission / Sports Wagering Division** — licenses up to twelve interactive sports-wagering operators, retail sports-wagering venues, and (when activated) horse-racing pari-mutuel and ADW. Administers the statewide **Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program**. https://nclotterycommission.nc.gov/sports-wagering and https://ncgaming.gov/
- **North Carolina Education Lottery** — game operations under the Commission. https://nclottery.com/
- **Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians Tribal Gaming Commission** — Harrah's Cherokee properties. https://ebci-tgc.com/
- **Catawba Nation Gaming Commission** — Two Kings Casino. (Catawba operates under a compact with North Carolina even though it is a South Carolina-based tribe.)
- **NC DHHS, Division of Mental Health/DD/SUS** — North Carolina Problem Gambling Program (https://www.morethanagame.nc.gov/).
- **Department of Public Safety, Alcohol Law Enforcement (ALE) Division** — charitable bingo enforcement; **Secretary of State** maintains bingo/raffle license records.

## Voluntary self-exclusion

The **NC State Lottery Commission Voluntary Self-Exclusion (VSE) Program**, codified at 24 NCAC 06A .0602 and approved by the Commission on the eve of the March 11, 2024 mobile launch, is the state's only formal statewide self-exclusion mechanism. It covers sports wagering only — both mobile and retail — and (when activated) pari-mutuel horse race wagering. It does **not** apply to the Education Lottery, to charitable gaming, or to the tribal casinos.⁷

**Scope.** All twelve potential interactive sports-wagering licensees and any retail sportsbook tied to a state license. The list is shared with operators; they must freeze accounts and block account creation in North Carolina.

**Duration.** One, three, five years, or lifetime. (Three years is unusually offered, mirroring New York.)

**How to enroll.** Two paths. (1) **Online** through the Lottery Commission portal — enrollees verify identity by ID upload or knowledge-based questions; the program takes effect within three to four business days. (2) **In person** at any NC Education Lottery Claims Center. Tribal casinos and the Lottery's retail terminals are separate environments; the EBCI Tribal Gaming Commission runs its own internal exclusion process for Harrah's Cherokee and Valley River.

**Removal.** At the end of a one-, three-, or five-year term, removal must be affirmatively requested; lifetime is permanent.

**Penalties for breach.** Any winnings placed by a self-excluded person are surrendered as unclaimed property; the operator faces Commission enforcement for failing to detect.

**Multi-state.** No formal multi-state compact. Most major operators apply cross-state policy at affiliated platforms.

**Uptake** has been low — local journalism reported only a few hundred enrollments in the first months of mobile betting, leading the Lottery Commission and DHHS to step up outreach.⁸

**Help.** Statewide gambling helpline 1-877-718-5543, with text and chat options through MoreThanAGame.nc.gov. Source: NC Gaming Commission Responsible Gaming page (https://ncgaming.gov/responsible-gaming-2/) and 24 NCAC 06A .0602.

## Problem gambling response

The **NC Division of Mental Health/DD/SUS** administers gambling-disorder treatment under a long-standing Lottery Act dedication (N.C. Gen. Stat. § 18C-163) and the new **Problem Gambling Education and Treatment Fund** created by S.L. 2023-42 — an initial $2 million annual sports-wagering dedication codified at N.C. Gen. Stat. § 18C-1014. The NC Problem Gambling Program is the state's NCPG affiliate of record and operates MoreThanAGame.nc.gov, the 1-877-718-5543 helpline, clinician training, and provider contracting.⁹
