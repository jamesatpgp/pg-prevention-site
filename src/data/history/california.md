# California — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
California is a high-volume, structurally fragmented gambling market: it has the largest tribal-gaming economy in the United States by gross gaming revenue and no commercial Las-Vegas-style casinos. Legal verticals include the **California State Lottery** (Powerball, Mega Millions, scratch, daily numbers; administered by the **California State Lottery Commission**); **pari-mutuel horse racing** at Santa Anita, Del Mar, and the fair circuit (regulated by the **California Horse Racing Board**); **tribal Class III casinos** at 60+ properties operated under Proposition 1A (2000) compacts; and a parallel **cardroom industry** of approximately 65 licensees offering player-banked games (regulated by the **California Gambling Control Commission** and enforced by the **Bureau of Gambling Control** at the Department of Justice). California has **no legal sports betting** following voter rejection of Propositions 26 and 27 in November 2022. Daily fantasy sports operates in legal uncertainty after the July 2025 California Attorney General opinion declaring DFS unauthorized. There is no legal online casino. Greyhound racing has never been legal in California.[^1][^2]

## How California got here
California's gambling history begins with the **Gold Rush (1849-1855)** and the unregulated commercial saloon culture of San Francisco, anchored by the El Dorado, Parker House, Empire, and Bella Union. Statehood arrived September 9, 1850, and the 1872 California Penal Code created the lasting legal framework: **Penal Code § 330** prohibited faro, monte, roulette, lansquenet, rouge et noir, rondo, tan, fan-tan, seven-and-a-half, twenty-one, hokey-pokey, and "any banking or percentage game" — but conspicuously **omitted poker**, a legislative gap that defines California cardroom law to this day. The 1879 Constitution added Article IV § 19 prohibiting lotteries. A 1911 Attorney General opinion confirmed draw poker remained legal under § 330's omission; 1937 amendments codified the banked-vs-non-banked distinction; and **Gardena** became a center of permitted draw-poker clubs from the late 1930s. Prohibition-era offshore gambling ships — the Rex, the Lux, the Tango — operated off Santa Monica until California Attorney General Earl Warren's 1939 raids.[^1][^3]

Pari-mutuel betting on horse racing was legalized by ballot in **1933**; the California Horse Racing Board was established the same year. **Santa Anita Park** opened December 25, 1934, **Del Mar** on July 3, 1937 (co-founded by Bing Crosby), and **Hollywood Park** (1938-2013) and **Bay Meadows** (1934-2008) both have since closed and been redeveloped; **Golden Gate Fields** ended live racing June 9, 2024. The **California State Lottery** was authorized by Proposition 37 (November 1984, 58% in favor) with first ticket sales October 3, 1985; Mega Millions joined June 22, 2005 and Powerball April 8, 2013. Net proceeds are constitutionally dedicated to public education.[^4][^5]

Tribal gaming followed three landmark legal events: the U.S. Supreme Court's 1987 *California v. Cabazon Band of Mission Indians* decision (480 U.S. 202) — which held California's Class III prohibition did not apply to tribal lands and directly triggered Congress's 1988 IGRA — followed by **Proposition 5** (November 1998, 63%-37%), which the California Supreme Court struck down in *Hotel Employees & Restaurant Employees v. Davis* (1999), and then **Proposition 1A** (March 2000, 65%-35%), which amended the constitution to authorize tribal Class III gaming by compact. Approximately 57 California tribes signed compacts ratified by the Legislature and approved by Interior. Pechanga, San Manuel, Yaamava', Cache Creek, Thunder Valley, and Morongo are among the largest properties. The parallel cardroom sector — Commerce Casino (the world's largest poker room by table count), Bicycle, Hustler, Hawaiian Gardens, Bay 101 / M8trix — operates under Penal Code § 337j and the **Gambling Control Act of 1997**.[^6][^7]

The November 2022 ballot fight produced the most expensive ballot-measure campaign in U.S. history (~$450 million spent) and rejected both **Proposition 26** (tribal retail sports betting, ~30% Yes) and **Proposition 27** (commercial online sports betting, ~16% Yes), freezing sports-wagering expansion through at least 2024-2026. **California Attorney General Opinion No. 23-1201** (July 2025) declared DFS contests an unlawful form of sports wagering.[^8]

## Regulatory framework today
The **California Gambling Control Commission** (CGCC, [cgcc.ca.gov](https://www.cgcc.ca.gov/)) licenses cardrooms and ratifies tribal-state compact terms. The **Bureau of Gambling Control** at the Department of Justice ([oag.ca.gov/gambling](https://oag.ca.gov/gambling)) provides enforcement and field investigations. The **California Horse Racing Board** ([chrb.ca.gov](https://chrb.ca.gov/)) regulates pari-mutuel racing. The **California State Lottery Commission** ([calottery.com](https://www.calottery.com/)) operates the lottery. **Tribal gaming** is administered by each tribe's Tribal Gaming Commission under IGRA and the federal NIGC. The **California Office of Problem Gambling (CalOPG)** within the **California Department of Public Health** ([cdph.ca.gov](https://www.cdph.ca.gov/Programs/OPG/Pages/opg-landing.aspx)) runs prevention, helpline, and treatment funding, capitalized via the Gambling Addiction Program Fund sourced from the tribal Special Distribution Fund.[^9]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
California operates **two parallel statutory self-exclusion mechanisms** plus tribal-by-tribe enrollment — a uniquely fragmented structure that mirrors the state's split regulatory architecture.

**(1) Statewide cardroom Self-Exclusion Program.** The **Bureau of Gambling Control** at the Department of Justice administers a confidential statewide list of patrons who have voluntarily agreed to be excluded from **all licensed cardrooms** in California. **Where to enroll**: the request form is printed from the CGCC website; the patron completes personal information, a physical description, encloses a recent photograph, and submits the package to the Bureau ([oag.ca.gov/gambling/exclusion_self](https://oag.ca.gov/gambling/exclusion_self)). **Duration options**: **one year, five years, or lifetime**. The agreement is **irrevocable** for the chosen term; patrons may not be removed before term expiration. The lifetime selection is permanent. **Scope**: covers cardrooms only. The list does **not** apply to tribal casinos and does **not** apply to horse racing pari-mutuel wagering facilities. **Penalties**: a self-excluded patron forfeits any winnings and cannot recover losses, and is subject to trespass; licensees that knowingly permit play face administrative sanction. There is also a separate **Self-Restriction Program** that licensees must administer at the property level: an irrevocable voluntary agreement restricting access, credit, check cashing, or marketing at a specific cardroom rather than statewide.[^9][^10]

**(2) Tribal casinos**. The statewide Bureau program does **not** cover California's 60+ tribal casinos. Each federally recognized tribe with a Class III compact administers its own self-exclusion list through its tribal Gaming Commission. A patron seeking effective exclusion from all California tribal venues must enroll separately at each tribe; there is no centralized tribal self-exclusion roster across California, although the **CALPG self-exclusion clearinghouse at [howtoexclude.org](https://howtoexclude.org/)** (a nonprofit project of the California Council on Problem Gambling) provides a navigation tool listing each tribe's contact information.[^10][^11]

**(3) National voluntary multi-state participation**. In 2024-2025 California joined the **National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program** coordinated through the NCPG — a private cross-state initiative — although the National program has no jurisdiction over California tribal casinos, which remain individually administered. CalOPG estimates that the combined statewide cardroom list contains several thousand enrolled patrons; exact 2026 figures are **[unconfirmed]** because the Bureau's list is confidential and only accessible to DOJ staff, law enforcement, and key cardroom employees. State helpline: **1-800-GAMBLER** (operated through CalOPG and the California Council on Problem Gambling). **Horse-racing pari-mutuel facilities** are administered separately by the CHRB and operate property-level account-suspension and self-restriction tools rather than a statewide racing-specific exclusion list.[^9][^11]
