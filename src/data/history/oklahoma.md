Oklahoma's pre-statehood gambling traditions are inseparable from Indian Territory: Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, Muscogee (Creek), Seminole, and Plains Nations carried stick, dice, and ball-play wagering traditions through removal and into the territorial period. Anglo-American commercial gambling arrived with the 1889 Land Run, the railroads, and cattle-trail towns. At statehood in 1907 the Progressive-era constitution prohibited both alcohol (until 1959) and lotteries: Article X § 36 read, "The Legislature shall have no power to authorize lotteries or gift enterprises for any purpose." Voters approved State Question 553 in November 1982, authorizing pari-mutuel wagering. Remington Park in Oklahoma City opened on September 1, 1988.

The most consequential 21st-century events were the November 2, 2004 ballot propositions. State Questions 705 and 706 created the Oklahoma Education Lottery; the first ticket sold October 12, 2005. State Question 712 authorized the Tribal Model Gaming Compact and authorized the three pari-mutuel racetracks to operate electronic gaming devices. Oklahoma now has the largest tribal gaming sector in the United States by number of facilities: 38 federally recognized tribes operate more than 130 casinos. The Chickasaw Nation's WinStar World Casino & Resort in Thackerville is by some measures the largest casino in the world by gaming-floor area. Governor Kevin Stitt asserted in 2020 that the model compacts had expired and sought higher exclusivity rates; the federal district court ruled in Cherokee Nation v. Stitt that the compacts had automatically renewed. Sports betting bills have failed repeatedly through 2025.
