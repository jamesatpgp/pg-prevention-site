# Arkansas — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
Arkansas is a moderately permissive state with a four-casino footprint, full retail and mobile sports betting, a scholarship-dedicated lottery, and a robust charitable-gaming sector. Legal verticals: the **Arkansas Scholarship Lottery** (Powerball, Mega Millions, scratch; administered by the Arkansas Scholarship Lottery within the Department of Finance and Administration); commercial casino gaming at **Oaklawn Racing Casino Resort** (Hot Springs), **Southland Casino Resort** (West Memphis), and **Saracen Casino Resort** (Pine Bluff) under Constitutional Amendment 100 (with a contested fourth Pope County license still in litigation); **pari-mutuel thoroughbred racing** at Oaklawn; **retail sports betting** since July 1, 2019 and **mobile sports betting** since March 5, 2022; and **charitable bingo and raffles** under Amendment 84. Live greyhound racing ended December 31, 2022. The **Arkansas Racing Commission** (within the **Arkansas Department of Finance and Administration**) is the principal gaming regulator.[^1][^2]

## How Arkansas got here
Arkansas territory (1819) and the state (admitted June 15, 1836) hosted frontier riverboat and saloon gambling along the Mississippi and Arkansas rivers, and the Arkansas Constitution of 1874 (still in force, heavily amended) included an explicit lottery prohibition. The state formally banned horse-race betting in 1907 as part of the Progressive reform wave. The single most distinctive thread in Arkansas gambling history is the **Hot Springs illegal-casino era**: openly tolerated casino gambling ran continuously from the late 1860s through the late 1960s, with a heyday of **1927-1947** featuring the Southern Club, Ohio Club, Belvedere, Arkansas Club, Indiana Club, Kentucky Club, Bridge Club, and later the Vapors (1960). **Owney "The Killer" Madden** — formerly of New York's Cotton Club and the Murder, Inc. milieu — relocated to Hot Springs in 1935 and ran bookmaking and wire-service operations until his death in 1965. Statewide enforcement crackdowns under **Governor Winthrop Rockefeller in 1967** finally closed the Hot Springs casinos — the only U.S. locale with such a continuous open-but-illegal casino tradition.[^3]

Pari-mutuel horse racing was formally re-authorized in 1935 (Act 46), and **Oaklawn Jockey Club** (founded 1904 in Hot Springs) became the state's sole continuous thoroughbred operation. **Southland Greyhound Park** opened in 1956 in West Memphis under Act 191 of 1957; live greyhound racing ended at Southland on December 31, 2022. The **Arkansas Scholarship Lottery** was authorized by Constitutional **Amendment 87** in November 2008 (648,122 to 383,467) and began ticket sales September 28, 2009; the lottery has raised more than $1.4 billion for the **Arkansas Academic Challenge Scholarship**. Modern casino gaming derives from **Constitutional Amendment 100** (Issue 4, November 2018, 54.1% in favor), which licensed Oaklawn and Southland by name and authorized two new casinos in Jefferson and Pope counties. **Saracen Casino Resort** opened in October 2020. The fourth license has been litigated through multiple Arkansas Supreme Court rulings; the Court nullified earlier decisions in 2023 (*Cherokee Nation Businesses LLC v. Gulfside Casino Partnership*) and the Pope County license remains contested into 2026.[^4][^5][^6]

Retail sports betting launched July 1, 2019 at Oaklawn and Southland under the Arkansas Racing Commission's Casino Gaming Rule 2.13; mobile sports betting launched March 5, 2022, with each operating casino licensed for a single mobile skin. There is no licensed online casino.[^5][^6]

## Regulatory framework today
The **Arkansas Racing Commission**, within the **Arkansas Department of Finance and Administration**, licenses and regulates casinos, racing, and sports wagering ([dfa.arkansas.gov/racing-commission](https://www.dfa.arkansas.gov/racing-commission/)). The **DFA Casino Gaming Section** administers casino-tax compliance and licensee oversight ([dfa.arkansas.gov/office/taxes/other-taxes/field-audit-administration/casino-gaming-section](https://www.dfa.arkansas.gov/office/taxes/other-taxes/field-audit-administration/casino-gaming-section/)). The **Arkansas Scholarship Lottery** ([myarkansaslottery.com](https://www.myarkansaslottery.com/)) is separately housed within DFA. **Charitable bingo and raffles** are regulated by the **DFA Office of Charitable Gaming** ([dfa.arkansas.gov/charitable-gaming](https://www.dfa.arkansas.gov/charitable-gaming/)). Problem-gambling treatment is coordinated through the **Arkansas Department of Human Services, Division of Aging, Adult, and Behavioral Health Services**.[^7]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
Arkansas's voluntary self-exclusion architecture is **decentralized**: there is no single statewide list maintained by the Racing Commission. Instead, the **Arkansas Racing Commission Casino Gaming Rules** (notably Rules 4.220 and 4.225 within Casino Gaming Rule 4 — Operation of Gaming Establishments) **require each licensed casino to administer its own self-exclusion program** and to provide information about treatment resources. The result is property-specific enrollment at each of the three operating casinos: **Oaklawn**, **Southland**, and **Saracen**.[^5][^8]

**Where to enroll**: There is no statewide online portal on the Racing Commission's website for direct VSE enrollment. Patrons must enroll at the casino itself. At **Southland Casino Resort** (West Memphis), the self-exclusion form is available through the Security department; the published contact is **(870) 732-8389**, and forms may be downloaded and submitted in person on the gaming floor. **Oaklawn** and **Saracen** maintain analogous in-property enrollment desks; both provide problem-gambling resources at responsible-gaming kiosks on the casino floor and offer self-exclusion through their compliance offices.[^8]

**Duration options**: Property-by-property — there is no single statewide menu. **Southland's** published program offers **two-year exclusion blocks**, with renewable extension. **Oaklawn** and **Saracen** durations are **[unconfirmed]** in publicly accessible Racing Commission filings; both operate within the Rule 4.220 / 4.225 framework that requires durations and removal procedures be documented in each licensee's internal responsible-gaming plan filed with the Commission.[^8]

**Scope**: Property-specific. Enrollment at Southland excludes the player from Southland's casino floor, retail sportsbook, and (per the licensee's mobile-skin terms) the casino-affiliated mobile sportsbook account. Enrollment does **not** automatically cross-bar the player from Oaklawn or Saracen; a player who wishes statewide effect must enroll separately at each casino. The Arkansas Scholarship Lottery operates an internal **iLottery responsible-play account block**; this is distinct from the casino self-exclusion lists.

**Removal**: Per casino-level policy. At Southland the two-year period elapses, and removal generally requires affirmative request after the term — auto-expiration with re-entry permitted upon request, not auto-renewal. **Lifetime** options have not been documented in publicly accessible Arkansas materials and are **[unconfirmed]**.[^8]

**Penalties for breach**: Self-excluded patrons who enter and play forfeit winnings; licensees that knowingly allow self-excluded play face regulatory action under Casino Gaming Rule 4 and Section 14 of the Arkansas Casino Gaming Rules. **Multi-state participation**: Arkansas is **not** a signatory to MSIGA (irrelevant — no licensed iGaming) and does not publicly join any tribal multi-state exclusion database. Treatment routing in 2026 uses helpline **1-800-522-4700** (the NCPG national line). This decentralized structure is a meaningful structural weakness in the Arkansas program; statewide aggregation of self-exclusion enrollment would require Racing Commission rulemaking that has not yet occurred.[^5][^8][^9]
