# Maryland — Gambling History

Maryland in 2026 hosts six commercial casinos (Hollywood Casino Perryville, Ocean Downs Casino, Maryland Live!, Rocky Gap, Horseshoe Casino Baltimore, and MGM National Harbor — the state's largest), the Maryland State Lottery (since 1973), thoroughbred and standardbred pari-mutuel racing (Preakness at Pimlico, Laurel Park, Rosecroft, Ocean Downs), retail and mobile sports wagering (retail since December 2021, mobile since November 2022), comprehensive county-specific charitable gaming, and authorized daily fantasy sports. The Maryland Lottery and Gaming Control Agency (MLGCA), formed by 2012 consolidation, regulates the casinos, sports wagering, and the lottery. The Maryland Racing Commission regulates the tracks, with Pimlico now undergoing a state-supervised rebuild under the Maryland Stadium Authority and the 2020 Racing and Community Development Act. Maryland has no federally recognized tribes with reservation land and therefore no IGRA tribal casinos. iGaming has been proposed repeatedly through 2024-2026 but not enacted.¹

Maryland's gambling history reaches back to the colonial era — the **Maryland Jockey Club**, organized in 1743, is one of the oldest sporting organizations in North America. Pimlico Race Course opened in Baltimore in 1870, and the **Preakness Stakes** was first run there in 1873, the second-oldest of the three Triple Crown races. Colonial and early-state lotteries financed roads, bridges, and the Baltimore Washington Monument (1815) before mid-19th-century reform — and the contagion of the Louisiana Lottery Company scandal (1868-1894) — pushed Maryland to ban lotteries by constitutional and statutory action. Slot machines were openly tolerated in mid-20th-century **Southern Maryland**, where the 1949 General Assembly explicitly authorized slots in St. Mary's, Charles, Calvert, and Anne Arundel counties — making Maryland the "slot machine capital of the East Coast" until 1968 legislation phased the machines out amid corruption scandals tied to local political machines.²

The modern lottery was authorized by 1972 amendment, and the Maryland State Lottery began ticket sales **January 2, 1973**. Maryland was innovative early: the first numbers-style daily game ("Pick Three") tied to a public drawing helped displace the illegal numbers racket. Casino expansion came in two votes. **November 2008 Question 2** authorized video lottery terminals (VLTs — technically a "lottery" expansion, not "casinos") at up to five locations across designated jurisdictions; Hollywood Casino Perryville opened first (Sept. 27, 2010), followed by Ocean Downs Casino (Jan. 2011), Maryland Live! at Arundel Mills (June 2012), Rocky Gap Casino Resort (May 2013), and Horseshoe Casino Baltimore (Aug. 2014). **November 2012 Question 7** authorized live table games at all five existing casinos and a sixth casino in Prince George's County; MGM Resorts and Penn National combined for roughly $80 million in advertising — at the time, the most expensive Maryland ballot-question campaign in history — and the measure passed by about 101,531 votes. Table games launched at Hollywood Perryville on March 6, 2013, and MGM National Harbor opened Dec. 8, 2016, immediately becoming the state's largest casino by revenue.³ **November 2020 Question 2** authorized sports wagering by constitutional amendment; HB940 (May 2021) was the implementing legislation, codified at Business Regulation Title 9, Subtitle 1A. The Sports Wagering Application Review Commission (SWARC) administered licensing. Retail launched Dec. 9, 2021; mobile was delayed by SWARC's slow review (Gov. Hogan publicly criticized the delay in June 2022) and launched Nov. 23, 2022. By mid-2024, sports wagering had generated more than $100 million for the Blueprint for Maryland's Future Fund (K-12 education).

## Regulatory framework today

The Maryland Lottery and Gaming Control Agency (https://www.mdgaming.com/) regulates casinos, sports wagering, and the lottery under Maryland Code State Government Title 9 Subtitles 1 and 1A and Business Regulation Title 9 Subtitle 1A. The Maryland Racing Commission regulates pari-mutuel racing under Business Regulation Title 11. Charitable gaming is regulated county-by-county under Maryland Code Criminal Law Title 12 Subtitle 1 and county-specific Public Local Laws — a structural feature unique to Maryland reflecting 19th- and early-20th-century reform compromises. The Maryland Department of Health, Behavioral Health Administration, administers the Problem Gambling Fund. The Maryland Center of Excellence on Problem Gambling at the University of Maryland School of Medicine (https://www.mdproblemgambling.com/) coordinates research and clinician training.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

The MLGCA administers the **Voluntary Exclusion Program (VEP)**, established in 2011 and expanded in 2021-2022 to cover sports wagering and daily fantasy sports. The Maryland program is comparatively broad in scope, applying to casinos, the lottery, MLGCA-regulated bingo halls, sports wagering, and daily fantasy sports under a single list.⁴

**Enrollment.** A person must complete the VEP application and submit it **in person** at one of two location types: (1) **Maryland Lottery and Gaming Headquarters in Baltimore** (1800 Washington Boulevard) **by appointment only** — scheduled by emailing the MLGCA Compliance team or calling 410-230-8798; or (2) **with a Maryland Lottery and Gaming Compliance official on duty at any of Maryland's six casinos** (Hollywood Perryville, Ocean Downs, Maryland Live!, Rocky Gap, Horseshoe Baltimore, MGM National Harbor). A **government-issued photo ID** is required. The applicant cannot be under the influence of alcohol or controlled substances during the application — a sobriety requirement Maryland enforces as a condition of validity. No fully online enrollment is offered as of 2026, although the Maryland Alliance for Responsible Gambling (MARG) and other clinical partners assist applicants through the process.⁵

**Duration.** **Two years or lifetime.** Maryland is among the more restrictive in offering only two duration tiers — no one-year, three-year, or five-year option exists. Two-year enrollees roll off automatically at the end of the term; lifetime enrollment is irrevocable.

**Scope.** The single MLGCA VEP list covers (a) all six commercial **casinos** (slots, table games, on-site sports wagering, and player-club privileges); (b) **retail and mobile sports wagering** with all Maryland-licensed operators (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars, Fanatics, ESPN BET, and casino-tethered books); (c) **daily fantasy sports** with MLGCA-licensed operators; (d) MLGCA-regulated **bingo halls in Anne Arundel and Calvert counties**; and (e) the **Maryland State Lottery**, including iLottery and Lottery retailer purchases. The Maryland lottery's inclusion under the same exclusion list is unusual — most states maintain a separate lottery program — and reflects the 2012 MLGCA consolidation. **Charitable gaming under county Public Local Laws is not within the MLGCA list** because charitable gaming is administered county-by-county. **Pari-mutuel racing wagers placed at the tracks fall outside the VEP**, though MLGCA-licensed casino sportsbooks at racinos (Ocean Downs) are covered.⁶

**Removal process.** Two-year enrollees are removed automatically at the end of the term. Lifetime enrollees may not be removed under current MLGCA policy. There is no formal petition-and-clinician-affidavit pathway comparable to Illinois.

**Penalties for breach.** A VEP enrollee who enters a Maryland casino, places a sports/fantasy wager, or attempts to claim lottery winnings forfeits all winnings to the State (with the forfeiture deposited to the Problem Gambling Fund); criminal trespass charges may be filed; player-club memberships and complimentary services are revoked. Operators that knowingly accept wagers from enrollees face MLGCA administrative discipline.

**Multi-state participation.** Maryland is not a member of MSIGA (no online casino). The VEP list is not formally reciprocal with other states, though national operators apply enrollments at the account level under internal compliance. There are no tribal exclusion lists in Maryland because no IGRA tribal casinos exist.

**State of the program in 2026.** Maryland is a national leader in problem-gambling infrastructure on the research and clinical side, anchored by the **Maryland Center of Excellence on Problem Gambling** at the University of Maryland School of Medicine, established by the 2008 VLT legislation. Funding flows through the **Problem Gambling Fund** (Md. Code, State Government §9-1A-33), capitalized by a percentage of casino gross gaming revenue. A persistent concern — flagged publicly by Center of Excellence director Mary Drexler in 2023 — is that **mobile sports wagering revenue under the 2020/2021 authorization does not flow to the Problem Gambling Fund**, leaving the Fund reliant on declining casino table-game and slot-tax revenue even as mobile sports betting expands gambling participation; reform proposals to redirect a share of sports-betting tax revenue have been considered in successive sessions. The November 2022 launch of mobile sports wagering was associated with a documented uptick in VEP enrollments. The state helpline is **1-800-GAMBLER** with state-specific routing through the Maryland Center of Excellence.
