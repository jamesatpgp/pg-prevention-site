# South Carolina — Gambling History

## The present

South Carolina in 2026 is among the most restrictive U.S. gambling jurisdictions. Legal forms are: the South Carolina Education Lottery (since 2002); charitable bingo and (since 2014–2015) charitable raffles; pari-mutuel-style off-shore "cruises to nowhere" departing from a couple of coastal ports, surviving in restricted form despite the 2005 Day Cruise Gambling Act; and the steeplechase races at Camden and Aiken, which operate without pari-mutuel wagering. There are **no commercial casinos, no tribal casinos in South Carolina** (the Catawba Indian Nation games at its Kings Mountain, North Carolina facility under a uniquely restrictive 1993 federal settlement), no pari-mutuel horse or greyhound racing, no commercial sports betting, no mobile sports betting, no iGaming, no licensed daily fantasy sports, and no licensed casino-style gaming machines. The constitutional baseline at S.C. Const. art. XVII, § 7 (the 1895 prohibition, narrowly amended in 2000 for the Lottery and in 2014 for raffles) and S.C. Code § 12-21-2710 continue to bar most gambling. Regulation runs through a thin set of agencies: the South Carolina Education Lottery Commission, the South Carolina Department of Revenue (bingo), the State Law Enforcement Division (SLED, enforcement), and the South Carolina Department of Alcohol and Other Drug Abuse Services (DAODAS, treatment).¹ ²

## How it got here

Colonial Carolina's 1712 "to prevent immorality" act criminalized excessive gaming and Sunday gambling, but the General Assembly authorized lotteries to fund the College of Charleston (1785) and infrastructure. Lowcountry horse racing — the South Carolina Jockey Club (founded 1758) and the Washington Race Course in Charleston — was the era's elite social institution; Hampton Park in Charleston preserves remnants of the Washington Race Course infield. The 1895 Constitution at Article XVII, § 7 declared "No lottery shall ever be allowed or be advertised by newspapers, or otherwise, or its tickets be sold in this State" — uniquely also criminalizing advertising.³

Through the 20th century S.C. Code Title 16 ch. 19 and § 12-21-2710 enforced the ban; charitable bingo was authorized in the mid-20th century with periodic recodifications.

**The video poker era (1986–2000) — "the wild west of gambling."** Beginning in the mid-1980s, an interpretive opening in S.C. Code § 12-21-2710 allowed early video gaming machines to spread. Operators developed workarounds (paying out via "free game" credits redeemed off-premises) that effectively created cash gambling. The 1991 General Assembly affirmatively legalized cash payouts under licensing (Act 248), formalizing the gray market. By 1999 the state had an estimated **34,000+ video poker machines** producing roughly **$2.5 billion in annual gross wagering**, with no statewide cap, no centralized monitoring, no self-exclusion program, and minimal player protections. The Grand Strand, Charleston suburbs, the Pee Dee, and the I-95 corridor were saturated.⁴ Governor Jim Hodges and the General Assembly enacted Act 125 of 1999 banning video poker effective **July 1, 2000**, after *Joytime Distributors and Amusement Co. v. State*, 338 S.C. 634 (1999), upheld the General Assembly's authority. Machines went dark statewide on that date — one of the most consequential rapid-rise-and-fall episodes in U.S. gambling history.

**The Education Lottery (2002).** Voters approved a constitutional amendment November 7, 2000; the General Assembly enacted Act 59 of 2001 (S.C. Code Title 59 ch. 150); first ticket sold January 7, 2002. Net proceeds fund LIFE, HOPE, and Palmetto Fellows scholarships and K-12 technology grants. Cumulative transfers exceeded $7 billion by FY2024.⁵

**Cruises-to-nowhere.** Beginning in the 1990s, vessels departed Little River (north of Myrtle Beach) and Charleston Harbor for international waters, conducted casino-style gambling, and returned the same day. The federal Johnson Act amendments and the **South Carolina Day Cruise Gambling Act of 2005** (S.C. Code § 3-11-100 et seq.) substantially restricted the practice; the Big "M" Casino in Little River continues in some form as of 2026.

**Catawba Indian Nation.** The 1993 federal settlement (Catawba Indian Tribe of South Carolina Land Claims Settlement Act, Pub. L. No. 103-116, 25 U.S.C. § 941 et seq.) uniquely barred the Catawba from Class III gaming **in South Carolina specifically** — driving the tribe to seek and obtain land into trust in North Carolina, where Catawba Two Kings Casino opened in 2021.⁶

**Sports betting and iGaming.** Not authorized. Multiple bills have been introduced (HB 5277 of 2022, HB 3625 of 2023, others through 2025); none has cleared. DFS operates under a non-affirmative posture rather than affirmative licensing; major DFS operators have varied in offering services in South Carolina based on legal-risk assessments.

## Regulatory framework today

- **South Carolina Education Lottery Commission** — Lottery games and "Better U" responsible-gambling outreach. https://www.sceducationlottery.com/
- **South Carolina Department of Revenue, Bingo Tax** — charitable bingo licensing under S.C. Code Title 12 ch. 21 Article 24. https://dor.sc.gov/
- **South Carolina Secretary of State** — charitable raffle registration under S.C. Code §§ 33-57-100 et seq.
- **South Carolina Law Enforcement Division (SLED)** — gambling-device enforcement under § 12-21-2710.
- **South Carolina Department of Alcohol and Other Drug Abuse Services (DAODAS)** — gambling-addiction treatment funded by Lottery dedication. https://www.daodas.sc.gov/

## Voluntary self-exclusion

South Carolina has **no statewide voluntary self-exclusion program** — primarily because there is no statewide commercial casino, sportsbook, or iGaming operator to self-exclude from. This is the most restrictive corner of the U.S. self-exclusion landscape.⁷

**What exists in practice:**

- **South Carolina Education Lottery.** The Lottery does **not** maintain a statewide self-exclusion list because point-of-sale lottery retailers do not check ID against a list. The Lottery's responsible-gambling outreach (https://www.sceducationlottery.com/PlayResponsibly/About and the "Better U" program) emphasizes self-management — deposit limits, time tracking, and helpline referral — rather than formal self-exclusion.
- **iLottery account.** The Lottery's online subscription products allow account-level self-exclusion at the individual account holder's request.
- **Catawba Two Kings Casino.** A South Carolinian seeking to be excluded from gambling at the only Catawba casino must enroll in the **North Carolina** State Lottery Commission's Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (for sports wagering — operates separately) or with the **Catawba Nation Gaming Commission's** internal exclusion list for casino floor purposes. Both are out-of-state mechanisms from South Carolina's perspective.
- **Cruise operators.** The two surviving day-cruise gambling operators may maintain internal exclusion lists; there is no state requirement.
- **Charitable bingo and raffles.** No mechanism.

**Duration, scope, removal, multi-state.** Not applicable — no statewide program exists. Any of the above operator-specific tools follow that operator's internal rules.

**Help.** **South Carolina Gambling Helpline 1-877-452-5155**, a lottery-funded, toll-free, confidential referral service managed by DAODAS. The national **1-800-GAMBLER** helpline also serves South Carolinians. DAODAS funds gambling treatment through a network of 46 county offices; since 2002 a total of $2.55 million in Lottery funds has been appropriated to DAODAS for problem gambling treatment.⁸

**Where the gap matters.** A South Carolinian with a problem gambling disorder who is using sweepstakes "casino" sites, illegal online sportsbooks, social casinos, daily fantasy contests, North Carolina mobile sports betting (geofenced out), or out-of-state casino travel has no state-level tool to enroll in a binding self-exclusion. The advocacy community — including DAODAS, NCPG, and the national 1-800-GAMBLER call center — recommends **commercial blocking software (Gamban, BetBlocker)** and operator-by-operator account exclusion for South Carolina residents. NCPG State of the States surveys consistently identify South Carolina as among the lowest-funded states for problem-gambling services on both total and per-capita measures, and the absence of regulated gambling has limited political-economic pressure for a unified self-exclusion architecture.⁹

## Problem gambling response

DAODAS administers gambling-disorder treatment funded by Lottery dedication and operates the **1-877-452-5155** helpline. The South Carolina Council on Problem Gambling has had periods of dormancy per NCPG surveys; the NCPG state-affiliate role has not always been continuously filled. Treatment is delivered through DAODAS' network of 46 county offices alongside substance-use services.
