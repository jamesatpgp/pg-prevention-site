# U.S. Virgin Islands — Gambling History

## Current landscape

The U.S. Virgin Islands today legally permits the **Virgin Islands Lottery** (territory-wide), **commercial casinos on St. Croix only**, **horse racing** under racino-style enterprise zones, and **charitable bingo and raffles**. There is no comprehensive mobile sports-wagering market and no casinos on St. Thomas or St. John. Two casinos currently operate on St. Croix: **Divi Carina Bay Resort & Casino** (east end) and **Caravelle Casino & Hotel** (Christiansted). The principal regulators are the **Virgin Islands Casino Control Commission (VICCC)** and the **Virgin Islands Lottery**, both autonomous entities of the territorial government.¹

## Compressed history

The Danish West Indies — St. Thomas, St. John, and St. Croix — passed from Denmark to the United States by treaty in 1917 and were organized as an unincorporated territory. Through the Danish colonial period the islands had a maritime/sugar-plantation economy with no chartered lottery or licensed casino; horse racing existed informally and became culturally important, with carnival-season racing dating from the early twentieth century. Cockfighting was an established cultural practice (later preempted by the 2018 Farm Bill amendments to the federal Animal Welfare Act).

The **Virgin Islands Lottery** is one of the oldest U.S. lottery operations, established approximately ninety years ago in the mid-1930s, per VI Legislature budget testimony.² It is an autonomous government entity receiving no federal or central-government appropriations; it returns roughly 80% of proceeds to the territory, supporting education, healthcare, GERS (Government Employees Retirement System), and veterans services.

The **Casino and Resort Control Act of 1995** (32 V.I.C. §§ 401 et seq.) legalized casino gaming on **St. Croix only** — a deliberate policy choice channeling casino-driven development to the largest island while protecting the tourism brand of St. Thomas and St. John. The VICCC was created as the licensing and enforcement body.³ Horse racing was modernized under the **Virgin Islands Horse Racing Industry Assistance Acts of 2011 and 2016**, permitting racing-zone enterprise operations in Christiansted and Frederiksted.

Hurricanes Irma and Maria (September 2017) closed **Clinton E. Phipps Racetrack** on St. Thomas for seven years; racing resumed May 3, 2024. In April 2024, the VICCC issued an initial racino license to **VIGL Operations, LLC** for the Randall "Doc" James Racetrack on St. Croix; that license expired April 19, 2025, when construction did not proceed. Per VICCC's July 14, 2025 testimony, the two operating casinos reported FY2025 gross taxable revenues of $11.1 million through April 2025, with casino revenue taxes down to $1.3 million (from $2.2M in FY2024).⁴ COVID-era legislation permitted operation of cruise-ship casinos while docked in St. Croix.

## Regulatory framework today

The **Virgin Islands Casino Control Commission** — https://casinocontrolcommission.vi — licenses casinos and racinos and enforces responsible-gaming requirements under the 1995 Act. The **Virgin Islands Lottery**, listed under the **Department of Property and Procurement's** government-agencies directory at https://dpp.vi.gov/government-agencies/vi-lottery/, operates traditional drawings and instant games. The **Division of Gaming Enforcement** within the V.I. Department of Justice — https://usvidoj.com/service/division-of-gaming-enforcement/ — supports criminal enforcement. The **V.I. Department of Health** — https://doh.vi.gov — partners with VICCC on problem-gambling programming.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

**Administering agency:** The **Virgin Islands Casino Control Commission** operates the territory's only formal self-exclusion program, the **Voluntary Self-Exclusion (VSE) Program** for licensed casinos.⁵ The program is publicly fronted through the **PlayWiseVI** responsible-gaming portal (https://playwisevi.org/voluntary-exclusion/) and is limited in scope to **St. Croix's licensed casinos** because no other commercial gaming is licensed in the territory. The VI Lottery does not administer a separate exclusion list.

**Where to enroll:** Submission must be made **in person at the Virgin Islands Casino Control Commission**, located at **3005 Orange Grove, Christiansted, St. Croix, VI 00820**. There is no online enrollment option. The PlayWiseVI portal provides the Voluntary Exclusion Request form for review before the in-person visit.

**Duration options:** Three fixed terms — **1, 2, or 3 years**. There is no lifetime option, distinguishing the USVI program from most state programs.

**Scope:** Exclusion covers all licensed casinos in the U.S. Virgin Islands (currently Divi Carina Bay and Caravelle). Self-excluded persons are barred from entering casino floors, holding slot or players-club membership, receiving casino credit, cashing checks at a casino, receiving complimentary goods or services, and collecting any winnings or recovering any losses. Signing the Voluntary Exclusion Request includes acknowledgment that the participant "is a problem gambler" — a stronger declarative element than most U.S. programs use.

**Removal process:** The participant remains on the list for the elected term (1, 2, or 3 years) and is not eligible for early removal during the term. Reinstatement is not automatic at term end; the participant must affirmatively contact the VICCC.

**Penalties:** Winnings are forfeited; licensees that fail to enforce the list face VICCC disciplinary action.

**2026 program state:** Active. The VICCC and the **V.I. Department of Health** executed a Memorandum of Understanding on **October 24, 2024**, under which VICCC funds training and certification of six counselors — three on St. Thomas-St. John and three on St. Croix — to treat problem-gambling disorders.⁶ Training is delivered through PlayWiseVI's clinical partners.
