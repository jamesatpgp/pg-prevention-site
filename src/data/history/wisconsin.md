# Wisconsin — Gambling History

## Present-day posture

Wisconsin in 2026 is exclusively a tribal-gaming state for casino-style products. All 11 federally recognized Wisconsin tribes operate Class III gaming under compacts negotiated with the state; there are no non-tribal commercial casinos, no commercial card rooms, and no commercial pari-mutuel racing (greyhound racing ended December 31, 2009 at Dairyland Greyhound Park in Kenosha). The Wisconsin Lottery, operated by the Department of Revenue, runs draw and instant games but is constitutionally barred from casino-style products. Sports wagering is permitted only on tribal lands via amended Class III compacts; the Oneida Nation became the first Wisconsin tribe to negotiate such an amendment with Governor Tony Evers on July 1, 2021, and most other tribes have followed. There is no statewide mobile sports betting, no online casino, and no iLottery. Charitable bingo, raffles, and pull-tabs are licensed under Wis. Stat. ch. 563.¹

## Compressed history

Wisconsin was admitted as the 30th state on May 29, 1848. The 1848 constitution included Art. IV § 24 prohibiting lotteries — language amended five separate times in the 20th century to expand permitted forms of gambling. Wisconsin maintained Progressive-era anti-gambling statutes through the first half of the 20th century under the La Follette political tradition.² Charitable bingo was authorized by constitutional amendment in 1973 and implemented under Wis. Stat. ch. 563; charitable raffles followed in 1977; the lottery in April 1987 (first ticket sales September 1988); pari-mutuel wagering in November 1987. The pari-mutuel amendment uniquely authorized wagering on horse, dog, and **snowmobile** races, though no snowmobile or auto-pari-mutuel wagering ever developed. Five greyhound permits issued in 1989 produced tracks at Delavan (Geneva Lakes), Kaukauna (Fox Valley), Hudson (St. Croix Meadows), Lake Delton (Wisconsin Dells), and Kenosha (Dairyland). The five tracks drew approximately 3.5 million visitors in 1991, but the rapid expansion of tribal Class III casino gaming through the 1991-1992 compact wave drained the greyhound market; tracks closed sequentially through the 1990s and 2000s, with Dairyland the last on December 31, 2009.³

The 1993 constitutional amendment explicitly limited authorized state-run gambling to bingo, raffles, pari-mutuel on-track betting, and a state-run lottery, and expressly prohibited the state-run lottery from conducting casino-style games. This left tribal compacts as the only path to casino expansion. The 2006 *Panzer v. Doyle* litigation considered whether the 1993 amendment invalidated existing compacts; the Wisconsin Supreme Court held it did not, preserving the distinctive Wisconsin structure: state-run gambling is constitutionally limited to non-casino forms; full casino-style gambling is available only through tribal-compact authorization.⁴ Lottery proceeds uniquely fund the Wisconsin Lottery and Gaming Credit, which provides direct property-tax relief to homeowners — an unusual structural choice among U.S. lotteries. The Forest County Potawatomi's Potawatomi Hotel & Casino in downtown Milwaukee is the single largest Wisconsin gaming property by revenue; the Ho-Chunk Nation operates the state's largest portfolio (six facilities).

## Regulatory framework today

The **Wisconsin Department of Administration, Division of Gaming / Office of Indian Gaming and Regulatory Compliance (OIGRC)** (https://doa.wi.gov/Pages/AboutDOA/Office-of-Indian-Gaming-and-Regulatory-Compliance.aspx) administers tribal-state compact compliance, charitable gaming licensing, and (formerly) pari-mutuel regulation. OIGRC concurrent-regulates Class III casino gaming alongside the 11 tribal gaming commissions. The **Wisconsin Lottery** (https://wilottery.com/), within the Department of Revenue under Wis. Stat. ch. 565, operates lottery games. The **Wisconsin Department of Health Services** funds problem-gambling services in partnership with the **Wisconsin Council on Problem Gambling (WCPG)**, founded in 1980 and one of the longest-operating state PG councils in the country.⁵

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Wisconsin's self-exclusion situation is **distinctively decentralized**. There are no self-exclusion program requirements outlined in Wisconsin's tribal-state compacts or tribal gaming ordinances, and the state does not administer a unified statewide list because the casino market is exclusively tribal. Each of the 11 federally recognized Wisconsin tribes operates its own tribal-gaming-commission-administered VSE program for its property or properties; enrollment, durations, scope, and removal rules vary by tribe.⁶

For example:
- **Forest County Potawatomi's Potawatomi Casino Hotel** (Milwaukee) — enrollment by contacting the property at 414-847-7608; duration options and scope set by the Potawatomi Gaming Commission. In **March 2025**, the Forest County Potawatomi Community became the **first U.S. tribal operator to join a new national tribal-wide self-exclusion program** — an industry-led initiative permitting cross-tribal honoring of a single self-exclusion across participating tribal casinos, supported by national problem-gambling-prevention leaders.⁷
- **Ho-Chunk Nation** (six properties: Black River Falls, Madison, Wisconsin Dells, Wittenberg, Nekoosa, Tomah) — enrollment at any Ho-Chunk property; the Ho-Chunk Gaming Commission operates a unified list across all six locations under tribal law.
- **Oneida Nation Casino Hotel** (Green Bay) — enrollment through the Oneida Gaming Commission; covers retail sportsbook at the property and Oneida-affiliated mobile wagering geo-fenced to Oneida lands.
- **St. Croix, Lac du Flambeau, Lac Courte Oreilles, Bad River, Red Cliff, Menominee, Stockbridge-Munsee, Sokaogon, Mole Lake** — each operates its own enrollment process through its tribal gaming commission.

Because tribal Class III gaming is the only casino market and tribal exclusion lists are not legally required to be honored across tribes, a Wisconsin resident seeking statewide self-exclusion has historically had to enroll separately at every property they might visit. The March 2025 tribal-wide program is the most significant structural development in Wisconsin VSE in years; if other Wisconsin tribes join, it could produce de facto statewide coverage on an opt-in basis comparable to (and potentially more comprehensive than) Washington's WSGC cross-tribal opt-in model.

**Sports wagering self-exclusion** is conducted through each tribal gaming commission for the wagering offered at that tribe's property — there is no statewide mobile platform to exclude from.

**Charitable bingo and raffles** are not covered by any statewide self-exclusion list; exclusion at charitable bingo halls is handled at the operator level under WCPG-recommended responsible-gaming practices.

**The Wisconsin Lottery** has no consumer-side VSE program for retail lottery products (operationally impossible across 3,000+ retailers); the Lottery includes responsible-gaming messaging on tickets and at retail point-of-sale under Wis. Stat. ch. 565.⁸

Wisconsin's principal problem-gambling support resource is the **24/7 Wisconsin Problem Gambling Help Line** (1-800-426-2535), operated by WCPG, which provides counselor referral and assistance with locating the relevant tribal gaming commission VSE process. WCPG funding is drawn from a combination of lottery transfers, tribal compact responsible-gaming contributions, and Department of Health Services appropriations. Wisconsin does not participate in any state-administered multi-state self-exclusion compact.⁹
