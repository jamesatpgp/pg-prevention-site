# Pennsylvania — Gambling History

## The present

Pennsylvania in 2026 has one of the deepest and broadest regulated gambling markets in the United States — and arguably **the most comprehensive single-state self-exclusion architecture in the country**. Legal forms include: the Pennsylvania Lottery, the country's only state lottery whose net proceeds are dedicated entirely to programs for older adults; the second-largest U.S. iGaming market by revenue (behind New Jersey); one of the largest sports-betting markets by handle; 16 land-based casinos (Categories 1, 2, 3, and 4 mini-casinos); video gaming terminals (VGTs) at qualifying truck stops; daily fantasy sports; pari-mutuel wagering on Thoroughbred and Standardbred horse racing; charitable bingo, small games of chance, and raffles; and airport tablet gaming at select facilities. The **Pennsylvania Gaming Control Board (PGCB)** is the dominant regulator, complemented by the State Horse Racing Commission, the Pennsylvania Lottery (Department of Revenue), and county-level charitable gaming authorities.¹ ²

## How it got here

Pennsylvania's gambling history spans the most extreme positions a U.S. state can hold. William Penn's "Frame of Government" (1682) criminalized cards, dice, and lotteries — yet colonial Pennsylvania ran lotteries to fund Philadelphia's defenses, Christ Church's steeple, and Pennsylvania Hospital (1751), with Benjamin Franklin as the chief promoter.³ The Commonwealth banned lotteries by statute in 1833, and the 1873 Constitution at Article III, § 17 reinforced a flat prohibition that survived for **138 years until the 1971 amendment**.

The **Pennsylvania Lottery** opened March 7, 1972 under the State Lottery Law (Act of August 26, 1971, P.L. 351), with **net proceeds dedicated entirely to programs for older Pennsylvanians** — PACE/PACENET prescription assistance, free or reduced-fare transit, property-tax/rent rebates, and county Area Agency on Aging services. Cumulative lottery transfers to older-adult programs exceeded **$50 billion by FY2025** — among the most consequential single-purpose state lotteries in U.S. history.⁴ Pari-mutuel horse racing was authorized in 1959 (Act of December 11, 1959, P.L. 1786; 4 Pa.C.S. ch. 3). The Race Horse Industry Reform Act of 1981 consolidated harness and Thoroughbred regulation.

The transformative moment was the **Race Horse Development and Gaming Act (Act 71 of 2004)**, signed by Governor Ed Rendell July 5, 2004. Act 71 created the PGCB and authorized 14 gaming licenses across three categories (racinos, standalone slot casinos, resort casinos). Mohegan Sun at Pocono Downs opened the first slot-only casino November 14, 2006. **Table games** were added by Act 1 of 2010. **Act 42 of 2017** (October 30, 2017) — the broadest single gaming-expansion law in U.S. history at the time — authorized sports wagering, iGaming, daily fantasy sports, Category 4 satellite "mini-casinos," and truck-stop VGTs.⁵

After *Murphy v. NCAA*, 138 S. Ct. 1461 (2018), Pennsylvania was second only to Delaware among non-Nevada states to launch sports betting (Hollywood at Penn National, November 17, 2018; mobile launch May 28, 2019). **iGaming launched July 15, 2019.** Pennsylvania became the second-largest U.S. iGaming market and one of the largest sports-betting markets by handle. The sports-betting tax rate is among the highest in the country — 34% state plus 2% local share, total 36% of gross gaming revenue (4 Pa.C.S. § 13C62).

## Regulatory framework today

- **Pennsylvania Gaming Control Board (PGCB)** — casinos (all four categories), iGaming, sports wagering, daily fantasy sports, video gaming terminals at truck stops, airport tablet gaming, and the **multi-vertical Self-Exclusion programs**. https://gamingcontrolboard.pa.gov/ and https://responsibleplay.pa.gov/
- **Pennsylvania Lottery (Department of Revenue)** — Lottery games, iLottery interactive instants and Keno, online Powerball/Mega Millions, and Lottery responsible-play programs. https://www.palottery.state.pa.us/
- **State Horse Racing Commission** — Thoroughbred and Standardbred racing, simulcast, ADW. https://www.shrc.pa.gov/
- **Department of Drug and Alcohol Programs (DDAP)** — Compulsive and Problem Gambling Treatment Fund administration; statewide helpline 1-800-GAMBLER through CCGP. https://www.ddap.pa.gov/
- **Council on Compulsive Gambling of Pennsylvania (CCGP)** — NCPG state affiliate. https://www.pacouncil.com/
- **County treasurers** — local Bingo Law and Small Games of Chance Act licensing; **PA State Police, Bureau of Liquor Control Enforcement** — investigatory authority.

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Pennsylvania operates **the most comprehensive set of voluntary self-exclusion programs in the United States**, codified at 58 Pa. Code Chapter 503a and administered by the PGCB through the unified ResponsiblePlay.pa.gov portal. The program structure is unusual in that **enrollees can choose separate or combined enrollment across four distinct verticals**, each with its own enrollment record, term, and removal process.⁶ ⁷

**Administering agency.** Pennsylvania Gaming Control Board (PGCB) — Office of Compulsive and Problem Gambling.

**Where to enroll.** Two pathways:

1. **Online** via the PGCB Self-Exclusion Portal at https://responsibleplay.pa.gov/self-exclusion/. Identity verification by knowledge-based questions or document upload.
2. **In person** at any of the 16 PA casinos, at PGCB headquarters in Harrisburg, or at any PGCB regional office.

**The four vertical programs** (a participant may enroll in one, several, or all four — coverage extends only to verticals selected):

1. **Casino Self-Exclusion** — bars the participant from the gaming floor and gaming-related areas of all 16 licensed casinos (Categories 1-4). **Casino self-exclusions for one year or five years remain in effect until the self-excluded person affirmatively requests removal** — meaning a 1-year or 5-year Casino term effectively runs as a "minimum 1 year, then continuing" or "minimum 5 years, then continuing" until written removal request is made and granted by PGCB. A **lifetime** term is permanent and no removal is possible.
2. **iGaming Self-Exclusion** — covers all licensed online casino sites (online slots, table games, online poker) operated under PGCB iGaming certificates. iGaming terms run **1 year, 5 years, or lifetime**, and the 1- and 5-year iGaming exclusions **automatically expire** at the end of the chosen term (in contrast to Casino).
3. **Video Gaming Terminals (VGT) Self-Exclusion** — covers VGT terminals at qualifying truck stops licensed under 4 Pa.C.S. ch. 39. Terms **1, 5, or lifetime**; 1- and 5-year terms expire automatically.
4. **Fantasy Contests Self-Exclusion** — covers daily fantasy contests licensed under 4 Pa.C.S. ch. 3B. Terms **1, 5, or lifetime**; 1- and 5-year terms expire automatically.

A single ResponsiblePlay.pa.gov account allows simultaneous enrollment across all four verticals. **Sports wagering** is integrated into the **Casino** vertical because sports wagering is operated under casino-tied licenses; online sports wagering accounts are closed automatically upon Casino self-exclusion enrollment that includes the online component.

**Scope summary.** A participant who enrolls across all four verticals at the lifetime level is barred from: the gaming floors of all 16 PA casinos (and any future casino), all PGCB-licensed online casino sites, all PGCB-licensed online sportsbooks, all PGCB-licensed retail sportsbooks, all licensed VGTs at PA truck stops, all PGCB-licensed daily fantasy sites, and all complimentaries/credit at any of these. **Not covered**: the Pennsylvania Lottery's retail games (point-of-sale lottery purchases cannot be reliably blocked) — though the Lottery maintains its own separate iLottery account self-exclusion process. Charitable bingo and small games of chance are also outside the PGCB list.

**Removal.** Casino: affirmative written request required for 1- and 5-year terms; lifetime is permanent. iGaming, VGT, Fantasy: automatic removal at end of 1- or 5-year term; lifetime is permanent.

**Penalties for breach.** Self-excluded persons "are prohibited from collecting any winnings, recovering any losses or accepting complimentary gifts or services or any other thing of value from a licensee or operator." Winnings are confiscated; the person may be removed and trespassed. Operators face PGCB enforcement, fines, and license action for failing to detect and bar enrollees.

**Multi-state.** PGCB does not formally compact with other states. Major operators (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars) commonly apply cross-state corporate exclusion when a customer self-excludes in PA — but that is operator policy, not a PA state guarantee.

## Problem gambling response

Pennsylvania's problem-gambling response is funded through statutory dedications:

- **Compulsive and Problem Gambling Treatment Fund** (4 Pa.C.S. §§ 1408 and 1509) — funded by a per-slot-machine daily assessment (initially $1.50/machine/day, subsequently increased), plus dedicated allocations from casino license fees and from sports-wagering and iGaming taxes (§§ 13C62 and 13B62). The Fund flows to DDAP.
- **Department of Drug and Alcohol Programs (DDAP)** administers treatment, prevention, and helpline funding.
- **Council on Compulsive Gambling of Pennsylvania (CCGP)** is the NCPG state affiliate, operates **1-800-GAMBLER** (the helpline number Pennsylvania adopted in 1990 and which is now used nationally), and provides outreach, peer support, and training.⁸

The Pennsylvania Lottery's own responsible-play program runs in parallel. The State Horse Racing Commission requires responsible-gambling signage and helpline information at all tracks. Pennsylvania ranks highly in NCPG State of the States surveys for absolute dedicated funding.
