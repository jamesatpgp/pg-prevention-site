# Indiana — Gambling History

Indiana's legal gambling in 2026 includes ten land-based commercial casinos (the former riverboats, now permanently moored or relocated inland), two racinos (Hoosier Park, Horseshoe Indianapolis/Indiana Grand), the Four Winds Casino South Bend operated by the Pokagon Band of Potawatomi under a 2021 Class III compact, French Lick Resort (a statutorily authorized inland casino), the Hoosier Lottery, pari-mutuel horse racing, retail and mobile sports wagering since 2019, and charitable gaming. The Indiana Gaming Commission (IGC) regulates casinos, racinos, sports wagering, and charitable gaming; the Indiana Horse Racing Commission regulates the tracks; the State Lottery Commission of Indiana operates the Hoosier Lottery. iGaming (online casino) has been proposed in multiple sessions but has not passed.¹

Indiana entered the Union in 1816 with a constitutional ban on lotteries, preserved verbatim in Article 15 §8 of the 1851 constitution — language that constrained Indiana gambling policy until voters amended it in 1988. Through the 19th century, gambling existed in saloons, fairgrounds, and back-room games; in the 20th, illegal slot machines, policy houses, and bookmakers operated in factory towns (Gary, Hammond, East Chicago) tied to mid-century organized-crime networks documented in the Kefauver-era investigations. The 1988 amendment opened the door, and the Hoosier Lottery began scratch sales in October 1989 (joining Powerball in 1990 and Mega Millions in 2009), with a private-management contract with IGT since 2013. Pari-mutuel betting was also legalized in 1989; Hoosier Park opened in Anderson in 1994 and Indiana Grand in Shelbyville in 2002.²

Riverboat casinos followed via the Indiana Riverboat Gambling Act (July 1, 1993), a budget-special-session compromise that authorized ten licenses — five on Lake Michigan, five on the Ohio River, and one at Patoka Lake that never materialized. Casino Aztar in Evansville opened first, in December 1995. A statutory exception authorized a single inland casino at French Lick, which opened in 2006. 2007 legislation authorized slots at Hoosier Park and Indiana Grand, creating racinos; 2015 legislation added live table games at the racinos starting in 2021. The Pokagon Band opened Four Winds South Bend on Jan. 16, 2018, initially limited to Class II gaming; after requesting Class III negotiations in 2019, the Tribe's compact with Indiana was ratified in 2021 by Gov. Eric Holcomb, authorizing full Class III gaming and tribal sports wagering. 2019's House Enrolled Act 1015 (signed May 8, 2019) authorized commercial sports wagering — retail launched Sept. 1, 2019, and mobile Oct. 3, 2019, with mobile registration permitted remotely from day one. The same act permitted some riverboat licenses to convert to inland locations and authorized a Vigo County casino; Hard Rock Casino Northern Indiana opened in Gary in 2021 (consolidating two former Gary boat licenses), and Hard Rock Terre Haute followed under the relocated Vigo County authorization.³

## Regulatory framework today

The Indiana Gaming Commission (https://www.in.gov/igc/) regulates riverboats, inland casinos, racinos, the Four Winds tribal compact, sports wagering, and charitable gaming under Indiana Code Title 4, Articles 33, 35, and 38 and IC 4-32.3. The Indiana Horse Racing Commission (https://www.in.gov/hrc/) regulates pari-mutuel racing under IC 4-31. The State Lottery Commission of Indiana / Hoosier Lottery (https://hoosierlottery.com/) operates the lottery under IC 4-30. Problem-gambling services are administered through the Indiana Family and Social Services Administration, Division of Mental Health and Addiction (DMHA), via the Addiction Services Fund, with prevention/research housed at Indiana University's Indiana Problem Gambling Awareness Program (IPGAP).

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

The Indiana Gaming Commission administers the **Voluntary Exclusion Program (VEP)** under 68 IAC, in effect since July 1, 2004 and amended repeatedly to broaden coverage. The VEP is one of the more accessible state programs because Indiana permits both online and in-person enrollment.

**Enrollment.** A person may enroll at any Indiana casino (signed in the presence of a gaming agent), at the IGC office in Indianapolis, with a designated problem-gambling treatment provider, or online at https://secure.in.gov/apps/igc/selfexclusion/vep/enrollment after passing an identity-verification check. The participant must complete the form of their own free will and not be under the influence of alcohol or controlled substances. The list of excluded persons is confidential and shared only with licensees for enforcement.⁴

**Duration.** One year, five years, or lifetime. Selecting "lifetime" is irrevocable: lifetime enrollees cannot apply for removal.

**Scope.** The VEP covers all Indiana commercial casinos and racinos, the Four Winds South Bend tribal casino (under the 2021 compact's responsible-gaming provisions), all retail sports wagering at Indiana licensees, and — under expansion enacted with the 2019 sports-wagering authorization — all mobile sportsbook certificate holders (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars, ESPN BET, Fanatics, and the casino-tethered books). The IGC also operates a separate **Internet Self-Restriction Program** (https://www.in.gov/igc/internet-self-restriction-program/) for online sports wagering and related accounts, allowing a faster online-only restriction.

The Hoosier Lottery operates its own retailer-side responsible-gambling program but is not covered by the IGC VEP; pari-mutuel racing wagers placed live at tracks are covered through the racino licenses. Charitable bingo is not within the VEP.

**Removal process.** One-year and five-year enrollees are automatically removed at the end of the term unless they re-enroll. Lifetime enrollees may not seek removal. Indiana's lifetime irrevocability is among the strictest in the country and is one reason clinicians often counsel one-year or five-year enrollment first.

**Penalties for breach.** A VEP enrollee who enters a casino, places a sports wager, or attempts to collect winnings forfeits all winnings to the State; the IGC may pursue criminal trespass charges under IC 35-43-2-2 and disciplinary action against licensees who knowingly accept wagers. The Indiana Council on Problem Gambling helpline (1-800-994-8448) is required signage at all licensees.

**Multi-state participation.** Indiana is not a member of MSIGA (no online casino is authorized). The IGC has no formal reciprocity statute with neighboring state lists. The Pokagon Band's tribal gaming commission coordinates with the IGC on enforcement under the 2021 compact but maintains its own tribal responsible-gaming oversight at properties in Michigan as well as Indiana.

**State of the program in 2026.** The Indiana VEP is comparatively mature and well-funded through the Addiction Services Fund, and Indiana was an early mover on online enrollment (allowed for sports-wagering exclusion since 2019). The dual-track design — VEP for casinos plus the Internet Self-Restriction Program for online sports — has been studied as a model that reduces friction without sacrificing identity verification. Indiana University's IPGAP (https://indianaproblemgambling.org/) is one of the longer-running academic-housed problem-gambling programs in the country and produces prevention curricula, clinician training, and prevalence research that informs IGC rulemaking. The IGC's VEP page is https://www.in.gov/igc/voluntary-exclusion-program/.⁵
