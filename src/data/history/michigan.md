# Michigan — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

Michigan operates one of the most complete and lucrative gaming markets in the United States: three commercial casinos in Detroit (MGM Grand Detroit, MotorCity, and Hollywood Casino at Greektown), 23 tribal casinos run by twelve federally recognized tribes, a state lottery that has returned over $30 billion to the School Aid Fund, statewide mobile sports betting, **statewide online casino (iGaming)** that frequently clears $200 million in monthly GGR, and charitable bingo and "millionaire party" games.¹ Live pari-mutuel horse racing in the state effectively ended February 3, 2024 when Northville Downs ran its final card, leaving simulcast and ADW wagering as the remaining racing footprint. Michigan was one of the first jurisdictions to join the multi-state online poker compact in 2022.

## A compressed history

Anishinaabe peoples — the Three Fires Confederacy of Ojibwe, Odawa, and Potawatomi — practiced moccasin games, snow snake, and bowl-and-die wagering long before French and British contact. Upon statehood in 1837 Michigan adopted New York-style lottery and gaming-house prohibitions, and the 1850 Michigan Constitution at Article IV § 33 barred any lottery authorization — a provision that held for 122 years.² Detroit's proximity to Windsor, Ontario (legal pari-mutuel from 1908) and the Purple Gang's grip on Prohibition-era vice produced a robust illegal-gambling shadow economy through the 1930s. Voters approved pari-mutuel racing by constitutional amendment in 1933, and the Racing Act (Public Act 199 of 1933) created the Racing Commissioner.

The lottery prohibition fell in 1972 when voters approved Proposal 1. The McCauley-Traxler-Law-Bowman-McNeely Lottery Act (Public Act 239 of 1972) sold its first ticket on November 13, 1972, with net proceeds dedicated to the School Aid Fund under MCL 432.41. Michigan's iLottery launched in August 2014, an early move that anticipated the broader iGaming launch a decade later.³

Tribal gaming came next. Bay Mills Indian Community opened bingo in 1984, and after IGRA's 1988 passage Michigan signed Class III compacts with seven tribes in August 1993. Soaring Eagle Casino (Saginaw Chippewa) opened December 17, 1993 and remains among the largest tribal properties in the Midwest. Commercial casino legalization followed a 1996 Detroit referendum (Proposal E) and the Michigan Gaming Control and Revenue Act, Public Act 69 of 1997 (MCL 432.201 et seq.), which established the Michigan Gaming Control Board. MGM Grand Detroit opened July 29, 1999, MotorCity December 14, 1999, and Greektown November 10, 2000. A 2004 ballot measure (Proposal 1) further constitutionalized voter approval for any new non-tribal casino, freezing the commercial footprint at three Detroit properties.⁴

The decisive expansion came December 20, 2019 when Governor Gretchen Whitmer signed the Lawful Sports Betting Act (PA 149 of 2019) and the Lawful Internet Gaming Act (PA 152 of 2019). Tribal compacts were amended to allow each compacted tribe to offer statewide mobile sports betting and online casino. Both went live January 22, 2021. Tax rates run 8.4% on retail sports betting GGR and a tiered 20–28% on online casino GGR. Michigan joined the Multi-State Internet Gaming Agreement (MSIGA) in 2022 for shared online poker liquidity with Nevada, New Jersey, Delaware, Pennsylvania, and West Virginia.⁵

Defunct forms: Detroit Race Course (Livonia, 1950–1998), Hazel Park Raceway (1949–2018), Northville Downs (1944–February 2024), Wolverine Raceway (closed 1986), Mt. Pleasant Meadows (closed 2014). Greyhound racing was never legalized in Michigan.

## Regulatory framework today

The Michigan Gaming Control Board (https://www.michigan.gov/mgcb) licenses and regulates the three Detroit commercial casinos, all iGaming and mobile sports betting operators, charitable "millionaire parties," and statewide responsible-gaming initiatives under MCL Chapter 432. The Michigan Lottery (https://www.michiganlottery.com), within the Department of Treasury, runs draw and instant games, iLottery, Club Keno, and the Charitable Gaming Division for bingo and raffles under PA 382 of 1972. Tribal Class III gaming operates under twelve compacts overseen at the federal level by the National Indian Gaming Commission (https://www.nigc.gov) with state coordination through MGCB. Problem-gambling treatment funding flows through the Compulsive Gaming Prevention Fund administered by the Michigan Department of Health and Human Services (https://www.michigan.gov/mdhhs/keep-mi-healthy/mentalhealth/problemgambling).

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Michigan runs **two separate state-administered self-exclusion programs** through MGCB, plus a tribal-side program at each compacted tribal property. The split reflects the bifurcated 1997-era commercial regime and the 2019 iGaming framework.

**1. The Disassociated Persons List (DPL)** covers the three Detroit commercial casinos — MGM Grand Detroit, MotorCity Casino, and Hollywood Casino at Greektown — under MCL 432.225. The DPL is **lifetime only**: enrollees commit to a permanent ban with no shorter option. Until 2020 the list was truly permanent and irrevocable; under amendments effective October 2020, an enrollee who has been on the list at least five years may petition MGCB for removal, and the Board has reinstated more than 500 individuals through the petition process.⁶ Entry by a Disassociated Person onto any Detroit casino floor is criminal trespass under state law, punishable by up to one year imprisonment and/or $1,000 fine, and any winnings are forfeited to the Michigan Compulsive Gaming Prevention Fund. Enrollment is in person at the MGCB offices in Detroit or Lansing.

**2. The Internet Gaming and Sports Betting Responsible Gaming Database** covers all licensed online casino and online/retail sports betting operators (BetMGM, DraftKings, FanDuel, Caesars, etc.) statewide. Enrollment offers either a **1-year or 5-year** term, with automatic removal at term expiration. A person can self-exclude from online casino only, online sports betting only, or both. Enrollment is online through the MGCB portal at https://www.michigan.gov/mgcb/resources/responsible-gaming/self-exclusion-forms.⁷

**Tribal casinos** operate their own property-by-property self-exclusion programs under each tribe's compact; these are not consolidated with the state lists, and an individual seeking exclusion from tribal venues must enroll at each property.

In 2026 MGCB added two important supplemental tools. First, in **April 2026** MGCB partnered with **Gamban** to offer all Michigan residents — DPL/database enrolled or not — free Gamban gambling-blocking software licenses, accessible through michigan.gov/MGCB.⁸ Second, Michigan was one of the four founding NVSEP jurisdictions when the National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program at nvsep.org launched in August 2024 (alongside Colorado, Iowa, and Tennessee), allowing Michigan residents to self-exclude across all participating states with a single enrollment.⁹ For interstate online poker, MSIGA partner-state operators honor Michigan exclusions on shared-liquidity tables.

The combined enrollment across the DPL and the iGaming Database has exceeded 11,000 individuals as of 2024 MGCB reporting [enrollment figures updated periodically].
