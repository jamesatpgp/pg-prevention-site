# Maine — Gambling History

Maine in 2026 has a tightly limited gambling landscape: two commercial casinos (Hollywood Casino Hotel & Raceway Bangor, owned by Penn Entertainment with real estate held by GLPI; and Oxford Casino, owned by Churchill Downs Incorporated); the Maine State Lottery (since 1974), including the Tri-State Lottery cooperative with New Hampshire and Vermont since 1985; harness racing only — no thoroughbred, greyhound, or quarter-horse circuit — concentrated at Bangor Raceway and the nine agricultural fairs; advance-deposit wagering through Penn ADW LLC; tribal-controlled mobile sports betting through the Penobscot Nation, Houlton Band of Maliseet Indians, and Aroostook Band of Mi'kmaq (Caesars partnership) and the Passamaquoddy Tribe (DraftKings partnership), launched November 3, 2023; daily fantasy sports (authorized 2017); charitable bingo ("beano"), raffles, and games of chance. The Maine Gambling Control Unit (GCU) within the Department of Public Safety regulates casinos, sports wagering, ADW, fantasy contests, and charitable gaming. The Maine Harness Racing Commission regulates harness racing. The Maine State Lottery is part of the Bureau of Alcoholic Beverages and Lottery Operations within DAFS. No commercial iGaming is authorized, though the GCU now maintains an Internet Gaming page anticipating future authorization.¹

The lands that became Maine — part of Massachusetts until separate statehood in 1820 — were home to the Wabanaki confederacy (Penobscot, Passamaquoddy, Maliseet, Mi'kmaq) long before European contact, with traditional games of skill and chance including the bowl-and-dice waltes. The Maine Constitution of 1820 (Article IV, Part Third, §16) banned lotteries — a clause that constrained Maine gambling policy for over 150 years. Maine was also a national leader in alcohol prohibition (the 1851 "Maine Law" was the first state alcohol-prohibition statute), and the Protestant reform impulse extended to gambling. Harness racing emerged at agricultural fairs after the Civil War; Bangor's Bass Park began continuous harness racing in 1893, and the 1924 statute authorizing pari-mutuel wagering at agricultural-society fairgrounds was Maine's first formal gambling legalization. The 1973 constitutional amendment lifted the lottery ban; the Maine State Lottery began operations June 27, 1974, with Gov. Kenneth Curtis drawing the first numbers and Ivan Lazure of Lewiston winning the first $20 prize. Beano was legalized for charitable organizations in 1975 (8 M.R.S. Chapter 13). In 1985 Maine joined Vermont and New Hampshire to launch the Tri-State Lottery — one of the earliest U.S. multi-state lottery cooperatives — and Maine joined Powerball in 2004 and Mega Millions in 2010.²

The defining feature of Maine tribal gaming is the **Maine Indian Claims Settlement Act of 1980 (Pub. L. 96-420)**, signed by President Carter on Oct. 10, 1980, and its companion **Maine Implementing Act** (30 M.R.S. §§6201-6214). The Settlement Act resolved a sweeping land-claim suit by the Penobscot, Passamaquoddy, Maliseet, and Mi'kmaq based on the federal Trade and Intercourse Act of 1790; the settlement paid $81.5 million. Crucially, the Maine Implementing Act subjected the federally recognized Maine tribes to most Maine state laws — a structural posture distinct from the general federal-Indian-law framework. Federal courts (First Circuit decisions in *Aroostook Band of Micmacs v. Ryan* and *Passamaquoddy Tribe v. State of Maine*) have interpreted this to subject Maine tribes to state gambling law and to limit IGRA's applicability — meaning Maine tribes structurally could not open IGRA Class III casinos as the Mashantucket Pequot did at Foxwoods in 1992. Repeated efforts in the Legislature and at the ballot box (notably the November 2003 Sanford "casino on the river" referendum, defeated about 2-to-1) failed.³ In **November 2003**, voters approved slot machines at the commercial harness-racing tracks ("racinos"); Hollywood Slots opened a temporary Bangor facility in 2005, and the $132 million permanent Hollywood Casino Hotel followed in July 2008. Table games were authorized in 2011. The **November 2010** ballot measure authorized Oxford Casino, which opened June 5, 2012; Churchill Downs bought it from Black Bear Entertainment for $160 million in 2013. Subsequent expansion proposals — a York County casino in 2017, periodic tribal-casino proposals — were defeated.

The 2022 sports-wagering authorization was Maine's first formal tribal gaming authorization. **LD 585 / Public Law 2021, Chapter 681**, signed by Gov. Janet Mills on May 2, 2022, reserved **online sports wagering licenses for the four federally recognized Wabanaki tribes**, with retail sports wagering at the two commercial casinos, four off-track betting facilities, and Maine's licensed commercial harness racing tracks. Three of the four tribes (Penobscot, Maliseet, Mi'kmaq) partnered with Caesars Entertainment; the Passamaquoddy Tribe partnered with DraftKings. Maine mobile sports wagering went live simultaneously at 9 a.m. on **November 3, 2023**.⁴

## Regulatory framework today

The Maine **Gambling Control Unit**, Department of Public Safety (https://www.maine.gov/dps/gcu), regulates Casinos, Sports Wagering, ADW, Fantasy Contests, Internet Gaming (pending authorization), and Charitable Gaming under 8 M.R.S. Chapters 11, 13, 13-A, 31, 33, and 35; the Gambling Control Board (within the GCU) is the rulemaking body. The Maine **Harness Racing Commission** (https://www.maine.gov/dacf/harnessracing/index.shtml) regulates harness racing under 8 M.R.S. Chapter 11. The Maine **State Lottery**, within the Bureau of Alcoholic Beverages and Lottery Operations, DAFS (https://www.mainelottery.com/), operates the lottery. The Maine Department of Health and Human Services / Maine Center for Disease Control and Prevention (https://www.maine.gov/dhhs/mecdc/healthy-living/substance-use-and-behavioral-health/problem-gambling) administers prevention and treatment funding through the **Gambling Addiction Prevention and Treatment Fund** (5 M.R.S. §20006-B), with revenue allocated under 8 M.R.S. §1036.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Maine operates **two distinct GCU-administered self-exclusion lists**, both recently expanded to permit online enrollment (announced March 2024 during Problem Gambling Awareness Month). The lists are the **Casino Self-Exclusion List** and the **Sports Wagering Unauthorized Persons / Unauthorized Exclusion List**. The official lists are held by the Gambling Control Board / Gambling Control Unit, with release of names only to casino/slot operators (for the casino list) or licensed sports wagering operators (for the sports list).⁵

**Administering agency.** The Maine Gambling Control Unit (Department of Public Safety) under 8 M.R.S. Chapters 31 and 35 and the related rules in Title 16. The clinical service-delivery contractor is **AdCare Educational Institute of Maine**, now operating under the name **ADEPT Maine**; ADEPT manages the Gambling Addiction Treatment Reimbursement Network and coordinates the self-exclusion enrollment infrastructure. Lori Manson is ADEPT Maine's Problem Gambling Services Coordinator: (207) 620-5229 (talk or text, M-F 8:30 a.m.-4:30 p.m.) or lmanson@adeptme.org / lmanson@adcareme.org. AdCare's main line is (207) 626-3615 ext. 20.⁶

**Enrollment options.** A person may enroll in either list (or both) in three principal ways: (1) **Online via Zoom or FaceTime** with trained ADEPT staff — contact Lori Manson to schedule; she emails a fillable form, the person completes and signs online, and the signed form is forwarded to the GCU. This pathway was announced March 2024 and became the practical default. (2) **In person at one of two GCU-published locations**: the **Maine Gambling Control Unit, 45 Commerce Drive Suite 3, Augusta, ME 04333** (main line 207-626-3900), or **Hollywood Casino, 500 Main Street, Bangor, ME 04401** (1-877-779-7771), or **Oxford Casino, 777 Casino Way, Oxford, ME 04270** (207-539-6700). The ADEPT-maintained location directory adds in-person ADEPT-affiliated sites at Bangor Public Health and Community Services (Bangor), BARN/Bangor Area Recovery Network (Brewer), Kennebec Behavioral Health (Augusta, Skowhegan, Waterville), Portland Recovery Community Center (Portland), Searsport Counseling Associates (Belfast), and Coastal Recovery Community Center (Rockland). (3) **Bring a completed form** downloaded from the GCU website. At enrollment the person reads and signs the State of Maine Gambling Control Board Self-Exclusion Request Form and Waiver of Liability, presents government-issued photo ID with current address and date of birth (a state ID, driver's license, or passport is acceptable), provides a photo (or has one taken) less than six months old, and may optionally provide Players Card number and Social Security number. Forms and photos are forwarded immediately to the Gambling Control Board.⁷

**Duration.** Both lists offer **1 year, 3 years, 5 years, or lifetime**. For the **Sports Wagering Unauthorized Exclusion List**, Maine imposes a **distinctive lifetime-staging requirement**: a person must complete one of the shorter durations (1, 3, or 5 years) **before** they can sign up for the lifetime list. This staged-lifetime model is rare among U.S. jurisdictions and was a deliberate design choice during 2022-2023 rulemaking for the new sports market.⁸

**Scope.** The **Casino Self-Exclusion List** covers Hollywood Casino Bangor and Oxford Casino — all slot machines, table games, and on-site sports wagering at those properties — plus check-cashing and player-privilege programs. The **Sports Wagering Unauthorized Exclusion List** covers retail and **mobile** sports wagering with Caesars (Penobscot/Maliseet/Mi'kmaq) and DraftKings (Passamaquoddy), as well as retail sportsbooks at off-track betting facilities and the licensed harness-racing tracks. **Pari-mutuel ADW** through Penn ADW LLC is a separate licensee; Penn applies its own responsible-gaming controls and honors the GCU sports-wagering list at the account level. The **Maine State Lottery** is not within the GCU lists — there is no Maine lottery-administered statewide self-exclusion list, although the lottery participates in NASPL-coordinated responsible-play training and signage. **Charitable beano and games of chance** are not within the lists. **Maine has no commercial iGaming**, so no online-casino exclusion list exists yet, though the GCU has built an Internet Gaming framework page anticipating authorization.

**Removal process.** One-, three-, and five-year enrollees roll off automatically at the end of the period on the last day of the month of the original enrollment date (the date is recorded on the request form). **Lifetime enrollees may petition the Executive Director of the Gambling Control Unit, on behalf of the Gambling Control Board, in writing, five years after placement on the list.** The petition is discretionary; Board approval is not guaranteed. There is no automatic removal of lifetime enrollees. A clinician letter is commonly submitted with the petition.⁹

**Penalties for breach.** A person on the Casino Self-Exclusion List who enters Hollywood Bangor or Oxford Casino may be **arrested for trespass** under 17-A M.R.S. §402 and Maine casino rules; **all winnings are forfeited to the State** for the entire enrollment period; losses are not recoverable; and check-cashing and complimentary services are barred. Sports-wagering breach by an Unauthorized Exclusion enrollee results in account closure, winnings forfeiture, and licensee discipline by the GCU. **A family member cannot place another adult on either list** — both lists are strictly voluntary and require the enrollee's in-person or supervised-online signature.¹⁰

**Multi-state participation.** Maine is not a member of MSIGA (no online casino is authorized). Caesars and DraftKings, as national operators, apply enrollments on other state lists to a Maine account at the operator account level under internal compliance, but Maine law does not require formal reciprocity. The two tribal-licensed mobile platforms (Caesars and DraftKings under the Wabanaki tribal licenses) honor the GCU Sports Wagering Unauthorized Exclusion List as a condition of their Maine licensure under 8 M.R.S. Chapter 35.

**State of the program in 2026.** Maine's program operates against the backdrop of the **Gambling Addiction Prevention and Treatment Fund (5 M.R.S. §20006-B)**, a dedicated nonlapsing fund administered by DHHS, with a statutory $100,000 annual transfer from net slot-machine income (8 M.R.S. §1036) since FY2013, plus additional revenue from the 2022 sports-wagering allocation. The state helpline is **211 Maine** — call 2-1-1, text "gambling" to 898-211, or visit https://211maine.org — staffed 24/7 and the routing destination for calls from Maine area codes (207) to 1-800-GAMBLER. ADEPT Maine (https://adeptme.org/) operates the Gambling Addiction Treatment Reimbursement Network of clinicians providing subsidized or no-cost counseling, partners with GamFin for financial counseling, and created the Maine Problem Gambling Screening Toolkit in 2021. The Maine Council on Problem Gambling (https://maineproblemgambling.org/), a volunteer 501(c)(3), supplements state-funded services. Annual reports to the Joint Standing Committee with jurisdiction over gambling matters are published each March 1 by the DHHS commissioner.¹¹
