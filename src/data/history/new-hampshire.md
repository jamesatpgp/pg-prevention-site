# New Hampshire — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

New Hampshire operates the **first modern state lottery in America** (launched 1964), statewide mobile and retail sports betting under an exclusive DraftKings contract, a substantial quasi-casino **charitable gaming** sector (poker, blackjack, roulette, craps at roughly 15–20 commercial-style "charitable gaming" halls with a nonprofit beneficiary requirement), and traditional bingo and pull-tab gaming for nonprofits. There is **no commercial Las Vegas-style destination casino** and no federally recognized tribe with gaming-eligible territory. Pari-mutuel racing has effectively ended.¹

## A compressed history

Colonial New Hampshire authorized at least a dozen public lotteries between the 1740s and 1810s to fund roads, bridges, schools, and Dartmouth College (with mixed success), before public opposition built and the legislature suppressed lotteries by 1842. The Abenaki and Pennacook peoples had pre-contact gaming traditions including the bowl-and-die "hubbub" game documented by Pilgrim and Puritan observers; the Abenaki are state-recognized but not federally recognized in New Hampshire.²

Pari-mutuel racing was authorized in 1933 (in response to neighboring Massachusetts' 1934 statute) and the Racing Commission was established. **Rockingham Park** in Salem — which had briefly opened June 21, 1906 before being shut down by the attorney general for unauthorized wagering — reopened under the 1933 statute and ran thoroughbreds until 2002, harness until later in the 2000s, and continued simulcast and charitable gaming through 2016 before final closure in 2009/demolition in 2017. **Hinsdale Greyhound Park** (1958–2008) and **Seabrook Greyhound Park** (1973–2008) ran dog racing. Greyhound racing was prohibited effective January 1, 2010 by HB 630 (Chapter 263 of the Laws of 2008).

**The New Hampshire Sweepstakes — the first modern state lottery in America.** Governor John W. King and Representative Larry Pickett championed the New Hampshire Sweepstakes Act (Chapter 327 of the Laws of 1963) as a way to fund education without imposing a sales or income tax — preserving New Hampshire's "no broad-based tax" political identity. Voters approved in March 1964; the first ticket sold March 12, 1964, and the first drawing — structured as a horse-race sweepstake tied to Rockingham Park to skirt the technical "lottery" term — was held September 12, 1964. Federal authorities initially threatened mail and broadcast enforcement, but careful in-person ticket-sales-only structure avoided preemption. Within seven years New York (1967), New Jersey (1970), Massachusetts (1971), Connecticut (1972), and many others followed.³ The Tri-State Megabucks compact with Vermont and Maine (launched 1985) was the first multi-state lottery game in U.S. history. New Hampshire launched **iLottery** in September 2018, among the earliest states to do so.

**Charitable gaming evolved into a quasi-casino sector** unique in New England. Under RSA 287-D, "games of chance" — blackjack, poker, roulette, craps, and other house-banked games — are authorized at licensed charitable gaming halls with net proceeds (after operator's fee and prizes) going to qualifying nonprofits. The result: approximately 15–20 commercial-style facilities including The Nash Casino (Nashua), Boston Billiard Club & Casino (Nashua), Filotimo Casino (Manchester), Lakes Region Casino (Belmont), and others. Daily fee/buy-in limits, prize caps, and operator-fee restrictions apply.

**Sports betting.** The Sports Betting Act (HB 480, Chapter 348 of the Laws of 2019) was signed by Governor Chris Sununu on July 12, 2019. DraftKings won the **exclusive statewide mobile sports betting contract** through competitive procurement. Mobile sports betting launched December 30, 2019; retail sports betting at lottery-authorized locations (Dover, Manchester, Seabrook, Nashua) followed in 2020. The exclusive-operator model is unusual — one mobile operator statewide — and produces a 51% revenue share to the state but limits competition.⁴

Defunct: Rockingham Park (closed 2009, demolished 2017); Hinsdale and Seabrook greyhound (closed 2008, prohibited 2010); colonial lotteries (suppressed by 1842).

## Regulatory framework today

The New Hampshire Lottery Commission (https://www.nhlottery.com) operates the state lottery, iLottery, and — through its Sports Betting Division — regulates sports wagering under RSA 287-H and RSA 287-I. Its Charitable Gaming Division (https://www.compliance.lottery.nh.gov/) regulates the RSA 287-D games of chance halls, RSA 287-E bingo and lucky-7 pull-tab gaming, and simulcast wagering. Pari-mutuel authority remains under RSA 284 within Lottery Commission jurisdiction. The New Hampshire Council on Problem Gambling (https://www.nhproblemgambling.org) is the principal NCPG-affiliated nonprofit, and the New Hampshire Department of Health and Human Services Bureau of Drug and Alcohol Services (https://www.dhhs.nh.gov) administers state problem-gambling treatment funding under the Problem Gambling Fund (RSA 287-I).

## Voluntary self-exclusion

New Hampshire's self-exclusion landscape is split between the **DraftKings-operated mobile sports betting platform**, **charitable gaming halls**, and **iLottery products**, all coordinated under the New Hampshire Lottery Commission and the umbrella **GROA (Gaming Responsibility Outreach Alliance) / NH Responsible Gambling Program**.⁵

**1. Sports betting self-exclusion.** Because DraftKings is the exclusive mobile sports betting partner for the NH Lottery, the self-exclusion process is tightly integrated into the DraftKings platform under state contract requirements. A registered DraftKings NH user may activate self-exclusion through the account-management interface, choosing a duration (typical options: 6 months, 1 year, 5 years, or lifetime). The exclusion blocks the user from both the mobile app and the four retail DraftKings sports books at Dover, Manchester, Seabrook, and Nashua. The Lottery Commission also requires **deposit limits and cooling-off periods** to be available to all NH DraftKings users under the exclusive-operator contract. Removal at term expiration requires an affirmative request through DraftKings customer service and a brief cooling-off period before reinstatement.⁶

**2. Charitable gaming hall self-exclusion.** The NH Lottery Commission's Charitable Gaming Division administers a self-exclusion program for the RSA 287-D charitable gaming halls. Enrollment is in person at any licensed hall or at the Lottery Commission's offices in Concord. Standard durations are 1 year, 5 years, and lifetime. The exclusion is propagated to all licensed RSA 287-D halls statewide — a person excluded from one hall is excluded from all. Bingo halls and lucky-7 venues operate under separate but coordinated procedures.

**3. iLottery self-exclusion.** New Hampshire iLottery players may self-exclude through their iLottery account, with the same general framework as charitable gaming halls. Terms include 1 year, 5 years, and lifetime.

The three programs are **administratively unified under GROA** but not yet fully integrated into a single enrollment portal. A person seeking comprehensive exclusion typically must enroll separately in each subsystem (DraftKings, charitable gaming, iLottery). New Hampshire is **not** currently a participant in the multi-state National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP at nvsep.org), though NCPG advocacy has urged accession. New Hampshire has no commercial casino, no online casino, and no MSIGA online poker exposure.

Breach: An excluded person who places a wager at any covered facility forfeits winnings to the Lottery Commission's Problem Gambling Fund. DraftKings is required under its exclusive contract to detect and block self-excluded accounts and may be subject to Lottery Commission enforcement penalties for failure. Charitable gaming hall operators face license sanction for failing to enforce. Aggregate enrollment counts across the three subsystems are not regularly published; the program is widely considered modest in size relative to neighboring Massachusetts.⁷
