Wisconsin became a state in 1848. The 1848 constitution prohibited lotteries — language that was amended five separate times in the 20th century to expand permitted forms of gambling. Charitable bingo was authorized by constitutional amendment in 1973; charitable raffles followed in 1977. The state lottery was approved by voters in April 1987; first tickets sold in September 1988. Voters also approved pari-mutuel wagering in November 1987 — uniquely authorizing horse, dog, and snowmobile races, though no snowmobile pari-mutuel ever developed. Five greyhound permits in 1989 produced tracks across the state. The tracks drew about 3.5 million visitors in 1991, but the rapid expansion of tribal Class III casino gaming drained the market; the last track, Dairyland in Kenosha, closed December 31, 2009.

The 1993 constitutional amendment limited state-run gambling to bingo, raffles, pari-mutuel, and the lottery, and barred the lottery from casino-style games. This left tribal compacts as the only path to casino expansion. Lottery proceeds uniquely fund the Wisconsin Lottery and Gaming Credit, which provides direct property-tax relief to homeowners. All 11 federally recognized Wisconsin tribes operate Class III casinos under compacts negotiated through the 1991-1992 compact wave. The Oneida Nation became the first Wisconsin tribe to negotiate a sports-wagering compact amendment with Governor Tony Evers on July 1, 2021; most other tribes have since followed. There is no statewide mobile sports betting and no commercial casino.
