# District of Columbia — Gambling History

## Current landscape

The District of Columbia today permits three legal forms of gambling: the **DC Lottery** (draw, scratch, and iLottery), **charitable gaming** (bingo, raffles, Monte Carlo nights), and **sports wagering** through both retail sportsbooks and competitive mobile platforms. The District has no commercial casinos, no parimutuel racing, and no card rooms. All regulation is consolidated in a single agency — the **Office of Lottery and Gaming (OLG)**, an executive-branch unit under the Office of the Chief Financial Officer, which simultaneously operates the lottery, licenses charitable games, and regulates sports wagering through its Sports Wagering Division.¹

## Compressed history

Through the nineteenth and most of the twentieth century, the District inherited the federal common-law and statutory hostility to gambling that governed the seat of government. No chartered colonial lottery operated in the federal city, and informal numbers games and pool selling were prosecuted under federal District criminal statutes. The constitutional structure — a federal enclave under the plenary authority of Congress with no commercial-casino zoning and dense federal property concentrated around the National Mall and Federal Triangle — has shaped every subsequent gambling-policy choice.

The modern era began with **Initiative 6**, the "Law to Legalize Lotteries, Daily Numbers Games, and Bingo and Raffles for Charitable Purposes," which District voters approved 104,899 to 59,833 on November 4, 1980.² It became D.C. Law 3-172 effective March 10, 1981, and the DC Lottery launched **August 25, 1982**, making the District one of the earliest jurisdictions in the post-1964 lottery era. The original regulator — the DC Lottery and Charitable Games Control Board — was later restructured into the present OLG under the OCFO. Since 1982 the lottery has awarded more than $5 billion in prizes and transferred over $2.43 billion to the General Fund, with charitable licensing supporting more than $141.5 million in non-profit fundraising.³

After the Supreme Court's decision in *Murphy v. NCAA* (2018), the DC Council passed **Bill 22-944** (the Sports Wagering Lottery Amendment Act of 2018) on December 18, 2018; it became D.C. Law 22-312 and took effect May 3, 2019.⁴ The statute designated OLG as both regulator and operator of a District-wide mobile platform, branded "GambetDC," which launched in 2020 and drew sustained criticism over revenue performance. The **Sports Wagering Amendment Act of 2024** (effective July 15, 2024) ended the mobile monopoly: it expanded Class A authorization to mobile/online wagering, created a new **Class C** license for sports-team applicants, and set gross-gaming-revenue tax rates at 20% for Class A, 10% for Class B, and 30% for Class C.⁵

## Regulatory framework today

The **Office of Lottery and Gaming (OLG)** — https://lottery.dc.gov — administers all three legal forms. Within OLG, the **Sports Wagering Division** handles sports-wagering licensing, internal controls, and responsible-gaming oversight. **Charitable Games** licensing is housed in OLG's Fundraising and Charitable Games unit. Criminal prohibitions sit at D.C. Code Title 22 (§ 22-1717 enumerates permissible activities) and the regulatory regime at D.C. Code Title 36, Chapter 6. Treatment referrals are coordinated through the **Department of Behavioral Health (DBH)** — https://dbh.dc.gov.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

**Administering agency:** The **OLG Self-Exclusion Program** is operated by the Office of Lottery and Gaming through its Responsible Gaming Program Specialist. Authority derives from D.C. Code § 36-621.02 and OLG regulations governing sports wagering.⁶

**Scope:** Critically, the DC program is unified across all legal forms — enrolling for sports wagering simultaneously excludes the participant from the DC Lottery, iLottery, and any licensed retail or mobile sports-wagering platform. This is a stricter, more comprehensive scope than most state programs, which typically silo casino, lottery, and sports-betting exclusion lists.

**Where to enroll:** Three pathways. (1) In person at any **Class A or Class B sports-wagering facility** licensed by OLG, each of which is authorized to process applications. (2) In person at **OLG headquarters**, 2235 Shannon Place SE, Washington, DC 20020, by appointment with the Responsible Gaming Program Specialist at **202-788-2102**. (3) **Online** through the OLG website.⁶

**Duration options:** Four terms — **18 months, 3 years, 5 years, or lifetime**.

**Removal process:** Enrollees on the 18-month, 3-year, or 5-year terms remain on the list until the elected term expires; OLG does not provide early removal. **Lifetime enrollment is irrevocable** — there is no removal mechanism.

**Penalties:** Self-excluded persons forfeit any winnings and are prohibited from collecting prizes, recovering losses, or accepting complimentary services from any OLG-licensed retail location, mobile platform, or lottery agent. Licensed operators must refuse service and confiscate winnings.

**2026 program state:** Active. OLG promotes the program every March during Problem Gambling Awareness Month and through year-round messaging on lottery and sportsbook platforms. Source: https://dclottery.com/player-resources/play-responsibly/self-exclusion-program
