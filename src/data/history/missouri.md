St. Louis and Kansas City sit at the confluence of nineteenth-century river-gambling cultures. Mark Twain's Life on the Mississippi documented faro, poker, and three-card monte on St. Louis-bound steamers. The Drake Constitution of 1865 banned any lottery, and the 1875 Constitution carried the prohibition forward — a ban that held for 109 years. Tom Pendergast's Kansas City political machine ran one of the country's most extensive open-gambling operations from 1925 to 1939. It franchised slot machines, dice rooms, and the numbers game to specific operators in exchange for political support. St. Louis had its own structures through Egan's Rats and the Cuckoo Gang. Federal indictments in 1939 and the 1951 Kefauver hearings curtailed but did not end the operations.

Constitutional reform came in stages. Charitable bingo was authorized by amendment in 1980; the state lottery by amendment in November 1984. The first lottery ticket sold January 20, 1986. In 1992, voters approved Proposition A authorizing "games of chance" on excursion gambling boats. The original statute imposed a uniquely Missouri $500 loss limit per two-hour boarding session (repealed in 2008), a cruising requirement (repealed in the early 2000s), and a local-option referendum for each host city. The Argosy in Riverside opened first on June 21, 1994. A 2008 ballot measure capped commercial licenses at thirteen. Voters approved Amendment 2 in November 2024 by a 7,500-vote margin, legalizing retail and statewide mobile sports wagering; both launched at 12:00 a.m. on December 1, 2025.
