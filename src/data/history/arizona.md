Arizona's territorial era (1863-1912) produced the standard frontier saloon-gambling pattern in Tombstone, Bisbee, Prescott, and Jerome. By 1907 the territorial legislature had banned most commercial gambling. Statehood arrived February 14, 1912 without an explicit constitutional lottery prohibition, but anti-gambling statutes carried forward. Arizona was one of only three states that allowed legal pari-mutuel horse betting during national Prohibition (1919-1933) — a consequential exception that seeded the modern racing industry. Rillito Park in Tucson, built in 1940 on the Rukin Jelks stud farm, is recognized as the birthplace of organized Quarter Horse breed racing. Turf Paradise opened January 7, 1956 in Phoenix and anchored Arizona thoroughbred racing for more than 65 years before ending live racing in May 2024.

Proposition 200 in November 1980 made Arizona the first state west of the Mississippi to authorize a lottery; the first ticket sold July 1, 1981. Tribal gaming developed in two waves. The first 16 Class III compacts ran from 1993 to 2003, producing ten tribal casinos by year-end 1994. After federal litigation and a 1996 voter initiative, the Salt River Pima-Maricopa Indian Community signed a compact with Governor Jane Hull in 1998. Proposition 202 in November 2002 authorized a standardized compact framework with revenue sharing to the state. Greyhound racing ended when Governor Doug Ducey signed HB 2127 in 2016. Sports betting was authorized by HB 2772, signed April 15, 2021; event wagering went live on September 9, 2021.
