Gambling ran through every phase of Texas settlement. Spanish colonial card games were legal Crown-revenue activities. The Republic of Texas outlawed gambling in 1837, but enforcement was lax. Frontier-saloon faro and poker dominated the cattle-drive era. Galveston operated an open-city gambling culture run by the Maceo family from the 1920s until the Texas Rangers' 1957 raids ended it. Pari-mutuel racing was legalized in 1933 as a Depression revenue measure, with Arlington Downs (built by oilman W. T. Waggoner) as the marquee track. The legislature repealed pari-mutuel betting in 1937 amid corruption concerns, and Arlington Downs was demolished. After 50 dry years the Texas Racing Act of 1986 was ratified by voters in November 1987.

The Texas Lottery was authorized by Proposition 11 in November 1991. Charitable bingo was authorized by Proposition 4 in 1980. Tribal gaming has had a uniquely litigious history. The 1987 Restoration Act for the Ysleta del Sur Pueblo and Alabama-Coushatta contained "non-gaming clauses" the state used to shut Speaking Rock Casino in 2002 and enjoin Naskila. The 2022 U.S. Supreme Court decision in Ysleta del Sur Pueblo v. Texas held 5-4 that the Restoration Act incorporated only Texas's prohibitions, not its regulations, of gambling — freeing the tribes to expand Class II operations. The Kickapoo Lucky Eagle Casino in Eagle Pass remains the state's largest gaming facility. Greyhound racing ended in December 2019. There is no commercial casino or statewide sports betting in Texas as of 2026.
