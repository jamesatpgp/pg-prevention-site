# New Mexico — Gambling History

## The present

As of 2026 New Mexico operates a mid-sized, hybrid gambling market. Legal forms include: the New Mexico Lottery; pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing at five racetracks (all of which are also racinos); Class II and Class III tribal gaming at facilities run by ten Pueblos, two Apache nations, and the Navajo Nation; charitable bingo, raffles, and pull-tabs; and retail sportsbooks on tribal land under IGRA compacts. There is no statewide commercial sports betting, no iGaming, and no statewide mobile wagering — a tribal-on-premises sportsbook in Santa Ana was the first legal book in the state and remains the model. Regulatory authority is split among the New Mexico Gaming Control Board (racinos, fraternal/veterans gaming, charitable gaming, statewide self-exclusion), the New Mexico Racing Commission (pari-mutuel), the New Mexico Lottery Authority, and the tribal gaming commissions of each compacted nation.¹ ² ³

## How it got here

Gambling in what is now New Mexico is older than the United States. Spanish colonists brought monte and faro into Santa Fe; Pueblo, Apache, and Navajo nations had their own stick-dice and hidden-ball traditions long before contact. After the Mexican-American War the Territorial Assembly initially taxed and licensed gambling houses — Doña Gertrudis "La Tules" Barceló's Burro Alley monte sala in Santa Fe is the era's most famous landmark.⁴ Mining-camp faro and roulette in Elizabethtown, Cimarron, and Lake Valley flourished into the 1880s.

Protestant reform pressure ended the licensing era: the Territorial Assembly criminalized public gambling in 1889 (Laws of 1889, ch. 84), and the 1911 state constitution adopted with statehood in 1912 included Article IV, § 38 restricting lotteries and gift enterprises.⁵ Illegal slots and bookmaking nonetheless flourished along U.S. 66 from the 1930s through the 1950s, and the Kefauver Committee in 1950–1951 documented New Mexico as a Southwest node.

Modern legalization came in waves. Pari-mutuel horse racing was authorized in 1947, with Ruidoso Downs opening the same year and later hosting the All American Futurity (1959), still the world's richest Quarter Horse race. Charitable bingo was authorized by the Bingo and Raffle Act of 1981. Pueblo and Apache nations opened bingo halls in the late 1980s and added Class III devices in the early 1990s — leading to a multi-year compact dispute that the New Mexico Supreme Court resolved against the governor's unilateral authority in *State ex rel. Clark v. Johnson*, 120 N.M. 562 (1995).⁶ The Compact Negotiation Act and Gaming Control Act (Laws of 1997, ch. 190) settled the dispute, created the Gaming Control Board, and authorized up to six racinos plus fraternal/veterans machine gaming. Tribal-state compacts were renegotiated in 2001, 2007, and 2015. Voters approved the Lottery in November 1995; the New Mexico Lottery sold its first ticket on April 27, 1996, with proceeds dedicated to the Lottery Tuition Fund (the "Lottery Scholarship").⁷ The Pueblo of Santa Ana opened a sportsbook on October 16, 2018 under its 2015 compact — the first tribal sportsbook outside Nevada; other Pueblos followed. Statewide mobile/online wagering has been introduced repeatedly in the legislature but has not passed as of 2026.

## Regulatory framework today

- **New Mexico Gaming Control Board (NMGCB)** — racino licensing, fraternal/veterans gaming, charitable bingo and raffles, and the statewide self-exclusion list. https://www.gcb.nm.gov/
- **New Mexico Racing Commission (NMRC)** — pari-mutuel wagering, track licensing, drug-testing. https://www.nmrc.state.nm.us/
- **New Mexico Lottery Authority** — quasi-public corporation administering the state Lottery. https://www.nmlottery.com/
- **Tribal gaming commissions** — Class II/III gaming on each nation's lands under IGRA and compact terms; the **National Indian Gaming Commission** publishes the compacts and gaming-tribe roster.⁸ https://www.nigc.gov/

## Voluntary self-exclusion

New Mexico runs a **single statewide self-exclusion list administered by the NMGCB** that covers every racino, fraternal/veterans licensee, and (through compact obligations) every tribal Class III casino in the state — a meaningful contrast with neighboring states that fragment the list between commercial and tribal operators. The program is grounded in 15.1.2 NMAC and the tribal-state compacts.⁹

**Where to enroll.** Enrollment is in person by appointment at the NMGCB office, 4900 Alameda Blvd. NE, Albuquerque, with additional appointment locations announced by the Board. The applicant brings government-issued ID and completes a notarized "Request for Self-Exclusion from New Mexico Gaming Facilities" form. There is no online enrollment portal as of 2026.¹⁰

**Duration.** Applicants choose one year (minimum 12 months), five years, or lifetime. Removal at the end of a one- or five-year term is not automatic — the participant must affirmatively request removal in writing.

**Scope.** The list covers every licensed gaming facility in New Mexico — five racinos (Sunland Park, Ruidoso Downs, The Downs at Albuquerque, SunRay Park, Zia Park) plus fraternal/veterans gaming machines and, by compact term, the Class III casinos operated by the Pueblos of Sandia, Isleta, Santa Ana, Pojoaque, San Felipe, Santa Clara, Tesuque, Acoma, Laguna, and Ohkay Owingeh, the Mescalero Apache Tribe, the Jicarilla Apache Nation, and the Navajo Nation. The Lottery is not covered by the NMGCB list; individual retailers do not enforce it.

**Penalties for breach.** A self-excluded person discovered on premises forfeits winnings, may be removed and trespassed, and risks criminal trespass charges. Marketing, complimentaries, and credit must be terminated by every licensee.

**Multi-state.** New Mexico does not participate in any multi-state self-exclusion compact. Major operators (Caesars, MGM, Boyd) may apply corporate cross-property exclusion at affiliated facilities outside New Mexico, but that is a corporate policy and not a state guarantee.

**Help.** The 24/7 NM gambling helpline runs at 1-888-696-2440 through the **Responsible Gaming Association of New Mexico**, the operator-funded coalition. ## Problem gambling response

The **Responsible Gaming Association of New Mexico (RGANM)**, incorporated 2007, is the operator-funded coalition of tribal and commercial licensees, the Lottery Authority, and treatment providers; it funds the helpline and clinician training. The New Mexico Human Services Department and Department of Health periodically fund treatment slots, but NCPG's annual State of the States survey has historically ranked New Mexico low for dedicated per-capita public funding relative to gambling revenue.¹¹
