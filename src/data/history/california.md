California's gambling history begins with the Gold Rush of 1849-1855. The 1872 California Penal Code created the lasting legal framework: Penal Code § 330 prohibited faro, monte, roulette, lansquenet, rouge et noir, fan-tan, twenty-one, and "any banking or percentage game" — but omitted poker. That legislative gap defines California cardroom law to this day. The 1879 Constitution added Article IV § 19 prohibiting lotteries. Gardena became a center of permitted draw-poker clubs from the late 1930s. Prohibition-era offshore gambling ships — the Rex, the Lux, the Tango — operated off Santa Monica until Attorney General Earl Warren's 1939 raids.

Pari-mutuel horse racing was legalized by ballot in 1933; Santa Anita Park opened in 1934 and Del Mar (co-founded by Bing Crosby) in 1937. The California State Lottery was authorized by Proposition 37 in November 1984; first ticket sales began October 3, 1985. Tribal gaming followed three landmark legal events. The U.S. Supreme Court's 1987 California v. Cabazon Band of Mission Indians decision triggered Congress's 1988 Indian Gaming Regulatory Act. Proposition 5 in November 1998 was struck down by the California Supreme Court; Proposition 1A in March 2000 amended the constitution to authorize tribal Class III gaming by compact. The November 2022 ballot fight produced the most expensive ballot-measure campaign in U.S. history at about $450 million; voters rejected both Proposition 26 (tribal retail sports betting) and Proposition 27 (commercial online sports betting). California has no legal sports betting as of 2026.
