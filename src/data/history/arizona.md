# Arizona — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
Arizona is a permissive, tribally anchored gambling state with a robust mobile-sports-betting industry. Legal verticals include the **Arizona State Lottery** (Powerball, Mega Millions, scratch, Keno; administered by the **Arizona Department of Gaming**); **pari-mutuel horse and Quarter Horse racing** (Arizona Racing Division, also within the Department of Gaming); **tribal Class III casinos** at over 20 properties run by federally recognized tribes under the 2002 amended compacts; **event wagering** (retail and mobile sports betting) launched September 9, 2021 under HB 2772; and authorized **fantasy sports contests**. There is no commercial (non-tribal) casino industry and no legal online casino. Live greyhound racing is prohibited. The **Arizona Department of Gaming** is the central regulator across nearly every vertical, with tribal-state compacts adding a tribal-gaming layer.[^1][^2]

## How Arizona got here
Arizona's territorial era (1863-1912) produced the standard frontier saloon-gambling pattern in Tombstone, Bisbee, Prescott, and Jerome; by 1907 the territorial legislature had banned most commercial gambling. Statehood arrived February 14, 1912 without an explicit constitutional lottery prohibition, but anti-gambling statutes carried forward. **Arizona was one of only three states that allowed legal pari-mutuel horse betting during national Prohibition (1919-1933)** — a consequential exception that seeded the modern racing industry. Rillito Park (Tucson, built 1940 on the Rukin Jelks stud farm) is recognized as the birthplace of organized Quarter Horse breed racing and was added to the National Register in 2012; Turf Paradise opened January 7, 1956 in Phoenix and anchored Arizona thoroughbred racing for 65+ years before ending live racing in May 2024.[^1][^3]

The lottery and modern gaming sectors arrived in steady increments. **Proposition 200** (November 1980) made Arizona the first state west of the Mississippi to authorize a lottery; the first ticket — a "Scratch It Rich" scratch game — went on sale July 1, 1981. Arizona joined Powerball on April 3, 1994 and Mega Millions on April 18, 2010. Tribal gaming developed in two waves: the first 16 tribal-state Class III compacts ran 1993-2003 and produced ten tribal casinos by year-end 1994. The Salt River Pima-Maricopa Indian Community's federal litigation, the *Rumsey* Ninth Circuit doctrine, and a 1996 voter initiative forced Governor Jane Hull to sign the SRP-MIC compact on August 18, 1998. **Proposition 202** (November 2002) authorized a standardized compact framework with revenue sharing to the state and is the legal foundation of every modern Arizona tribal casino — Talking Stick (SRP-MIC), Wild Horse Pass, Vee Quiva, and Lone Butte (Gila River), Desert Diamond properties (Tohono O'odham), Harrah's Ak-Chin, and Fort McDowell. Live greyhound racing — once anchored by Tucson Greyhound Park and Phoenix Greyhound Park — ended when Governor Doug Ducey signed **HB 2127** in 2016; Tucson's last race ran June 25, 2016.[^1][^4][^5]

Sports betting and modern event wagering followed *Murphy v. NCAA* (2018) by three years. Governor Ducey signed **HB 2772** on April 15, 2021; the Department of the Interior approved the amended Arizona Gaming Compact on May 24, 2021; and event wagering went live on September 9, 2021 — strategically timed to the NFL season opening. The Act authorizes up to 20 mobile/online event-wagering licenses split between tribal casinos and professional sports venues, retail sportsbooks at qualified locations, and fantasy sports contests under the same statute.[^6]

## Regulatory framework today
The **Arizona Department of Gaming** ([gaming.az.gov](https://gaming.az.gov/)) is the lead regulator, with internal divisions for **Tribal Gaming**, the **Racing Division**, **Event Wagering & Fantasy Sports (EWFS)**, and the **Division of Problem Gambling**. The **Arizona State Lottery Commission** is administered under the Department of Gaming. Charitable bingo and raffles are administered separately under A.R.S. § 5-401 et seq. by the **Arizona Department of Revenue, Bingo Section**. Tribal Class III gaming operates under each tribe's tribal gaming office subject to federal NIGC oversight and the compact framework.[^2][^7]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
Arizona operates one of the longer-running and more clearly documented VSE programs in the United States. The **Arizona Department of Gaming** administers a **single statewide Self-Exclusion Program** with two coverages that a registrant may choose individually or together: (1) **Arizona Tribal Gaming Facilities** (all Class III tribal casinos in Arizona, including ancillary services on casino property such as restaurants, concerts, hotels, and conferences), and (2) **Event Wagering & Fantasy Sports (EWFS) Contests** (all licensed retail sportsbooks and all in-state mobile/online sportsbook platforms, plus fantasy-sports operators). Lottery retail purchases at convenience stores are not within the scope of the formal self-exclusion list — that is a known gap.[^7][^8]

**Where to enroll**: The Department of Gaming runs an online portal at **[gaming.az.gov/exclude](https://gaming.az.gov/exclude)** with a downloadable Self-Exclusion Form ([gaming.az.gov/self-exclusion-form](https://gaming.az.gov/self-exclusion-form)). The form must be **notarized** and submitted with a recent color photograph; submission may be made in person at the Department of Gaming office (Phoenix) or by mail. Once received and processed, self-exclusion typically takes effect within 72 hours (often the same business day).[^7]

**Duration options**: registrants choose **one year, five years, or ten years**. (Arizona does not currently offer a lifetime option in published materials — a notable structural difference from Florida, Connecticut, and Delaware, which all offer lifetime.)[^7]

**Removal process**: Self-exclusion is **irrevocable for the chosen term**. The form is binding and cannot be altered or rescinded for any reason during the elected period. At term expiration the name does not auto-renew but the registrant must take affirmative steps to re-enter if desired; the Department processes renewal requests on a case-by-case basis.[^7]

**Penalties for breach**: A self-excluded person who enters a tribal gaming facility or places a wager on an EWFS platform is **prohibited from collecting any winnings and recovering any losses**. Operators that allow play by self-excluded persons face regulatory action and forfeiture of revenue. Tribal casino property may also pursue trespass charges under tribal law.[^7]

**Multi-state participation**: Arizona is **not a signatory to MSIGA** (which is irrelevant absent licensed iGaming) and does not publicly enroll in the National Indian Gaming Association's Tribal Self-Exclusion Database; tribal exclusions in Arizona are administered through the statewide Department of Gaming list. The Department's 2024 public-service campaign promoted the program statewide. The Department also operates the helpline **1-800-NEXT-STEP (1-800-639-8783)**. Standards for licensee responsible-gaming compliance appear in **Appendix M** to the model compact. As of 2025, the Department reported steady enrollment growth following the launch of the 2024 PSA campaign; exact 2026 enrollment counts are **[unconfirmed]** pending the Department's next annual report.[^7][^8]
