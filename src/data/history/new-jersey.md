# New Jersey — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

New Jersey is the most regulatorily mature multi-vertical gaming market in the United States. Nine commercial casinos operate in Atlantic City (Bally's, Borgata, Caesars, Golden Nugget, Hard Rock, Harrah's, Ocean, Resorts, and Tropicana); New Jersey was the **first state to authorize comprehensive online casino gaming** (slots, table games, poker — launched November 2013) and now runs the largest U.S. online casino market by revenue, frequently exceeding $200 million in monthly GGR. Statewide mobile and retail sports betting has been live since June 2018 with 20+ licensed brands; the New Jersey Lottery is among the highest per-capita lottery revenue producers; pari-mutuel racing continues at Monmouth Park, Meadowlands, and Freehold; and charitable bingo and raffles are operated under the Legalized Games of Chance framework. There are no federally recognized tribes with gaming-eligible reservation territory.¹

## A compressed history

New Jersey's colonial gambling record is among the longest in North America. The Province authorized at least 17 public lotteries between 1737 and 1776, funding the College of New Jersey (Princeton), churches, bridges, and the colonial militia. The 1844 Constitution at Article IV § 7, paragraph 2 prohibited any legislative lottery authorization — a ban that held for 125 years.² Horse racing has a longer continuous history than in almost any other northern state: the Monmouth Park Racing Association founded the first Monmouth Park in 1870 (closed 1894 after the anti-pool-selling law). Atlantic City was incorporated 1854 as a Quaker temperance resort but ran extensive illegal back-room casinos through the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries; Mayor Enoch "Nucky" Johnson (1911–1941) tolerated gambling, prostitution, and bootleg liquor. The 1929 Atlantic City Conference at the President Hotel — Lansky, Luciano, Capone, Costello, Torrio — helped form the National Crime Syndicate's framework.³

Pari-mutuel was legalized by 1939 amendment; Monmouth Park reopened June 19, 1946 on a new Oceanport site. Garden State Park (Cherry Hill, 1942–2001, with a 1977 fire interregnum) and the Meadowlands (East Rutherford, 1976) anchored the postwar racing era. Greyhound racing was authorized in 1934 but never operated commercially; the Racing Commission's greyhound jurisdiction was repealed in 1955.

The lottery prohibition fell by 1969 amendment, and the New Jersey Lottery launched December 16, 1970 under P.L.1970, c. 13 (N.J.S.A. 5:9-1 et seq.), with proceeds dedicated to the Aid to Education and State Institutions Fund.

**Casino legalization** came after a 1974 statewide referendum failed and a 1976 Atlantic-City-only proposal succeeded. The **Casino Control Act** (P.L.1977, c. 110, N.J.S.A. 5:12-1 et seq.), signed by Governor Brendan Byrne on June 2, 1977, made New Jersey the second U.S. state — 45 years after Nevada — to authorize commercial casino gambling. The Act created two regulators: the **Casino Control Commission (CCC)** as adjudicatory licensing body and the **Division of Gaming Enforcement (DGE)** within the Department of Law and Public Safety as the investigative arm, a bifurcated structure modeled on Nevada. Resorts International opened May 26, 1978; eight major casino hotels followed within four years. Trump Taj Mahal opened April 2, 1990 as the largest casino-hotel in the world. The golden era peaked at $5.2 billion gross gaming revenue in 2006. The 2014 contraction closed four casinos (Atlantic Club, Showboat, Revel, Trump Plaza) in a single year. June 27, 2018 brought the dual reopenings of Hard Rock (former Taj Mahal) and Ocean Casino Resort (former Revel).⁴

**Internet gaming.** Assembly Bill A2578, signed by Governor Chris Christie on February 26, 2013 (P.L.2013, c. 27, N.J.S.A. 5:12-95.17 et seq.), authorized Atlantic City casino licensees to offer online casino games to players physically located in New Jersey. The launch on November 21–26, 2013 made New Jersey the first U.S. state with comprehensive online casino. NJ joined Nevada and Delaware in the **Multi-State Internet Gaming Agreement (MSIGA)** in 2017 for online poker shared liquidity; Michigan (2022), Pennsylvania (2025), and West Virginia have since joined.⁵

**Sports betting** — New Jersey's most historic contribution. After losing repeated PASPA challenges (*NCAA v. Christie I* (2013); Third Circuit en banc 2016), the state's partial-repeal strategy reached the Supreme Court. **Murphy v. NCAA**, decided **May 14, 2018**, struck down PASPA on Tenth Amendment anti-commandeering grounds. New Jersey enacted P.L.2018, c. 33 (signed by Governor Phil Murphy June 11, 2018), and Monmouth Park took the first legal sports bet outside Nevada on **June 14, 2018**. Mobile launched August 6, 2018.⁶

Defunct: original Monmouth Park (1870–1894); Garden State Park (closed 2001); Atlantic City Race Course (live racing ended 2015); Brighton Park/Sands AC (closed 2006, demolished 2007); Atlantic Club, Showboat, Revel, Trump Plaza (closed 2014); commercial greyhound racing (authorized but never operated).

## Regulatory framework today

The **New Jersey Casino Control Commission** (https://www.nj.gov/casinos) licenses Atlantic City casinos and adjudicates contested licensing matters under the Casino Control Act. The **New Jersey Division of Gaming Enforcement** (https://www.njoag.gov/about/divisions-and-offices/division-of-gaming-enforcement-home/), within the Office of the Attorney General, is the investigative and enforcement arm and **directly administers New Jersey's comprehensive statewide self-exclusion program** for casinos, online casino, mobile sports betting, and racetrack sports books. The New Jersey Lottery (https://www.njlottery.com) operates under N.J.S.A. 5:9. The New Jersey Racing Commission (https://www.nj.gov/oag/racing) regulates pari-mutuel under Title 5. The Council on Compulsive Gambling of New Jersey (CCGNJ, https://www.800gambler.org), founded 1983, runs the 1-800-GAMBLER helpline. The Rutgers Center for Gambling Studies (https://gamblingstudies.rutgers.edu), founded 1983, is one of the oldest U.S. academic gambling research centers. The Department of Human Services Division of Mental Health and Addiction Services (https://www.nj.gov/humanservices/dmhas) administers state-funded treatment through the Compulsive Gambling Treatment Fund (N.J.S.A. 5:12-189).

## Voluntary self-exclusion

The Division of Gaming Enforcement operates **the most comprehensive multi-vertical voluntary self-exclusion (VSE) program in the United States** — a single state-administered list covering Atlantic City casinos, the casinos' iGaming products, all licensed mobile and retail sports wagering operators statewide, racetrack sports wagering facilities, and online sports betting. One enrollment covers everything regulated by DGE. The program is administered under N.J.A.C. 13:69G-2 and is widely cited as the U.S. model.⁷

**Enrollment.** Three pathways:
1. **Online through the DGE self-exclusion portal** at https://www.njportal.com/dge/selfexclusion — the primary route, with identity verification via uploaded government ID and biometric photo.
2. **Video conference** with DGE staff, available by appointment for individuals who prefer assisted enrollment or who want exclusion limited to internet gaming and sports wagering only.
3. **In-person** at the DGE offices in Atlantic City or Trenton during business hours.

**Duration.** Three options: **1 year, 5 years, or lifetime**. Lifetime is irrevocable. The 1-year and 5-year enrollments do **not** lapse automatically at term expiration — the enrollee must submit Form 71 (Request for Removal from Voluntary Self-Exclusion List) to DGE after the term ends, and DGE conducts a review before removal.⁸

**Scope.** Enrollment on the DGE list covers, with a single application, **all** of the following:
- All Atlantic City casinos (gaming floors, sports books, hotels, restaurants, marketing lists, check-cashing, credit issuance)
- All New Jersey-licensed mobile sports betting operators (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars, Fanatics, ESPN BET, BetRivers, Hard Rock Bet, and roughly 20 total licensed brands)
- All Atlantic City casino-affiliated online casino (iGaming) sites (slots, table games, online poker)
- All retail sports wagering facilities at the four New Jersey racetracks (Monmouth Park, Meadowlands, Freehold, and Atlantic City Race Course's successor wagering operations)

A separate "internet-only" enrollment option is available for individuals who wish to exclude from online casino and online sports wagering but not from physical casino floors; this is the option most often used by remote enrollees via video conference.⁹

**Multi-state participation.** New Jersey is a founding member of MSIGA (since 2017) for online poker shared liquidity with Nevada, Delaware, Michigan, Pennsylvania, and West Virginia. A DGE self-exclusion automatically blocks the enrolled player from all MSIGA shared-liquidity online poker tables, providing de facto interstate exclusion for online poker. New Jersey is **not currently** a participating NVSEP (https://www.nvsep.org/) jurisdiction, though the DGE program is itself often cited as the gold-standard state-level model.

**Penalties for breach.** An enrolled person who places a wager — in person or online — is subject to criminal trespass charges, and **all winnings are forfeited to the Compulsive Gambling Treatment Fund** (N.J.S.A. 5:12-189). Operators face significant regulatory penalties for knowingly permitting an excluded person to gamble, including DGE-administered fines and license sanction. DGE has been active in publicly reporting forfeitures and enforcement actions.

**2026 context.** A bill moving through the New Jersey legislature in May 2026 would prohibit online sportsbooks from sending promotional credits, bonus bets, or other incentives to customers who have activated any responsible-gaming tool with any operator or with the state — a proposed strengthening of the protective regime around self-excluded and responsible-gaming-active players.¹⁰ Cumulative DGE VSE enrollment is reported in the tens of thousands; DGE publishes the active list size periodically [precise 2026 enrollment count not regularly disclosed].
