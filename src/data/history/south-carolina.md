South Carolina has one of the most restrictive constitutional postures in the country. The 1895 Constitution at Article XVII § 7 banned lotteries and uniquely criminalized lottery advertising. The most dramatic chapter in state gambling history is the video poker era of 1986-2000, often called "the wild west of gambling." An interpretive opening in the gaming-device statute allowed video gaming machines to spread in the mid-1980s. By 1999 the state had more than 34,000 video poker machines operating with no statewide cap. Governor Jim Hodges and the General Assembly banned video poker effective July 1, 2000. Machines went dark statewide on that date.

Other legal gambling forms are limited. Charitable bingo was authorized for nonprofits. Voters approved a state lottery by constitutional amendment on November 7, 2000; the Education Lottery sold its first ticket on January 7, 2002. Net proceeds fund LIFE, HOPE, and Palmetto Fellows scholarships. Charitable raffles were authorized by 2014 constitutional amendment. "Cruises to nowhere" departed Little River and Charleston Harbor for international waters in the 1990s; the Day Cruise Gambling Act of 2005 substantially restricted the practice. The 1993 federal settlement with the Catawba Indian Nation uniquely barred the tribe from Class III gaming in South Carolina. The Catawba instead opened Two Kings Casino across the line in North Carolina in 2021. There is no legal sports betting and no commercial casino.
