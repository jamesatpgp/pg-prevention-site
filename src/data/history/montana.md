Montana's gaming culture grew out of the 1862 Bannack gold strike and the 1863 Virginia City rush. Faro, poker, and roulette ran openly in Butte, Anaconda, Helena, and Virginia City through the late nineteenth century. Indigenous nations practiced stick games, hand games, and horse racing on pre-contact networks; stick games are still played at intertribal celebrations today. Montana was unusual: territorial codes after 1864 licensed commercial gambling, and the 1889 Constitution barred only lotteries, not ordinary commercial games. Cities continued to license slot machines and card rooms into the early twentieth century. The narrowing came with the Montana Anti-Gambling Act of 1949 and the 1951 federal Johnson Act. Pari-mutuel horse racing had been authorized earlier, in 1929.

The lottery prohibition fell by voter initiative in November 1986; enabling legislation launched the Montana Lottery on June 24, 1987. Montana joined Powerball in 1990 as a founding state. The distinctive modern Montana model was built statute-by-statute through the 1980s and 1990s. Rather than concentrated casino floors, Montana licenses up to 20 video gambling machines per tavern or beer/wine licensee location, with $2 maximum wagers. Approximately 1,400 licensed locations operate statewide. Several federally recognized tribes operate Class III casinos under IGRA compacts. Sports betting arrived in May 2019 through HB 725, with the Montana Lottery as state monopoly operator. Kiosks launched in November 2019 and a mobile app followed, but mobile wagering is geofenced to licensed retailer premises.
