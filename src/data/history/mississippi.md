Mississippi's gambling identity was forged on the river. Antebellum riverboats carried legendary professional gamblers running faro, three-card monte, and poker between New Orleans and St. Louis; Vicksburg and Natchez-under-the-Hill ran open gambling districts. The 1835 Vicksburg lynchings — five professional gamblers hanged by vigilantes on July 6, 1835 — chilled but did not eliminate the trade. The 1869 and 1890 Mississippi Constitutions both expressly prohibited lotteries; the 1890 Constitution's anti-gambling clause was one of the strictest in the South for a century. The Gulf Coast ran open slot machines through the 1940s and 1950s — Biloxi and Gulfport were nicknamed the "Las Vegas of the South" — until federal Johnson Act enforcement after 1951 pushed slots underground.

The Mississippi Gaming Control Act, signed by Governor Ray Mabus on June 29, 1990, authorized dockside casino gaming on cruise vessels in approving Gulf Coast and Mississippi River counties. Mississippi's critical innovation was permitting dockside gaming without a cruising requirement. Isle of Capri Biloxi opened first, on August 1, 1992. Hurricane Katrina destroyed nearly every Gulf Coast casino in 2005; emergency legislation allowed casinos to rebuild on land within 800 feet of the high-water mark. The Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians opened the Silver Star Hotel & Casino in 1994 under a Class III IGRA compact. The Mississippi Lottery launched in November 2019 — the 44th state to authorize one. Retail sports betting launched in August 2018; mobile sports betting off-property remains illegal.
