# Texas — Gambling History

## Present-day posture

Texas in 2026 has one of the most restrictive commercial gambling regimes among large states. There are no commercial casinos, no statewide sports betting, no online casino, and no historical horse racing. The legal gambling market consists of the Texas Lottery (transferred to the Department of Licensing and Regulation effective September 1, 2025), charitable bingo and raffles administered by TDLR's Charitable Bingo Operations Division, pari-mutuel horse racing at Class 1 and Class 2-4 tracks regulated by the Texas Racing Commission (greyhound racing ended December 2019), and tribal Class II gaming on Kickapoo, Alabama-Coushatta, and Ysleta del Sur Pueblo lands under federal IGRA authority following the 2022 *Ysleta del Sur Pueblo v. Texas* decision. The 1876 constitution's anti-lottery clause (Art. III § 47) bars any new form of gambling absent a voter-ratified constitutional amendment.¹

## Compressed history

Gambling ran through every phase of Texas settlement. Spanish colonial card games (notably *monte*) were legal Crown-revenue activities; the Republic of Texas outlawed gambling in 1837 but enforcement was lax. Frontier-saloon faro and poker dominated the cattle-drive era; Galveston operated an open-city gambling culture run by the Maceo family from the 1920s until the Texas Rangers' 1957 raids ended it under Attorney General Will Wilson.² Pari-mutuel racing was legalized in 1933 as a Depression revenue measure, with Arlington Downs (built by oilman W. T. Waggoner) as the marquee track; the legislature repealed pari-mutuel betting in 1937 amid corruption concerns, and Arlington Downs was demolished. After 50 dry years the Texas Racing Act of 1986 was ratified by voters in November 1987, restoring pari-mutuel wagering effective 1988 — modern Class 1 tracks include Sam Houston Race Park, Lone Star Park at Grand Prairie, and Retama Park.³ Greyhound racing was legalized alongside; Gulf Greyhound Park at La Marque was the largest greyhound facility in the U.S. at its 1992 opening, and live greyhound racing in Texas ended with Gulf's final card on December 19, 2019.

The Texas Lottery was authorized by Proposition 11 on November 5, 1991 with roughly 64 percent support; Governor Ann Richards bought the first ticket on May 29, 1992, and sales crossed $1 billion within seven months. The 1980 Proposition 4 had separately authorized charitable bingo, implemented through the Bingo Enabling Act of 1981. Tribal gaming has had a uniquely litigious history: the 1987 Restoration Act for Ysleta del Sur Pueblo and Alabama-Coushatta contained "non-gaming clauses" the state used to shut Speaking Rock Casino in 2002 and enjoin Naskila. The 2022 U.S. Supreme Court decision in *Ysleta del Sur Pueblo v. Texas*, 596 U.S. 685, held 5-4 that the Restoration Act incorporated only Texas's *prohibitions* (not regulations) of gambling, freeing the tribes to expand electronic-bingo Class II operations.⁴ The Kickapoo Lucky Eagle Casino in Eagle Pass remains the state's largest gaming facility.⁵ A 2025 statute abolished the Texas Lottery Commission as a stand-alone agency effective September 1, 2025, transferring lottery and bingo regulation to TDLR.

## Regulatory framework today

The **Texas Racing Commission** (https://www.txrc.texas.gov/) regulates pari-mutuel horse racing under Tex. Occ. Code ch. 2025. The **Texas Lottery / Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation** (https://www.txlottery.org/ — transitioning to https://www.tdlr.texas.gov/) administers lottery and charitable bingo as of September 1, 2025. The **Texas Health and Human Services Commission, Behavioral Health Services** coordinates problem-gambling services in partnership with the **Texas Council on Problem Gambling** (NCPG affiliate), which operates the helpline 1-800-GAMBLER / 1-800-742-0443. Tribal casinos are regulated by tribal gaming commissions under federal IGRA oversight from the National Indian Gaming Commission.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Texas has **no statewide self-exclusion program** because Texas has no commercial casinos and no statewide sports betting. Each segment of the legal market runs its own narrow self-exclusion process:⁶

- **Pari-mutuel racetracks** — The Texas Racing Commission regulates voluntary exclusion under 16 Tex. Admin. Code Part 8. The Commission and Texas racetracks accept voluntary self-exclusion requests; each Class 1 track maintains its own list, and the racetrack association may also exclude or eject patrons under TRC rules with hearing-and-appeal procedures. Practical enrollment is conducted at the track security/customer-service office. There is no unified pari-mutuel exclusion list spanning all tracks. Durations are typically set by the licensee under TRC oversight.⁷

- **Tribal casinos** — Each tribal gaming operation maintains an independent VSE program. The **Kickapoo Lucky Eagle Casino** in Eagle Pass (Kickapoo Traditional Tribe of Texas), **Naskila Gaming** in Livingston / forthcoming Naskila Casino Resort in Leggett (Alabama-Coushatta Tribe), and **Speaking Rock Entertainment Center** in El Paso (Ysleta del Sur Pueblo) each operate their own tribal-gaming-commission-administered exclusion programs with distinct enrollment forms, durations (typically 1 year, 5 years, or lifetime), and breach penalties. Tribal exclusion lists are not shared across the three tribes and not shared with non-tribal venues.⁸

- **Charitable bingo** — There is no formal self-exclusion mechanism for charitable bingo halls; problem-gambling notice requirements apply but exclusion is conducted at the individual hall level.

- **Texas Lottery** — Beyond second-chance entry account closure, the lottery has no consumer-side VSE program because lottery products are sold over the counter at any of approximately 20,000 retail locations and exclusion is not operationally feasible.

The lack of a unified Texas registry means that a Texan seeking comprehensive self-exclusion typically must (a) self-exclude from each tribal property separately, (b) self-exclude at each racetrack visited, and (c) enroll in the National Council on Problem Gambling's National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP) at https://www.nvsep.org/ for online-wagering operators that have agreed to honor it. Texas does not participate in any multi-state compact. The Texas Council on Problem Gambling helpline (1-800-742-0443) provides referral to operators and to certified gambling counselors. If statewide sports betting or commercial casinos were authorized in a future constitutional amendment, a unified state VSE program would presumably be created — every legalization bill proposed in 2021-2025 has included such a provision.⁹
