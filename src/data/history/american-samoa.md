American Samoa came under U.S. sovereignty by the 1900 Deed of Cession (Tutuila and Aunu'u) and the 1904 Deed of Cession (Manu'a). The territory was under U.S. Naval administration through 1951 and civilian administration thereafter. Traditional Samoan governance — fa'amatai, the chiefly system — emphasizes communal land tenure and deference to matai authority, layered with a strongly Christian moral framework introduced by London Missionary Society missionaries beginning in the 1830s. The resulting cultural and religious matrix, predominantly Congregational, Catholic, and Methodist, has been the defining force in shaping a prohibitionist gambling policy.

American Samoa has the smallest legal-gaming footprint of any U.S. jurisdiction: no government lottery, no commercial casino, no pari-mutuel racing, no licensed sports wagering, and no slot or video-gambling devices. The only legal exception is occasional charitable bingo and raffles where proceeds support religious, educational, or charitable purposes. The modern prohibition sits at American Samoa Code Annotated § 46.4301 (criminalizing gambling and keeping gaming facilities) and § 46.4302, which carves the sole meaningful exception. That exception traces to Public Law 16-43 § 2 of 1979. A separate territorial poker-machine licensing scheme was held preempted by the federal Johnson Act in American Samoa Government v. Muavaefa'atasi. The 2018 Murphy v. NCAA ruling did not produce territorial sports-wagering legislation, and no bill has been enacted by the Fono authorizing a sports-wagering framework.
