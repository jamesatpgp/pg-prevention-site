# Iowa — Gambling History

Iowa in 2026 is one of the most casino-saturated states per capita in the country: nineteen commercial casinos (the former riverboats, now effectively land-based) plus three tribal Class III casinos, the racino Prairie Meadows (the first racino in U.S. history, 1995), the Iowa Lottery, pari-mutuel horse racing at Prairie Meadows, retail and mobile sports wagering since August 2019, charitable gaming, and authorized daily fantasy sports. The Iowa Racing and Gaming Commission (IRGC) regulates commercial casinos, racinos, and sports wagering; the Iowa Department of Inspections, Appeals & Licensing (DIAL) administers charitable gaming and oversees tribal compacts; the Iowa Lottery Authority operates the lottery; and Iowa Health and Human Services funds the Iowa Gambling Treatment Program. Iowa pioneered the riverboat-casino model in 1989 and the racino model in 1995.¹

The 1857 Iowa Constitution banned lotteries (Article III, §28), and the state held that line through Prohibition and the postwar decades. The 1882 alcohol-prohibition amendment (repealed 1933) and the same Methodist-Baptist reform movement that drove temperance shaped Iowa's anti-gambling posture; only county-fair pari-mutuel exceptions emerged through mid-century. Pari-mutuel wagering on horse and dog racing was authorized in 1983 (creating the Iowa Racing Commission); Prairie Meadows opened as a thoroughbred and quarter-horse track in 1989, Bluffs Run Greyhound Park (Council Bluffs) in 1986, and Dubuque Greyhound Park in 1985. The Iowa Lottery was authorized by 1984 constitutional amendment and began ticket sales Aug. 22, 1985, established under House File 2275 (1985) and Iowa Code Chapter 99G. Iowa pioneered the electronic pull-tab format. Greyhound racing declined steadily and was effectively ended by 2014 legislation that paid the tracks to retain casino operations in exchange for shutting down live dog racing — the last meets at Dubuque (2014) and Bluffs Run (2015).²

Iowa was the first state to legalize riverboat casinos. The Excursion Gambling Boat Act was signed in spring 1989 and took effect April 1, 1991; early licensees included the Dubuque Casino Belle, the President Riverboat Casino (Davenport), the Diamond Lady (Bettendorf), and the Mississippi Belle II (Clinton). Initial law strictly limited wagers to $5 maximum and losses to $200 per cruise, and required vessels to cruise — caps Iowa loosened in 1994 after early boats relocated to Illinois and Mississippi; the cruising requirement ended in 2004. The 1994 constitutional referendum authorized slots at the racetracks, and on April 1, 1995 Prairie Meadows became the first racino in the United States, with its distinctive nonprofit ownership structure (Polk County Racing Association) channeling revenue to Polk County, the City of Des Moines, and charitable distributions. Three IGRA-compacted tribes operate casinos: the Sac and Fox Tribe of the Mississippi in Iowa (Meskwaki Bingo and Casino, opened Dec. 31, 1992); the Omaha Tribe of Nebraska (Blackbird Bend Casino); and the Winnebago Tribe of Nebraska (WinnaVegas, Sloan). House File 617 (2019, signed May 13) authorized sports wagering with retail and mobile launching Aug. 15, 2019; initial in-person registration sunset Jan. 1, 2021. iGaming has been studied but not enacted as of 2026.³

## Regulatory framework today

The Iowa Racing and Gaming Commission (https://irgc.iowa.gov/) regulates commercial casinos, racinos, and sports wagering under Iowa Code Chapters 99D, 99F, and Chapter 99E sports-wagering provisions. The Iowa Department of Inspections, Appeals & Licensing (https://dial.iowa.gov/) administers charitable gaming under Iowa Code Chapter 99B and oversees tribal compact coordination. The Iowa Lottery Authority (https://ialottery.com/) operates the lottery under Iowa Code Chapter 99G. The Iowa Department of Health and Human Services funds the Iowa Gambling Treatment Program (1-800-BETS-OFF). Tribal casinos are regulated by their respective tribal gaming commissions and the National Indian Gaming Commission, with state-tribal coordination via the 2004 compacts.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

The Iowa Racing and Gaming Commission administers the **Statewide Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program** under Iowa Administrative Code 491-5.4(99F) and related rules. Iowa's program is one of the most comprehensive in the country in scope.

**Enrollment.** Enrollment is in person, by appointment, at any of three location types: (1) any state-licensed gambling facility (the 19 casinos or Prairie Meadows racino), with no appointment needed; (2) the IRGC office in Des Moines, by appointment scheduled by calling 515-281-7352 (M-F 8:00 a.m. - 4:30 p.m.); or (3) any Iowa Gambling Treatment Program (IGTP) provider, which expands access into clinical settings statewide. Enrollment is free. A government-issued photo ID is required.⁴

**Duration.** Five years or lifetime. Iowa does not offer a one-year or three-year option. Five-year enrollees are automatically removed at the end of the term and may re-enroll. Lifetime enrollment is irrevocable — there is no removal mechanism.

**Scope.** Iowa's SE program covers casinos, racetracks, retail and mobile sports wagering, and licensed daily fantasy sports operators — one of the broadest single-list jurisdictions in the U.S. (Iowa's licensed daily-fantasy framework dates to 2019.) All licensed entities are required to remove enrollees from marketing lists and to deactivate slot/player-club memberships and online accounts used for sports betting or fantasy. The Iowa Lottery operates a separate voluntary self-exclusion program for its iLottery online platform at https://ialottery.com/Pages/Legal/SelfExclusion.aspx; this is not consolidated with the IRGC list.

**Removal process.** Five-year enrollees are automatically removed after the term and need not re-enroll affirmatively to gamble. Lifetime enrollees cannot be removed (no waiver, no clinician affidavit pathway). The IRGC operates a formal revocation page at https://irgc.iowa.gov/self-exclusion-program/self-exclusion-revocation.

**Penalties for breach.** A self-excluded person who enters the gaming floor of a state-licensed casino or racetrack, or who places a sports/fantasy wager, may be removed and charged with criminal trespass under Iowa Code §716.7. All winnings are forfeited to the State; financial losses are not recoverable. A 2023 PlayIA review documented approximately 9,000 enrollees returning to Iowa casinos despite enrollment — an enforcement-data point that has prompted IRGC outreach to operators on facial-recognition and player-tracking compliance.⁵

**Multi-state participation.** Iowa is not a member of MSIGA (no online casino). Iowa does not formally share its SE list with other states; however, the three tribal casinos in Iowa coordinate informally with the state list under their compacts. Iowa's larger operators (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars) honor enrollments on other states' lists through their national identity-verification systems.

**State of the program in 2026.** Iowa's SE list has been a model studied in academic research, in part because the same statutory regime that mandates problem-gambling funding (a percentage of gross gaming revenue going to the IGTP) also requires licensees to underwrite SE enforcement. Iowa is one of the few states that funds prevalence studies on a recurring five-year basis through the IGTP and Iowa State University, producing seminal work testing Shaffer's adaptation hypothesis — the finding that population-level problem-gambling prevalence stabilizes or even declines after an initial expansion shock. The IRGC SE program page is https://irgc.iowa.gov/self-exclusion-program. Helpline: 1-800-BETS-OFF.⁶
