# Guam — Gambling History

## Current landscape

Guam today has one of the narrowest legal-gambling footprints among U.S. jurisdictions. Commercial casino gambling has been **rejected by territorial voters in every referendum it has reached**. There is **no government lottery**, **no commercial casino**, **no operating racetrack**, and **no licensed sports-wagering market**. The only legal forms are **charitable bingo and raffles** under narrow statutory exceptions, licensed through the **Department of Revenue and Taxation (DRT)** Regulatory Division. The **Guam Liquor Control Board / Alcoholic Beverage Control Board (ABC Board)**, housed within DRT, holds parallel authority over licensed premises and gaming-related enforcement at those venues.¹

## Compressed history

Guam was ceded to the United States by the Treaty of Paris (1898) and organized as an unincorporated territory. Chamorro cultural practice during the Spanish colonial period (1668–1898) included cockfighting (galleras), card play, and small-stakes village gambling tied to fiestas. U.S. naval administration (1898–1950) and post-war civil administration emphasized military priorities and adopted criminal-code prohibitions on commercial gambling that survive in modern Guam law at **Title 9, Chapter 64 of the Guam Code Annotated**.²

The defining feature of modern Guam gambling policy is decisive voter rejection of casino legalization. In **1977**, voters rejected by roughly three-quarters a Legislature-passed 1976 package authorizing a government-owned casino and slot machines in the transit lounge of Guam International Airport. **Casino legalization initiatives failed in 1996, 2004, and again in November 2012** ("Proposal A," rejected ~21,200 to 13,300 per the Guam Election Commission, with the Attorney General publicly noting procedural failures by the Election Commission). On **January 5, 2008**, voters rejected slot machines at greyhound racetracks by 63%.³ Opposition has historically united political and religious leadership — the Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Agaña under Archbishop Anthony Apuron, then-Delegate Madeleine Bordallo, and successive Governors and senators — giving Guam the longest unbroken record of voter rejection of casino gambling among U.S. jurisdictions where the question has reached the ballot.

**Guam Greyhound Park** in Tamuning operated for decades under Governor Ricardo Bordallo's 1977–78 executive orders authorizing Guam Gaming Commission greyhound racing rules but has been **closed** for years; the 2008 slots-at-the-track defeat ended the business model. **Cockfighting**, a Chamorro cultural practice long licensed by the Department of Agriculture, was preempted by the **2018 Farm Bill** (Pub. L. 115-334) effective December 2019.

## Regulatory framework today

The **Department of Revenue and Taxation (DRT) Regulatory Division** — https://www.guamtax.com — administers limited gaming licenses (bingo, raffles). The **Guam Liquor Control Board / ABC Board**, housed in DRT, regulates licensed premises and conducts enforcement at venues. Criminal prohibitions sit at **Title 9 GCA, Chapter 64** (https://law.justia.com/codes/guam/title-9/chapter-64/). Gaming-tax provisions sit at **11 GCA, Chapter 5** (https://guamcourts.gov/compileroflaws/GCA/11gca/11gc005.PDF). Behavioral-health services are provided through the **Guam Behavioral Health and Wellness Center (GBHWC)** within the **Department of Public Health and Social Services (DPHSS)** — https://gbhwc.guam.gov.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

**Administering agency:** Guam has **no formal, statutory voluntary self-exclusion program**. Because there is no commercial casino, no government lottery, no licensed sports book, and no operating racetrack, there is no licensed-gaming entity from which a player could be formally excluded, and no statute charges any agency with maintaining an exclusion registry. The **DRT Regulatory Division** and **Liquor Control Board / ABC Board** license charitable bingo and raffles but do not operate a player-exclusion list. Any player-exclusion practice at a licensed bingo or raffle event is operator-level discretion, not a regulated program.

**Where to enroll:** No territory-administered enrollment pathway exists. Residents seeking online-gambling self-exclusion typically use individual operator tools (where available) or third-party services such as the GAMSTOP equivalent for offshore sites. There is no Guam government portal for self-exclusion.

**Duration options:** Not applicable — no government program.

**Scope:** Not applicable.

**Removal process:** Not applicable.

**Penalties:** Not applicable.

**2026 program state:** No formal program. Behavioral-health support for gambling concerns is delivered as part of broader substance-use and mental-health services at the **Guam Behavioral Health and Wellness Center (GBHWC)** within DPHSS, alongside other addiction services. The **National Council on Problem Gambling** helpline (1-800-GAMBLER) remains available to Guam residents.⁴ Underground and illegal-game activity (poker rooms, internet-cafe-style machines) is a recurring Guam Police Department enforcement issue, and any expansion of formal responsible-gaming infrastructure would require a predicate change in the territory's prohibitionist legal posture.
