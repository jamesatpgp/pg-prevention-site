# Ohio — Gambling History

## The present

Ohio in 2026 has one of the broadest and most coordinated regulated gambling markets in the United States. Legal forms are: four commercial casinos (Cleveland, Toledo, Columbus, Cincinnati); seven racinos operating Video Lottery Terminals at the state's racetracks; the Ohio Lottery, including iLottery and Keno; pari-mutuel wagering on Thoroughbred and Standardbred (harness) racing; mobile and retail sports gaming (Type A skins, Type B retail sportsbooks, Type C kiosks at Class D liquor permit locations); fantasy contests; charitable bingo, instant bingo, raffles, and games of chance; and skill-based amusement machines. Ohio has no commercial iGaming as of 2026 (multiple iGaming bills have been introduced through 2024 and 2025). Regulation runs through a four-agency coalition — the Ohio Casino Control Commission (OCCC), the Ohio Lottery Commission, the Ohio State Racing Commission, and the Ohio Department of Mental Health and Addiction Services (OhioMHAS) — branded together as Ohio for Responsible Gambling (ORG). Statewide self-exclusion operates under the unified **Time Out Ohio** program, jointly administered by OCCC and the Lottery.¹ ² ³

## How it got here

The territory that became Ohio (statehood 1803) inherited Anglo-American common-law hostility to commercial gaming. Ohio's pivot point was 1851: the new Ohio Constitution ratified June 17, 1851 added Article XV, § 6 — "Lotteries, and the sale of lottery tickets, for any purpose whatever, shall forever be prohibited in this state" — a flat ban that stood unamended for 122 years.⁴ Through the late 19th and early 20th centuries Ohio Revised Code Title 29, Chapter 2915 criminalized gaming, lotteries, gambling devices, and bookmaking. Illegal gambling nevertheless flourished: Cleveland Syndicate and Mayfield Road Mob operations across the Ohio River in Northern Kentucky drew Ohio patrons; Cincinnati's West End numbers banks were among the largest in the Midwest; suburban Cuyahoga County hosted the Pettibone and Thomas clubs. The Kefauver Senate hearings (1950-1951) documented all of it.⁵

Modern legal gambling arrived in three waves. **Pari-mutuel racing** was first: the General Assembly enacted the pari-mutuel statute in 1933, creating the Ohio State Racing Commission. Beulah Park, Thistledown, River Downs, and Northfield Park anchored the Thoroughbred and Standardbred industries through the 20th century. **Lottery** followed: voters approved Issue 1 on May 8, 1973 (constitutional amendment), the Ohio Lottery Act passed later that year, and tickets went on sale August 22, 1974, making Ohio the seventh modern U.S. lottery state.⁶ The 1987 Issue 3 amendment dedicated all net Lottery proceeds to education; cumulative transfers to the Lottery Profits Education Fund exceeded $30 billion by FY2024.

**Casinos** required a third constitutional amendment. After four prior ballot defeats (1990, 1996, 2006, 2008), Issue 3 of November 3, 2009 narrowly passed (~53% to 47%), authorizing exactly four casinos in Cleveland, Columbus, Cincinnati, and Toledo. The General Assembly created the **Ohio Casino Control Commission (OCCC)** under R.C. Chapter 3772. Horseshoe Cleveland (now JACK) opened May 14, 2012; Toledo and Columbus followed in May and October 2012; Cincinnati (now Hard Rock) on March 4, 2013. In parallel, Governor Kasich and the Lottery Commission moved to install VLTs at the seven existing racetracks under Lottery authority — a separate constitutional vehicle later upheld in *State ex rel. Walgate v. Kasich*, 147 Ohio St.3d 1 (2016).⁷ The racinos rolled out 2012-2014: Scioto Downs, Thistledown, Miami Valley, Northfield Park, Belterra Park, Dayton, and Mahoning Valley.

**Sports gaming** came with House Bill 29 of 2021, signed December 22, 2021. HB 29 created R.C. Chapter 3775, placed sports gaming with the OCCC, and launched mobile and retail simultaneously at 12:00 a.m. on **January 1, 2023**. Sixteen mobile operators went live on day one. The statutory tax began at 10% but was raised to 20% effective July 1, 2023 under the FY2024-25 budget (Am. Sub. HB 33) — a doubling driven by concerns over advertising volume. First-year handle exceeded $7.6 billion.⁸ The OCCC has been active in enforcement, including a 2023 fine and six-month suspension of DraftKings for a Super Bowl "free bet" rule violation.

## Regulatory framework today

- **Ohio Casino Control Commission (OCCC)** — casinos, sports gaming (Type A and Type B), fantasy contests, **skill-based amusement machines**, and the Time Out Ohio voluntary exclusion program. https://casinocontrol.ohio.gov/
- **Ohio Lottery Commission** — Lottery games, iLottery, Keno, VLT racinos, **Type C sports gaming kiosks**, and the Lottery side of Time Out Ohio. https://www.ohiolottery.com/
- **Ohio State Racing Commission** — pari-mutuel horse racing, simulcast, ADW. https://racingohio.gov/
- **OhioMHAS** — administers the Problem Casino Gambling and Addictions Fund and the Problem Sports Gaming Fund; statewide treatment and the helpline. https://mha.ohio.gov/
- **Ohio Attorney General's Charitable Law Section** — charitable bingo, instant bingo, raffles, schemes/games of chance for veterans, fraternal, and charitable organizations. https://www.ohioattorneygeneral.gov/Business/Charity-and-Bingo
- **Ohio for Responsible Gambling (ORG)** — the four-agency coordinating coalition (OCCC + Lottery + Racing + OhioMHAS) — operates BeforeYouBet.org and the **Ohio Problem Gambling Helpline 1-800-589-9966** (24/7 call, text, chat).

## Voluntary self-exclusion — Time Out Ohio

Ohio's voluntary self-exclusion program is **Time Out Ohio** — legally still the **Ohio Voluntary Exclusion Program (VEP)** under R.C. § 3772.031 and Ohio Adm. Code Chapter 3772-12 — rebranded in 2022 to emphasize that the act of stepping back is voluntary and to expand uptake beyond the people who would respond to the word "exclusion." Time Out Ohio is operated as a **joint program of the Ohio Casino Control Commission and the Ohio Lottery Commission**, a partnership formalized in March 2019 when the two agencies merged their lists into a single enrollment. The 2023 expansion (with sports gaming integration) made it one of the broadest single-portal exclusion programs in the country.⁹ ¹⁰

**Administering agency.** Ohio Casino Control Commission (lead) jointly with Ohio Lottery Commission. The program email is TimeOut@Ohio.gov.

**Where to enroll.** Three pathways as of 2026:

1. **Online self-service** at https://timeoutohio.com/ via the Time Out Ohio Online Portal (https://timeout.ohio.gov/participants/start). Available 24/7/365. Requires a camera-ready device, a valid government-issued ID, and full Social Security number for automated identity verification.
2. **In person at any of the four Ohio casinos or seven Ohio racinos.** Commission staff at the facility help the applicant complete the online enrollment, with ID verification on the spot.
3. **By appointment at the OCCC Central Office in downtown Columbus** (100 Civic Center Drive). Walk-in service is available, and virtual identity verification by email appointment can be requested for applicants whose online identity verification fails.

If online identity verification returns a "PENDING" status, the applicant must complete identity verification either by scheduling a virtual appointment via TimeOut@Ohio.gov or by visiting any casino, racino, or the Central Office with a valid government ID.

**Duration options.** Three tiers — and the participant must serve the full minimum:

- **1 year** (minimum 12 months);
- **5 years** (minimum 60 months);
- **Lifetime** (no removal possible).

Time Out Ohio is a **minimum-term** program — meaning a person who selects 5 years cannot exit early, and at the end of a 1-year or 5-year term must affirmatively request removal (and have it approved) before returning to any covered venue. Removal is **not automatic**. Re-enrollment is unlimited; an existing enrollee may at any time upgrade to a longer term, but cannot downgrade until the existing minimum is served.

**Scope.** Time Out Ohio is one of the broadest scopes in the country, banning the participant from all four categories simultaneously:

1. The **four Ohio casinos** (JACK Cleveland, Hollywood Toledo, Hollywood Columbus, Hard Rock Cincinnati).
2. The **seven Ohio VLT facilities (racinos)** — Eldorado Scioto Downs, JACK Thistledown, Belterra Park, Hollywood Gaming at Dayton Raceway, Hollywood Gaming at Mahoning Valley Race Course, Miami Valley Gaming, and MGM Northfield Park / Bally's Northfield Park.
3. **All sports gaming facility sportsbooks** (Type B retail sportsbooks at the casinos, racinos, and approved professional sports venues).
4. **All sports gaming conducted by sports gaming proprietors** — meaning every Type A mobile platform (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars, Hard Rock, Bet365, ESPN BET, Fanatics, and others). Type C kiosks at Class D liquor permit locations are also covered through the Lottery's enforcement obligations.

**Fantasy contests** licensed under R.C. Chapter 3774 are administered by the OCCC's Fantasy Contests Division; participation in Time Out Ohio also blocks fantasy operator access for the major operators that hold OCCC licenses. **Skill-based amusement machines** (R.C. Chapter 2915.30 et seq.) are regulated by OCCC but are not bookable accounts; operators are nonetheless expected to honor the exclusion list.

The **Ohio Lottery's traditional retail draw and instant games** (Pick 3, Pick 4, Powerball, Mega Millions, scratch tickets, Keno) are **not** automatically blocked through Time Out Ohio at the point of retail purchase, because retailers do not check ID against the list. Time Out Ohio enrollment blocks iLottery account creation and shuts down existing iLottery accounts, and the Ohio Lottery maintains an additional internal self-exclusion list for VLT play at racinos that is now merged with Time Out Ohio enrollment.

**The relationship to a separate Lottery program.** Historically, the Ohio Lottery operated its own Voluntary Self-Exclusion process for VLT racino play and iLottery, separate from the OCCC casino list. **In March 2019** the two agencies consolidated into a single enrollment; the Lottery's standalone list was rolled into the unified program. The Lottery now refers all enrollees to the Time Out Ohio portal. There is no longer a separately enrollable "Lottery VSE" — a single application covers casinos, racinos, sports gaming (including the sports gaming Type C kiosk proxies the Lottery runs), and fantasy contests. People who enrolled in the pre-2019 VEP or who enrolled before sports gaming launched on January 1, 2023 can re-enroll in Time Out Ohio to add sports gaming to their existing ban, and the new term length supersedes the previous one.¹¹

**Penalties for breach.** A self-excluded person caught at a banned facility or trying to wager with a banned entity:

- forfeits all money and any thing of value converted or attempted to be converted into a wagering instrument (jackpots, TITO slot tickets, chips, sports-wagering winnings); the surrendered funds are deposited to **fund state problem gambling and addictions services**;
- is ejected from the premises and may be charged with **criminal trespass** under R.C. § 2911.21;
- forfeits any pre-existing points, complimentaries, and promotional credits at all OCCC-regulated venues and at any sports gaming proprietor account.

Banned operators may apply **corporate cross-state policy**, meaning a Time Out Ohio enrollee with a DraftKings, FanDuel, Caesars, BetMGM, or Bally's account anywhere in the United States can find that account frozen or closed nationwide — that is the operator's choice, not a state guarantee. Ohio expressly disclaims any guarantee of out-of-state enforcement.

**Confidentiality.** OCCC, the Lottery, and all banned entities/facilities must comply with confidentiality rules. The Commissions can release anonymized aggregate data to researchers studying program effectiveness.

**Employees.** Casino, racino, and sports gaming employees and contractors who must enter banned facilities to perform their job may enroll in Time Out Ohio and request limited access from the applicable Commission for work duties only.

**Multi-state participation.** Ohio does **not** belong to any formal multi-state self-exclusion compact. Cross-state operator corporate policies are not a substitute.

**Companion tools (free to Time Out Ohio enrollees).** Ohio became the first state to provide gambling-blocking software free of charge through a partnership with **Gamban**: Time Out Ohio enrollees can choose a 6-month to 5-year complimentary Gamban subscription that blocks gambling websites and apps on personal devices worldwide. The program also partners with **GamFin** (financial harm-reduction coaching) and **RecoverMe** (CBT-based recovery app).¹²

## Problem gambling response

Ohio's problem-gambling infrastructure is among the most developed in the United States. Funding is mature and dedicated:

- The **Problem Casino Gambling and Addictions Fund** (Ohio Const. art. XV, § 6(C)(3)(c)) receives 2% of casino gross revenue tax, administered by OhioMHAS.
- The **Problem Sports Gaming Fund** (R.C. § 5753.031) receives 2% of sports gaming tax proceeds — after the 2023 doubling of the sports gaming tax to 20%, the absolute dollar value of this fund doubled while keeping the same percentage.
- Lottery / VLT problem-gambling funding flows via OhioMHAS appropriations.

**Ohio for Responsible Gambling (ORG)** is the four-agency coalition (OCCC, Lottery, Racing Commission, OhioMHAS) and is regularly cited as a national best-practice model because it brings every gambling regulator and the lead behavioral-health agency to the same table. ORG operates **BeforeYouBet.org** and the **Ohio Problem Gambling Helpline 1-800-589-9966** (24/7 call/text/chat).

The **Problem Gambling Network of Ohio (PGNOhio)** is the state NCPG affiliate, funded primarily through OhioMHAS contracts. PGNOhio hosts the annual Ohio Problem Gambling Conference, coordinates the **Ohio Certified Gambling Counselor (OCGC)** and **Ohio Certified Gambling Prevention Specialist (OCGPS)** credentialing pathways in partnership with the Ohio Chemical Dependency Professionals Board (https://ocdp.ohio.gov/), operates the helpline back-end under OhioMHAS contract, and publishes prevention curricula for K-12, college, and adult populations.¹³

The **OCGPS credential — Ohio Certified Gambling Prevention Specialist** — is the state-recognized prevention specialist credential held by the owner of this archive. Ohio is one of a small number of states with a state-recognized gambling-specific prevention specialist credential alongside gambling-specific counselor credentialing.
