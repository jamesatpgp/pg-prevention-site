Pre-statehood Colorado (territory 1861, state 1876) was a classic frontier gambling jurisdiction. The Pike's Peak Gold Rush of 1858-1859 and the silver booms at Leadville, Aspen, Cripple Creek, and Black Hawk produced robust saloon-gambling culture. Cripple Creek alone reportedly had over 70 saloons at its 1900 peak. The 1876 Constitution prohibited lotteries, and a 1901 anti-gambling statute formally closed commercial gambling. Pari-mutuel wagering on horse and greyhound racing was authorized by constitutional amendment in 1948. Greyhound tracks operated through 2008; the legislature formally banned live greyhound racing in 2014 and simulcast wagering in 2023. Thoroughbred racing now runs only at Arapahoe Park in Aurora, which opened in 1992.

The Colorado Lottery was authorized by 1980 amendment; ticket sales began in 1983. Net proceeds are constitutionally dedicated principally to parks, recreation, and conservation through Great Outdoors Colorado. Commercial casino gaming arrived in three steps. Amendment 4 in November 1990 authorized "limited stakes gaming" with a $5 maximum bet in Central City, Black Hawk, and Cripple Creek; gaming began October 1, 1991. Amendment 50 in November 2008 raised the bet to $100, extended hours to 24/7, and added craps and roulette. Amendment 77 in November 2020 removed bet caps entirely. Tribal Class III gaming developed in parallel at Sky Ute Casino Resort and Ute Mountain Casino, both opened in 1992. Proposition DD in November 2019 authorized sports betting with revenue dedicated principally to the Colorado Water Plan. Statewide retail and mobile sports betting launched May 1, 2020.
