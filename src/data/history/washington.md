Washington was admitted as the 42nd state on November 11, 1889. The 1889 constitution prohibited the legislature from authorizing "any lottery or gift enterprise" — language amended over the 20th century to permit specific authorized forms. Frontier-era gambling ran openly through Skid Road in Seattle and Pacific Avenue in Tacoma. The first half of the 20th century saw Seattle's notorious "tolerance policy" of police-precinct payoffs in exchange for bingo, card-room, and tavern slot-machine toleration, exposed by 1969 King County grand jury investigations. Pari-mutuel wagering was legalized in 1933; Longacres Race Track in Renton opened that August and ran until 1992. Greyhound racing was preemptively banned in 1996 — no tracks were operating at the time.

The 1973 Gambling Act created the Washington State Gambling Commission, the country's second-oldest stand-alone gambling regulator after Nevada's. The 1982 legislature authorized a state lottery, with sales beginning in 1982-1983. Tribal Class III gaming developed quickly under IGRA, with the Lummi and Tulalip tribes opening the first casinos in 1992; 29 federally recognized tribes now operate casinos. House Bill 2638, signed by Governor Jay Inslee on March 25, 2020, authorized sports betting only at tribal casinos via amended Class III compacts, with no commercial-operator pathway. The first amended compacts took effect September 1, 2021. Commercial card-room operator Maverick Gaming sued challenging the tribal-only structure; the Ninth Circuit affirmed against Maverick in 2024.
