Alabama remains one of the most restrictive gambling jurisdictions in the United States, and the reason is the 1901 Alabama Constitution. Still in force and heavily amended, the 1901 Constitution at Article IV § 65 entrenches a comprehensive ban on lotteries and "any scheme in the nature of a lottery." Every modern attempt to replace it has failed at the ballot box. Governor Don Siegelman's October 1999 education-lottery referendum, modeled after Georgia's HOPE Scholarship, was rejected by voters. Slot-machine and casino bills have passed the House and died in the Senate as recently as 2024. Alabama has no state lottery, no commercial casinos, no pari-mutuel live racing, and no legal sports betting.

Tribal gaming is the only commercial gambling in Alabama. The Poarch Band of Creek Indians received federal recognition in August 1984 and opened the Creek Bingo Palace in Atmore on April 13, 1985 — one of the earliest tribal gaming facilities in the country after the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act. The Poarch Band operates three Wind Creek properties (Atmore, Wetumpka, and Montgomery), and all remain Class II only — electronic bingo and pull-tabs. Alabama has never entered a Class III tribal-state compact. Greyhound racing operated in four counties under local constitutional amendments beginning with Mobile County in 1972. All four tracks have since ended live racing: Greenetrack in 1996, VictoryLand in 2011, Mobile Greyhound Park in 2017, and Birmingham Race Course in 2020. Charitable bingo exists only by local constitutional amendment in specific counties.
