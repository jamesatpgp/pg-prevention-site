Maine separated from Massachusetts in 1820, and its first constitution banned lotteries — a restriction that lasted more than 150 years. The first legal opening came in 1924, when the legislature authorized pari-mutuel wagering at agricultural-society fairgrounds; Bangor's Bass Park had been running harness racing since 1893. The 1973 constitutional amendment finally lifted the lottery ban. The Maine State Lottery began operations on June 27, 1974, with Governor Kenneth Curtis drawing the first numbers. Charitable beano was legalized for nonprofits in 1975. Maine joined New Hampshire and Vermont in 1985 to launch the Tri-State Lottery, one of the earliest multi-state lottery cooperatives. Greyhound and thoroughbred racing never took root; harness racing remains the only live circuit.

The 1980 Maine Indian Claims Settlement Act resolved a federal land claim by the Penobscot, Passamaquoddy, and Maliseet Nations and subjected the tribes to most state laws. The Mi'kmaq Nation in Maine was federally recognized later, in 1991. This structural carve-out blocked the kind of IGRA tribal casinos that opened in Connecticut in 1992. Modern commercial casinos came through the ballot box. Voters approved slots at Bangor's harness track in November 2003; Hollywood Casino opened in 2005 with a permanent building in 2008. Voters approved a second casino in November 2010, and Oxford Casino opened in June 2012. The 2022 sports-wagering law granted mobile sports betting licenses exclusively to the four Wabanaki tribes. Mobile sports betting launched on November 3, 2023.
