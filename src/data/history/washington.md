# Washington — Gambling History

## Present-day posture

Washington in 2026 has one of the most distinctive gambling structures in the United States. The legal market consists of (1) approximately 45 limited-stakes commercial card rooms (house-banked "mini-casinos") regulated by the Washington State Gambling Commission (WSGC); (2) **29 federally recognized tribes operating Class III IGRA casinos** that account for roughly 80 percent of all legalized gambling revenue in the state; (3) Washington's Lottery (passive draw and instant only — no state-run VLTs); (4) charitable bingo, raffles, punchboards, and pull-tabs licensed by WSGC; and (5) sports wagering offered exclusively at tribal casinos that have amended their Class III compacts, with mobile wagering geo-fenced to tribal property. There is no commercial retail sportsbook, no statewide mobile sports betting, no online casino, no iLottery, and no live commercial pari-mutuel since Emerald Downs was acquired by the Muckleshoot Indian Tribe in 2015 (which continues to operate live thoroughbred racing). Daily fantasy sports is unlicensed and not offered to Washington residents by major operators.¹

## Compressed history

Washington Territory was organized in 1853 and admitted as the 42nd state on November 11, 1889. The 1889 constitution included Article II § 24 prohibiting the legislature from authorizing "any lottery or gift enterprise" — language amended over the 20th century to permit specific authorized forms. Frontier-era gambling ran openly through Skid Road in Seattle and Pacific Avenue in Tacoma; the first half of the 20th century saw Seattle's notorious "tolerance policy" of police-precinct payoffs in exchange for bingo, card-room, and tavern slot-machine toleration, exposed by the 1969 King County grand jury investigations.² Pari-mutuel wagering was legalized by HB 59 on March 3, 1933 in the depths of the Depression; Longacres Race Track in Renton opened August 3 of that year and ran continuously until Boeing redeveloped the site after 1992. Emerald Downs in Auburn opened June 20, 1996 and was acquired by the Muckleshoot Tribe in March 2015. Greyhound racing was preemptively banned in 1996 — no tracks were operating at the time.³

The 1973 Gambling Act — passed in the wake of the King County corruption disclosures — created the Washington State Gambling Commission, the country's second-oldest stand-alone gambling regulator after Nevada's, and authorized a comprehensive limited-charitable-and-commercial framework. The 1982 legislature authorized a state lottery in response to a $253 million budget shortfall, with sales beginning in 1982-1983.⁴ Tribal Class III gaming developed quickly under IGRA, with the Lummi and Tulalip tribes opening the first IGRA-compliant Class III casinos in 1992. The 2020 House Bill 2638, signed by Governor Jay Inslee on March 25, 2020, took a unique approach to sports betting: it authorized wagering **only at tribal casinos via amended Class III compacts**, with no commercial-operator pathway. The U.S. Interior Secretary approved the first nine amended compacts effective September 1, 2021. Commercial card-room operator Maverick Gaming sued challenging the tribal-only structure on equal-protection and Indian-Commerce-Clause grounds; the Ninth Circuit affirmed against Maverick in 2024 and a 2025 cert petition was denied.⁵

## Regulatory framework today

The **Washington State Gambling Commission** (https://wsgc.wa.gov/) — established by the 1973 Gambling Act — regulates non-tribal commercial card rooms, charitable bingo and raffles, punchboards and pull-tabs, charitable gaming licensing, and (via amended Class III compacts) tribal Class III gaming oversight. The **Washington Horse Racing Commission** (https://www.whrc.wa.gov/) regulates pari-mutuel horse racing. The **Washington's Lottery** (https://www.walottery.com/) operates draw and instant lottery games under RCW 67.70. The **Washington State Health Care Authority Division of Behavioral Health and Recovery** administers the Washington State Problem Gambling Program in partnership with the **Evergreen Council on Problem Gambling** (NCPG affiliate, helpline 1-800-547-6133).

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Washington has the **most expansive and structurally distinctive statewide self-exclusion program of any U.S. state**: the WSGC Statewide Self-Exclusion Program, launched May 2022 following passage of House Bill 1302 in 2019.⁶ A single online or paper form, submitted to the WSGC, enrolls the individual in a unified statewide list covering **all non-tribal house-banked commercial card rooms statewide** — roughly 45 facilities. This is the country's first state-administered cross-property exclusion list for non-tribal commercial card rooms.

The program's structurally distinctive feature is its **cross-tribal participation pathway**. Tribal casinos in Washington — unlike in essentially every other tribal-gaming-heavy state — may **opt into** the WSGC statewide list, meaning a single WSGC enrollment can functionally cover both the non-tribal commercial market and participating tribal casinos. As of 2026, tribal casinos are not yet fully participating in the WSGC statewide list (each of the 29 tribal gaming operations runs its own VSE program through its tribal gaming commission), but the legal infrastructure for unified coverage exists and the WSGC actively encourages tribal participation. This is unique nationally: Washington is the only state where a single state form can be honored by tribal casinos under a voluntary tribal opt-in.⁷

Enrollment is conducted through the WSGC online portal at https://wsgc.wa.gov/responsible-gaming-self-exclusion/self-exclusion-voluntarily-exclude-yourself-gambling-activities. The enrollee submits identifying information, a current photograph, and a copy of photo ID; the WSGC verifies and distributes the list to participating licensees within seven days. Duration options are **1 year, 3 years, 5 years, or lifetime** — Washington's range is broader than many states. Enrollees may also separately request inclusion on participating tribal casino exclusion lists by contacting each tribal gaming commission directly; the WSGC publishes guidance directing enrollees to do so.⁸

Removal at the end of a fixed-duration enrollment is automatic. Lifetime enrollees may petition the WSGC for reinstatement after a minimum period (typically not before 5 years), with documented counseling engagement and a hearing. An excluded individual caught gambling at a participating venue is subject to forfeiture of winnings, trespass charges under RCW 9A.52.080, and referral to problem-gambling services; the licensee that knowingly allowed the wager faces WSGC administrative sanction.

Sports wagering — available only at tribal casinos and only within tribal-property geo-fencing — is currently covered by each tribe's individual VSE program, not by the WSGC list, although a future regulatory amendment incorporating sports wagering into the statewide list has been proposed. Washington's Lottery products are not covered by the WSGC list (ID-verification at the 4,000+ retail locations is operationally impractical), but the Lottery operates its own retail-level responsible-gaming and account-closure tools for iLottery-adjacent functions. Washington does not participate in any multi-state self-exclusion compact; the Evergreen Council on Problem Gambling provides referral to operator-internal exclusion tools for online operators that Washington residents access via travel to legal states.⁹
