# Vermont — Gambling History

## Present-day posture

Vermont in 2026 operates a small but distinct gambling market: a state-run lottery dating to 1978, charitable raffles and bingo conducted by nonprofits under 13 V.S.A. § 2143, daily fantasy sports under separate statutory authority, and **online-only mobile sports wagering** that launched January 11, 2024 with three licensed operators — DraftKings, FanDuel, and Fanatics Sportsbook. There are no commercial casinos, no tribal gaming (Vermont has no federally recognized tribes, though four Abenaki bands are state-recognized), and no retail sportsbooks. The Department of Liquor and Lottery, with the State Lottery Commission as policy board, is the consolidated regulator for lottery, sports wagering, and (since 2018) liquor.¹

## Compressed history

Vermont declared independence in 1777 and was admitted as the 14th state on March 4, 1791. The early-statehood political culture, dominated by Congregationalist and Quaker settlements, was broadly hostile to commercial gambling; early statutes criminalized lotteries and common gambling houses without going as far as the explicit constitutional anti-lottery clauses adopted in Tennessee or Texas.² Pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing was legalized in 1961. Green Mountain Race Track in Pownal (Bennington County) opened in 1963 — Vermont's only commercial pari-mutuel facility — and in 1976 uniquely became the first U.S. track to host thoroughbred, standardbred, and greyhound racing in a single calendar year. Thoroughbreds ended that year, standardbreds in 1977, and Green Mountain operated as a dedicated greyhound facility until live racing ended in 1992. Vermont enacted a 1995 statutory prohibition on greyhound racing.³ The Green Mountain grandstand was gutted by fire on the night of September 16-17, 2020 and the property remains one of the most prominently abandoned racetracks in New England.

Vermont voters approved a statewide lottery referendum in 1976. Act 82 of 1977 created the Vermont Lottery Commission with a $250,000 start-up appropriation; the first Green Mountain Game ticket sold February 14, 1978 and the first scratch ticket (Scratch 'N Match) followed June 20. Lottery profits flowed to the General Fund through 1998 and to the Vermont Education Fund thereafter. Vermont participates in Powerball, Mega Millions, Lucky for Life, and the 1985 Tri-State Megabucks consortium with Maine and New Hampshire. The 2018 merger of the Lottery Commission into the Department of Liquor and Lottery consolidated regulatory functions.⁴ Sports betting was legalized by Act 63 (H.127) of 2023, signed by Governor Phil Scott on June 14, 2023. The DLL conducted a competitive procurement in December 2023, selecting DraftKings, FanDuel, and Fanatics — each paying above the 20 percent minimum revenue share (DraftKings and Fanatics at 31 percent, FanDuel at 33 percent). Mobile wagering went live at midnight January 11, 2024, timed to the NFL playoffs.⁵

## Regulatory framework today

The **Vermont Department of Liquor and Lottery** (https://liquorandlottery.vermont.gov/) regulates the Vermont Lottery and online sports wagering, with the State Lottery Commission as policy board. Charitable gaming is administered under 13 V.S.A. § 2143 with statutory prize caps and use-of-proceeds requirements rather than ongoing licensing. The **Vermont Department of Mental Health** and the **Vermont Department of Health** fund problem-gambling treatment and prevention; Act 63 of 2023 dedicated a percentage of sports-wagering revenue to the Substance Misuse Prevention Special Fund / problem-gambling treatment account, providing the most substantial recurring funding stream for PG services in state history. The Vermont Council on Problem Gambling (or its successor) operates the Vermont Problem Gambling Help Line (1-800-GAMBLER).

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Vermont's self-exclusion program is administered by the **Vermont Department of Liquor and Lottery**, covering the entire commercial sports-wagering market (the only commercial casino-style product in the state) and the lottery's iLottery / second-chance functions. Enrollment is conducted online through the DLL portal at https://liquorandlottery.vermont.gov/exclusion-list. The portal requires the enrollee to verify identity through an email address and cell phone (for a one-time authentication code), upload a selfie photograph, and submit a photo ID.⁶

Duration options are **1 year, 3 years, 5 years, or lifetime**. Scope of the statewide list: all three licensed mobile sportsbook operators (DraftKings, FanDuel, Fanatics) are required to honor the DLL exclusion list and to close any accounts of listed individuals, return remaining balances, and remove the enrollee from all marketing. Fantasy-sports licensees are honored separately — to self-exclude from fantasy sports activities in Vermont, the individual must visit each individual fantasy-sports licensee's website and opt-in to that operator's program, because Vermont's DLL fantasy-sports licensing framework places the VSE responsibility on the operator rather than on the state. This is a notable gap relative to states like Tennessee where the SWC list spans both sports betting and fantasy sports.⁷

Removal at the end of a fixed-duration enrollment is automatic; lifetime enrollees may petition the DLL for reinstatement under conditions set out in DLL administrative rules, but reinstatement requires documented engagement with a certified problem-gambling counselor and is rarely granted in the early years of the program. A person who attempts to wager while listed forfeits any remaining account balance and any winnings, and the licensee is required to report the breach to the DLL; licensees that knowingly accept wagers from listed individuals face penalties under Act 63 enforcement provisions. Lottery products sold through 600-plus retail locations are not covered by the VSE list — the practical absence of ID verification at point-of-sale makes that operationally impossible — though the DLL operates lottery responsible-gaming messaging and account-closure tools for online lottery purchases.⁸

Vermont does not participate in any formal multi-state self-exclusion compact; however, like Tennessee, DLL enrollment will typically trigger operator-internal restrictions in other states under operator policy because DraftKings, FanDuel, and Fanatics each maintain centralized account systems. The Vermont Council on Problem Gambling helpline (1-800-GAMBLER) and the Vermont Gambling Help portal (https://vtgamblinghelp.org/) provide referral and treatment-finding resources. Because Vermont has no casinos, no racetracks, and no tribal gaming, the DLL list functionally covers most legal Vermont commercial wagering activity — but it does not cover charitable bingo halls, which operate without a centralized exclusion mechanism.⁹
