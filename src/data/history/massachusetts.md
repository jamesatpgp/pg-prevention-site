Massachusetts has spent four centuries swinging between Puritan prohibition and pragmatic revenue. The Bay Colony's General Court ordered all "cards, dice, or tables" destroyed in 1630, but by the eighteenth century the legislature was chartering lotteries to fund Faneuil Hall, Harvard's Stoughton Hall, and Revolutionary War supplies. Reformers prevailed in the 1830s: private lotteries were banned in 1833 and outstanding grants repealed in 1834. The first modern opening came in 1934, when the legislature authorized pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing. Suffolk Downs opened on July 10, 1935, and hosted Seabiscuit's 1937 Massachusetts Handicap victory. Raffles were legalized in 1969, charitable beano in 1971, and the state lottery launched March 22, 1972.

Casino legalization came late and deliberately. The Expanded Gaming Act of 2011, signed by Governor Deval Patrick on November 22, 2011, authorized three Category 1 destination resorts and one Category 2 slots license. Plainridge Park opened in June 2015, MGM Springfield in August 2018, and Encore Boston Harbor in June 2019. The fourth license, for the southeast, was never issued — the Mashpee Wampanoag Tribe's federal land-in-trust ruling was rescinded in 2018, halting the planned First Light Casino in Taunton. Sports wagering came through Chapter 173 of 2022. Retail sports books opened at the three casinos on January 31, 2023, and mobile sports betting launched March 10, 2023. Greyhound racing was abolished by 2008 ballot question; Suffolk Downs ran its final card in 2019.
