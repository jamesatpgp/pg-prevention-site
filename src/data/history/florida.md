# Florida — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
Florida is a high-volume but constitutionally constrained gambling jurisdiction. Legal verticals: the **Florida Lottery** (Powerball, Mega Millions, scratch, daily numbers; administered by the Florida Lottery, an executive agency); **pari-mutuel horse racing and cardrooms** at the remaining licensed facilities under F.S. § 849.086 and chapter 550; **slot machines** at racinos in **Broward and Miami-Dade** counties (authorized by 2004 Amendment 4); **Seminole tribal gaming** under the 2021 compact (Hard Rock Tampa, Hard Rock Hollywood, additional Seminole properties); **Miccosukee Class II gaming** at the Tamiami Trail reservation; and **mobile sports betting** through **Hard Rock Bet** as the sole legal operator under the Seminole compact's hub-and-spoke model (relaunched December 7, 2023 after litigation). All Florida greyhound racing ended December 31, 2020 by Amendment 13. Jai-alai has wound down with last live performances in the mid-2020s. There is no licensed online casino. The **Florida Gaming Control Commission (FGCC)** — created by the 2021 reorganization — is the state's principal gaming regulator.[^1][^2]

## How Florida got here
Spanish colonial and territorial Florida (statehood March 3, 1845) had no significant pre-statehood regulated gambling industry. The 1885 Florida Constitution (Article III § 23) included an anti-lottery provision. The defining modern pivot came in **1931**: with the Depression-era economy collapsing, the Legislature **overrode Governor Doyle Carlton's veto** to pass the Pari-Mutuel Wagering Act, making Florida the **first U.S. state to legalize betting on both horse and dog races**. **Hialeah Park** thoroughbred racing began January 25, 1925 and was rebuilt to architectural prominence under Joseph Widener for January 14, 1932 (National Historic Landmark, 1979); Gulfstream Park opened February 1, 1939; Tampa Bay Downs opened in the mid-1920s. Florida's first dog track, **Hialeah Kennel Club**, had opened in 1922 — nine years before betting on dogs was legal — and after the 1931 statute the sector exploded: by 1935 Florida had 10 greyhound tracks, and at its peak the state hosted **11 of the 17 operational U.S. greyhound tracks**. **Amendment 13** (November 6, 2018, 69.06%) ended all Florida greyhound racing on **December 31, 2020** by constitutional deadline. **Jai-alai** was legalized in 1935 and operated at Miami, Tampa, Dania, Daytona Beach, Fort Pierce, Ocala, Orlando, and West Palm Beach frontons; the Miami Jai-Alai Fronton (opened 1926) was at one point the world's largest. Florida briefly authorized **pari-mutuel wagering on midget-car auto racing** in the late 1930s-early 1940s [statutory authorization unconfirmed].[^3][^4]

The lottery prohibition fell with **Amendment 5** (November 4, 1986); the **Florida Lottery** began ticket sales **January 12, 1988**, joined Powerball January 4, 2009 and Mega Millions May 2013. Net proceeds are constitutionally dedicated to education, principally the **Florida Bright Futures Scholarship Program** (created 1997). The Legislature first authorized **poker as a "penny-ante" side game** at pari-mutuel facilities in 1996 ($10 max pots, live-racing days only); limits expanded in 2003, 2007, and 2010 to no-limit Texas Hold'em throughout the day under F.S. § 849.086. **Amendment 4** (November 2, 2004) authorized slot machines at existing pari-mutuel facilities in Broward and Miami-Dade by county-option; slots launched at Gulfstream, Mardi Gras Gaming, Pompano Park, Calder, Magic City, and Hialeah between 2006 and 2009. Seminole tribal gaming opened with the first **tribal high-stakes bingo hall in the United States** at Hollywood in 1979 — the legal predicate for the 1987 *Cabazon* decision and Congress's 1988 IGRA — and progressed through Hard Rock Tampa (2004), the April 2010 Crist-Cypress Class III compact, and Governor DeSantis's May 2021 30-year compact authorizing craps, roulette, and a controversial **hub-and-spoke mobile sports betting framework** in which mobile wagers are deemed to occur on tribal servers regardless of bettor location.[^5][^6][^7]

Sports betting launched in November 2021, was enjoined within weeks by the U.S. District Court for D.C. (*West Flagler v. Haaland*), reinstated by the D.C. Circuit in June 2023, allowed to stand after Supreme Court denials of stay (October 2023) and certiorari (June 2024), and **Hard Rock Bet** relaunched to all Florida residents on **December 7, 2023** as the sole legal mobile sportsbook in the state under compact exclusivity. **Amendment 3** (November 2018, ~71%) requires citizen-initiative approval for further expansion of casino gambling beyond the existing pari-mutuel and tribal frameworks.[^7]

## Regulatory framework today
The **Florida Gaming Control Commission (FGCC)** ([flgaming.gov](https://flgaming.gov/) and [fgcc.fl.gov](https://fgcc.fl.gov/)) — established by 2021 legislation that consolidated regulatory authority from the former Division of Pari-Mutuel Wagering — regulates pari-mutuel wagering, cardrooms, slot facilities, and enforces gaming laws. The **Florida Lottery** ([floridalottery.com](https://floridalottery.com/about)) is a separate executive agency. **Seminole tribal gaming** operates under the **Seminole Gaming Commission** with federal NIGC oversight and the 2021 compact. **Miccosukee Class II** gaming operates under the **Miccosukee Gaming Commission**. **Charitable bingo** is governed by F.S. § 849.0931 (sheriff enforcement); **charitable raffles** by F.S. § 849.0935, with the **Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services** as the registrar. Problem-gambling treatment is delivered through the **Florida Council on Compulsive Gambling (FCCG)** ([gamblinghelp.org](https://gamblinghelp.org/)), the NCPG state affiliate, funded principally by Seminole tribal contributions and Florida Lottery responsible-gaming allocations; helpline **1-888-ADMIT-IT (1-888-236-4848)**.[^8]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
Florida operates one of the largest and most legally complex VSE programs in the United States — **administered by the FGCC under F.A.C. Rule 61D-14.019** with delivery support from the **Florida Council on Compulsive Gambling (FCCG)**. The program covers the state's pari-mutuel facilities, cardrooms, and slot facilities, with separate enrollment available for the Seminole tribal properties.

**Where to enroll**: the FGCC exclusion list and request information is at **[flgaming.gov/pmw/exclusion](https://flgaming.gov/pmw/exclusion/)**; the FCCG's self-exclusion application materials are at **[gamblinghelp.org/selfexclusion](https://gamblinghelp.org/selfexclusion/)**. Enrollment requires **in-person sign-up at a qualifying location** (an FGCC office, a Compulsive Gambling Counselor location, or a designated FCCG enrollment site), presentation of valid photo ID, a photograph and physical description taken on site, surrender of player-club membership cards and similar materials, and selection of the duration. A statewide downloadable application form is available at **[playersedge.org/assets/pdf/florida_self_exclusion_form.pdf](https://playersedge.org/assets/pdf/florida_self_exclusion_form.pdf)**.[^9][^10]

**Duration options**: registrants may choose **one year, five years, or lifetime** under the FGCC framework. The lifetime option is **permanent** and cannot be removed. (Some FCCG materials describe the range as "1 year to lifetime"; the underlying FAC rule supports the 1/5/lifetime tier structure.)[^9][^10]

**Scope**: the FGCC list covers **all licensed pari-mutuel wagering facilities, all cardrooms, and all slot machine facilities** operating under chapter 550 and 551, F.S., across Florida. The list does **not** automatically cover the **Seminole Tribe of Florida** gaming facilities (Hard Rock Tampa, Hard Rock Hollywood, etc.) — those operate under a separate **Seminole self-exclusion program** through the Seminole Gaming Commission. A patron seeking comprehensive exclusion must enroll separately on the FGCC list **and** the Seminole list. The FGCC list also does **not** automatically cover **Hard Rock Bet** (the mobile sportsbook), which is operated under the Seminole compact and administered through Seminole responsible-gaming infrastructure. The **Miccosukee Resort & Gaming** facility administers its own Class II exclusion list. **Florida Lottery retail purchases** are outside the FGCC list (a structural gap).[^9][^11]

**Removal**: at term expiration the registrant must affirmatively request removal; the list does **not** auto-expire and does **not** auto-renew. The **lifetime** selection is irrevocable.[^9]

**Penalties for breach**: a self-excluded patron who plays forfeits winnings, cannot recover losses, and may be subject to trespass under F.S. § 810.09. Licensees that knowingly allow play by self-excluded persons face FGCC administrative discipline; the FGCC issues regular Final Orders against operators and individuals for compulsive-gambling-program violations and exclusion violations (published at [flgaming.gov/pmw/exclusion/docs/](https://flgaming.gov/pmw/exclusion/) — a notable transparency feature).[^9][^11]

**Multi-state participation**: Florida is **not** a signatory to MSIGA (no licensed iGaming) and the FGCC list does not interoperate with other states' lists. The Seminole compact responsible-gaming program is delivered separately and is not part of any tribal multi-state self-exclusion database publicly. Exact 2026 FGCC enrollment counts are **[unconfirmed]** in publicly available reports; FGCC published Final Orders show enforcement activity but not aggregate enrollment. The state of the program in 2026 is **mature, in-person heavy, and split across FGCC and tribal lists** — a structural complexity that makes Florida self-exclusion practical reach less comprehensive than the simpler centralized models of Connecticut and Delaware.[^9][^11][^12]
