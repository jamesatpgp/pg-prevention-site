Gambling in what is now New Mexico is older than the United States. Spanish colonists brought monte and faro into Santa Fe. Doña Gertrudis "La Tules" Barceló's Burro Alley monte sala in Santa Fe is the era's most famous landmark. After the Mexican-American War the Territorial Assembly initially taxed and licensed gambling houses. Mining-camp faro and roulette in Elizabethtown, Cimarron, and Lake Valley flourished into the 1880s. Protestant reform pressure ended the licensing era; the Territorial Assembly criminalized public gambling in 1889. The 1911 state constitution, adopted with statehood in 1912, included Article IV § 38 restricting lotteries. Illegal slots and bookmaking nonetheless flourished along U.S. 66 from the 1930s through the 1950s.

Modern legalization came in waves. Pari-mutuel horse racing was authorized in 1947, with Ruidoso Downs opening the same year and later hosting the All American Futurity in 1959, still the world's richest Quarter Horse race. Charitable bingo was authorized by the Bingo and Raffle Act of 1981. Pueblo and Apache nations opened bingo halls in the late 1980s and added Class III devices in the early 1990s. The Compact Negotiation Act and Gaming Control Act of 1997 created the Gaming Control Board and authorized up to six racinos. Voters approved the Lottery in November 1995; the New Mexico Lottery sold its first ticket on April 27, 1996. The Pueblo of Santa Ana opened a sportsbook on October 16, 2018 — the first tribal sportsbook outside Nevada.
