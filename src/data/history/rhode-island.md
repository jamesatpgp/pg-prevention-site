Rhode Island's 1663 Royal Charter was unusually tolerant of gambling, and the colonial assembly chartered lotteries to fund Brown University in 1764, Trinity Church, and bridges. The 1843 Constitution then put up a uniquely Rhode Island procedural gate: lotteries are prohibited unless the General Assembly approves by two-thirds vote of each house AND voters ratify at statewide referendum. That gate has shaped every gambling expansion since. The General Assembly authorized pari-mutuel wagering on dog racing in 1934, and Narragansett Park (thoroughbred) opened that year in Pawtucket. Lincoln Greyhound Park opened in 1947. Charitable bingo was authorized in 1937, among the earliest in the country. Live greyhound racing ended in 2009 after voters approved Question 4 in November 2008.

The modern Lottery arrived through a November 1973 constitutional amendment; the first ticket sold May 30, 1974. Video lottery terminals at the pari-mutuel facilities were authorized in 1992, structured as Lottery-operated terminals to fit the constitutional exception. Table games were voter-approved at Twin River in November 2012 and launched in June 2013. Bally's Tiverton Casino Hotel opened September 1, 2018 after voters approved it in 2016. The General Assembly authorized sports wagering in June 2018; retail launched in November 2018 and mobile in September 2019. The 2023 iGaming law made Bally's the Lottery's exclusive online casino vendor; iGaming launched March 5, 2024 — the seventh U.S. state to do so. Every regulated commercial gambling form in Rhode Island now runs through the Lottery.
