# Connecticut — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
Connecticut is a permissive, tribal-anchored state with full online casino, online sports betting, and online lottery. Legal verticals: the **Connecticut State Lottery** (Powerball, Mega Millions, daily numbers, scratch, online iLottery; operated by the **Connecticut Lottery Corporation**, a quasi-public agency); two tribal Class III destination casinos — **Foxwoods Resort Casino** (Mashantucket Pequot Tribal Nation) and **Mohegan Sun** (Mohegan Tribe); **retail and online sports betting** since September-October 2021 (CT Lottery, Mashantucket Pequot, and Mohegan as the three master wagering licensees, partnered with Fanatics/Rush Street, DraftKings, and FanDuel respectively); **online casino (iGaming)** since October 2021; **daily fantasy sports**; and **charitable bingo, raffles, and sealed-ticket pull-tabs**. There is no commercial non-tribal casino. Live greyhound racing was prohibited by P.A. 17-209 in 2017; jai-alai has wound down with the last fronton closures in the mid-1990s through 2001. The **Connecticut Department of Consumer Protection — Gaming Division** is the principal civilian regulator.[^1][^2]

## How Connecticut got here
Colonial Connecticut combined Puritan anti-gambling sentiment with pragmatic exceptions — the 1728 "Act for Preventing and Suppressing of Lotteries" (reiterated 1750 and 1773) was paired with public-purpose lottery grants such as the May 1747 license to **Yale College**. The 1778 "Act for the Preventing of Horse-Racing" provided for confiscation of racing horses and fines on bettors. The 1818 state constitution contained no explicit gambling prohibition, but criminal anti-gambling statutes carried forward through the post-Louisiana-Lottery reform wave. Connecticut remained one of the more restrictive northeastern states until the 1971-1972 expansion.[^3]

**Public Act 71-865** (signed by Governor Thomas Meskill in 1971) was the modern pivot, authorizing pari-mutuel wagering, the state lottery, off-track betting, and greyhound racing and jai-alai by subsequent regulation. The Connecticut State Lottery sold its first ticket on February 15, 1972. Greyhound racing operated at Plainfield Greyhound Park (1976-2005) and Shoreline Star (Bridgeport, 1995-2005); jai-alai operated at Hartford Jai-Alai (1976-mid-1990s), Bridgeport Jai-Alai (1976-1995, converted to greyhound), and Milford Jai-Alai (1977-2001). Tribal gaming arrived with federal recognition of the Mashantucket Pequot in 1983 (PL 98-134); the Mashantucket bingo hall opened in Ledyard in 1986; and **Foxwoods Resort Casino** opened February 15, 1992 — initially table games only, with slot machines added in January 1993 after Governor Lowell P. Weicker Jr.'s Memorandum of Understanding granting the tribe slot-machine exclusivity in exchange for **25% of gross slot revenue to the state** (rising to 30% if annual contributions fall below $100 million). The Mohegan Tribe received federal recognition in March 1994, signed a parallel compact in April 1994, and opened **Mohegan Sun** on October 12, 1996. Cumulative state slot-machine contributions from the two tribes exceeded $7 billion by the mid-2020s.[^3][^4]

The post-PASPA expansion came via **Public Act 21-23** (Substitute HB 6451), enacted May 25, 2021, authorizing retail and online sports wagering, online casino, online lottery, and DFS. The Act issued one master wagering license each to the Mashantucket Pequot (DraftKings mobile partner), the Mohegan Tribe (FanDuel), and the Connecticut Lottery Corporation (PlaySugarHouse, later Fanatics). Retail sports betting launched September 30, 2021; online sports betting soft-launched October 12 and fully launched October 19, 2021; online casino launched in October 2021.[^5]

## Regulatory framework today
The **Connecticut Department of Consumer Protection — Gaming Division** ([portal.ct.gov/dcp/gaming-division](https://portal.ct.gov/dcp/gaming-division)) is the principal regulator across all verticals, including charitable bingo and pari-mutuel licensing. The **Connecticut Lottery Corporation** ([ctlottery.org](https://www.ctlottery.org/)) is a quasi-public agency that operates the state lottery and one of the three master wagering licenses. The two tribal **Class III** casinos operate under each tribe's Tribal Gaming Authority with NIGC oversight and the 1993/1994 Memoranda of Understanding. Problem-gambling services are administered by the **Connecticut Department of Mental Health and Addiction Services — Problem Gambling Services** ([portal.ct.gov/dmhas/pgs](https://portal.ct.gov/dmhas/pgs)) and funded principally by the **Chronic Gamblers Treatment Rehabilitation Fund** (C.G.S. § 17a-713) capitalized by tribal slot-revenue contributions and Lottery responsible-gaming allocations. The **Connecticut Council on Problem Gambling** ([ccpg.org](https://ccpg.org/)) is the NCPG state affiliate, operating the **1-888-789-7777** helpline (established 1981).[^1][^6]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
Connecticut operates a centralized, statewide Voluntary Self-Exclusion (VSE) program administered by the **Department of Consumer Protection — Gaming Division**. The program was launched and rebuilt around the 2021 expansion, ahead of the October 2021 sports-betting/iGaming launch.

**Where to enroll**: the statewide portal is at **[portal.ct.gov/gaming/self-exclusion](https://portal.ct.gov/gaming/self-exclusion)** (also accessible at the shorthand URL **ct.gov/selfexclusion**). The portal accepts online enrollment after identity verification; alternatively, registrants may complete and submit a paper form to the Gaming Division. **Connecticut Council on Problem Gambling** ([ccpg.org/get-help/voluntary-self-exclusion](https://ccpg.org/get-help/voluntary-self-exclusion/)) provides counselor-assisted enrollment.[^7][^8]

**Duration options**: registrants choose **one year, five years, or lifetime**. The lifetime option is **permanent and irrevocable** — once enrolled, the registrant cannot be removed from the list.[^7]

**Scope**: the statewide list covers **online and mobile sports wagering, online casino, online lottery, fantasy contests, and the retail sports wagering facilities** operated under the three master wagering licenses (Winners, Bobby V's, and tribal sportsbooks). The list covers the platforms operated by **DraftKings (Mashantucket Pequot), FanDuel (Mohegan), and Rush Street/Fanatics (CT Lottery)**. The **Foxwoods and Mohegan Sun land-based casino floors** are also covered for self-excluded persons under tribal compact responsible-gaming provisions, though the operational mechanics are administered through each tribe's gaming authority in coordination with the statewide list.[^7]

**Removal**: after the **one-year** or **five-year** term elapses, the registrant must take affirmative steps to come off the list; expiration is **not automatic** and is **not auto-renewed**. The **lifetime** selection cannot be removed.[^7]

**Penalties for breach**: a self-excluded person who places a wager or gambles is **prohibited from collecting any winnings, recovering any losses, or accepting complimentary gifts or services** from any licensee or operator. Licensees that knowingly permit play by self-excluded persons face administrative discipline by the Gaming Division.[^7]

**Multi-state participation**: Connecticut is **not** currently a member of the Multi-State Internet Gaming Agreement (MSIGA — Connecticut online poker is small-volume; the major MSIGA members are NJ, NV, MI, PA, DE, WV). The Department's launch announcement in 2021 highlighted the statewide-portal model as a deliberate design choice — rather than per-licensee VSE — to make a single enrollment effective across the three master-wagering ecosystems. Exact 2026 enrollment counts are **[unconfirmed]** in publicly published Gaming Division reports; the program has been operating continuously since 2021 launch and is integrated with CCPG referral workflows. The 24/7 helpline **1-888-789-7777** is the entry point for counselor-assisted enrollment and follow-up.[^1][^7][^8]
