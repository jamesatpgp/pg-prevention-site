# South Dakota — Gambling History

## Present-day posture

South Dakota in 2026 operates one of the most expansive gambling markets in the Plains states. The Deadwood commercial casino district holds roughly two dozen licensees offering full table games, slot machines, and on-premises sports wagering; six tribal Class III casinos operate on reservations across the state; the South Dakota Lottery runs draw games, scratch tickets, and a statewide network of bar-located video lottery terminals (VLTs) that is the second-largest source of general fund revenue after sales tax; pari-mutuel racing survives in scaled-down form at the Fort Pierre fairgrounds and at four off-track betting outlets. Regulatory authority is split between the South Dakota Commission on Gaming (Deadwood and tribal compacts), the South Dakota Lottery (lottery and VLT), and the Commission on Gaming's racing division (pari-mutuel).¹

## Compressed history

Gambling in the Dakota Territory was a fixture of Black Hills frontier life — Deadwood's saloons ran open faro, poker, and roulette from the 1876 gold rush onward, and Wild Bill Hickok died holding aces and eights in Nuttall and Mann's No. 10 Saloon on August 2 of that year. Statehood in 1889 brought a constitutional anti-lottery clause (Art. III § 25), but enforcement was lax until 1947, when state authorities formally shuttered Deadwood's open gambling halls. Deadwood's economy then declined for four decades, with much of its 1879-rebuilt Main Street deteriorating badly. The catalytic event was the 1987 fire that destroyed the Syndicate Block, exposing the depth of the preservation crisis and energizing the Deadwood You Bet Committee. Voters approved limited gaming on November 8, 1988, and casinos opened November 1, 1989 — making South Dakota the second state, after Nevada, with state-licensed commercial casinos. Initial $5 bet limits rose to $100 in 2000 and $1,000 in 2012.²

Pari-mutuel racing peaked mid-century and collapsed by the early 1990s; the last commercial horse track closed in 1982 and the last greyhound tracks shortly after. The lottery launched September 30, 1987 after a 60-40 referendum the prior November. Video lottery — the country's first lottery-operated VLT network — began operating in October 1989, was briefly struck down by the state Supreme Court in 1994, and was reauthorized by constitutional amendment that November.³ Tribal Class III gaming followed quickly under IGRA, with the Flandreau Santee Sioux opening Royal River Casino in 1990 and five other tribes compacting through 1992. Sports wagering arrived through the same Deadwood-bound constitutional pathway — Amendment B (November 2020) permitted wagering only "within the city of Deadwood," and retail sportsbooks opened on September 9, 2021. Statewide mobile remained unauthorized as of 2026, though legislative resolutions to broaden the framework continue to advance.⁴

## Regulatory framework today

The South Dakota Commission on Gaming, housed in the Department of Revenue, regulates Deadwood casino licensees, sports wagering at Deadwood, tribal-state compacts, and the remaining pari-mutuel operations (https://dor.sd.gov/businesses/gaming/). The South Dakota Lottery, also under the Department of Revenue, regulates draw and instant lottery games and the statewide VLT network (https://lottery.sd.gov/). Charitable bingo, raffles, and pull-tabs are licensed under SDCL ch. 22-25, with distributors taxed at 5 percent of gross. The South Dakota Department of Social Services / Division of Behavioral Health funds problem-gambling treatment under SDCL ch. 22-25A (https://sdbehavioralhealth.gov/substance-use/problem-gambling).

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

South Dakota's self-exclusion program is administered by the **South Dakota Commission on Gaming** for Deadwood commercial casinos. The Commission overhauled the program in 2022-2023 to address the prior requirement that an enrollee physically enter a casino to sign up — a barrier widely criticized by problem-gambling advocates. Under the current process, an applicant downloads the form from the Commission or obtains it at any Deadwood player's club, completes it with a photo ID copy and current photograph, and submits it to the licensee (which has 24 hours to forward it to the Commission) or directly to the Commission office in Pierre. The Commission then disseminates the unified exclusion list to all licensed Deadwood operators.⁵

Enrollees choose from durations ranging from **six months to lifetime**. The list scope is limited to **Deadwood-licensed commercial casinos** — it does not automatically extend to tribal casinos (each of the six South Dakota tribal gaming operations runs its own separate VSE program through its tribal gaming commission), to the statewide VLT network (which is not subject to a unified state exclusion list — bars and taverns hosting VLTs are not configured for centralized ID checking), or to the South Dakota Lottery's draw games. Removal at the end of a fixed-duration enrollment is automatic; lifetime enrollees may petition the Commission for reinstatement but reinstatement is rarely granted. An excluded person caught gambling at a Deadwood casino is subject to trespass charges and forfeiture of winnings; licensees that knowingly allow an excluded person to gamble face disciplinary action under ARSD 20:18. South Dakota does not participate in any multi-state exclusion arrangement, though enrollment in the National Council on Problem Gambling's National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP) for online wagering is available separately to South Dakota residents who want broader coverage. The Commission published streamlined VSE rules and forms at https://dor.sd.gov/businesses/gaming/.⁶

For the VLT network specifically, the Lottery operates "voluntary loss limit" tools and a separate exclusion option at participating establishments, but the practical coverage of those tools is limited by the decentralized nature of the bar-and-tavern host environment. South Dakota Council on Problem Gambling operates the helpline 1-888-781-HELP (4357), which is required to appear on lottery tickets and on every VLT screen.
