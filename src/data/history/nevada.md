Nevada is the only U.S. state that has never legalized any form of state lottery — Article IV § 24 of the 1864 Constitution still bars it. The Comstock Lode strike in 1859 made mining-camp gambling instantaneous and ubiquitous. An 1869 statute legalized banking games under state and county licensing, making Nevada one of the first U.S. jurisdictions to fully license casino-style commercial gambling. Virginia City had over 100 licensed gaming venues by the 1870s. A Progressive-era prohibition in 1909 banned banking games. The decisive re-legalization came with Assembly Bill 98 of 1931, the "Wide Open Gambling Bill" introduced by Assemblyman Phil Tobin and signed by Governor Fred Balzar on March 19, 1931.

The mob era took shape after Bugsy Siegel's Flamingo opened on December 26, 1946. Kefauver Committee hearings in 1950-1951 forced reform. The 1955 Gaming Control Act created the Nevada Gaming Control Board, and the 1959 act added the Nevada Gaming Commission. The Black Book of excluded persons was created in 1960. Howard Hughes's 1966-1970 acquisition campaign forced corporate-ownership reform; the Corporate Gaming Act of 1969 let publicly traded companies hold gaming licenses. The modern mega-resort era began on November 22, 1989, when Steve Wynn's Mirage opened on the Strip. Nevada was the lone major exception to PASPA from 1992 to 2018 until Murphy v. NCAA opened sports betting nationwide. Online poker was authorized in 2011, and Ultimate Poker launched in 2013 as the first regulated U.S. online poker site.
