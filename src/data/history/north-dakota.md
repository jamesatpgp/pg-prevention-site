# North Dakota — Gambling History

## The present

North Dakota in 2026 has no commercial casinos and no statewide commercial sports betting, but it has one of the largest charitable-gaming sectors in the United States and a small, idiosyncratic Lottery. Legal forms include: tribal Class III casinos run by five federally recognized nations (Spirit Lake, MHA Nation, Standing Rock, Turtle Mountain, Sisseton-Wahpeton); on-premises tribal retail sports betting under 2021 compact amendments; charitable bingo, raffles, pull-tabs, twenty-one, poker, paddlewheels, sports-pool boards, and — driving most of the volume — electronic pull-tab devices; pari-mutuel horse racing and ADW; and the state Lottery, which uniquely sells only multistate draw games (no scratch tickets). Regulation is split among the **Attorney General** (Charitable Gaming Division and the Lottery), the **Racing Commission** (pari-mutuel), and **tribal gaming commissions**. There is no unified statewide self-exclusion list.¹ ²

## How it got here

The 1889 North Dakota Constitution barred lotteries (Article XIX, § 185) and frontier-progressive politics produced strong anti-vice statutes through the early 20th century, paralleling the state's alcohol prohibition (1889–1933). Despite the ban, illegal slot machines and punchboards proliferated through the 1930s–1950s.³ The Legislative Assembly authorized **charitable gaming** by 1976 constitutional amendment and 1977 implementing legislation (Laws of 1977, ch. 482; N.D. Cent. Code Chapter 53-06.1) — the start of what would become a multibillion-dollar charitable sector. Pari-mutuel horse racing came in 1987 (Laws of 1987, ch. 226; N.D. Cent. Code ch. 53-06.2), with the Racing Commission licensing tracks, simulcast, and (after 2001) advance-deposit wagering.

Tribal casinos opened in 1992–1993 after IGRA compacts: Spirit Lake (St. Michael), 4 Bears (MHA Nation, New Town), Prairie Knights (Standing Rock, Fort Yates), Sky Dancer (Turtle Mountain, Belcourt), and Dakota Magic (Sisseton-Wahpeton, Hankinson). Voters approved a state Lottery in November 2002; the North Dakota Lottery (within the AG's office) sold its first ticket March 25, 2004, offering only Powerball, Mega Millions, Lotto America, Lucky for Life, and 2by2 — no in-state scratch products.⁴

The 2017 legislative session **authorized electronic pull-tab devices** (Laws of 2017, ch. 359). The change radically transformed the charitable sector: e-pull-tabs now drive gross charitable gaming proceeds above **$2 billion annually**, placing North Dakota at or near the top of all states in per-capita charitable-gaming activity.⁵ Tribal sports betting was added by **2021 compact amendment**; Standing Rock opened the first sportsbook at Prairie Knights on November 1, 2021. Mobile sports betting is permitted only while the bettor is physically present on tribal land (geofenced). Statewide commercial sports-betting constitutional amendments have failed repeatedly in 2021, 2023, and 2025.

## Regulatory framework today

- **North Dakota Office of the Attorney General — Gaming and Charitable Gaming Divisions** — licenses charitable bingo, raffles, pull-tabs (paper and electronic), twenty-one, poker, paddlewheels, and sports-pool boards; publishes quarterly reports. https://attorneygeneral.nd.gov/licensing-and-gaming/gaming/
- **North Dakota Lottery (within the AG's office)** — administers the multistate draw games. https://www.lottery.nd.gov/
- **North Dakota Racing Commission** — pari-mutuel horse racing, simulcast, and ADW. https://www.nd.gov/racing/
- **Tribal gaming commissions** of the five compacted nations regulate Class III gaming on tribal land; NIGC publishes filings. https://www.nigc.gov/
- **NDHHS Behavioral Health Division** — compulsive gambling treatment and prevention. https://www.hhs.nd.gov/behavioral-health

## Voluntary self-exclusion

North Dakota has **no unified statewide self-exclusion program**. This is a meaningful gap in the responsible-gambling landscape and a direct consequence of the state's fragmented regulatory architecture: no single agency has authority over all forms of gambling, and the tribal-state compacts do not require operators to offer self-exclusion.⁶

**What does exist:**

- **Tribal casinos.** Each of the five tribes (Spirit Lake, MHA Nation, Standing Rock, Turtle Mountain, Sisseton-Wahpeton) operates its own internal exclusion process under tribal gaming commission rules. A person seeking to be banned from tribal casinos must contact each tribal casino separately. Terms are tribe-specific, typically one year, five years, or lifetime.
- **Lottery.** The North Dakota Lottery offers self-exclusion from its iLottery/online subscription channel; over-the-counter retail purchases cannot be effectively self-excluded because retailers do not check ID against a list.
- **Charitable gaming.** There is no mechanism to self-exclude from charitable bingo halls, alternative-site bars and restaurants offering e-pull-tabs, or paper pull-tab outlets — a particularly large coverage gap given the scale of the e-pull-tab sector (over $2B in annual gross proceeds).
- **Pari-mutuel ADW.** Individual ADW operators apply their own internal exclusion processes.

**Multi-state.** None.

**Help.** The state contracts (historically through Lutheran Social Services / Gamblers Choice) for statewide gambling helpline routing of the NCPG national line **1-800-522-4700** with North Dakota–local follow-up. The **NDHHS Behavioral Health Division** funds outpatient gambling-disorder treatment statewide under appropriations drawing on the State Lottery Act dedication (N.D. Cent. Code § 53-12.1-12) and the Charitable Gaming Tax Distribution Fund (§ 53-06.1-12). The **North Dakota Council on Problem Gambling** functions as the NCPG state affiliate.⁷

**2026 status.** Multiple advocacy groups, including the ND Council on Problem Gambling and the NCPG, have called for a statewide unified self-exclusion list as part of any future commercial sports-betting authorization. As of 2026 no legislation has advanced.

Source: NDHHS Behavioral Health Division and NCPG ND-state page (https://www.ncpgambling.org/) plus tribal gaming commission policies as documented in tribal regulations.

## Problem gambling response

The State Lottery Act and Charitable Gaming Act both contain dedicated funding mechanisms transferring portions of net proceeds to the **Compulsive Gambling Prevention and Treatment Fund** administered by NDHHS Behavioral Health Division. Statewide outpatient treatment is delivered through contracted clinicians. The North Dakota Council on Problem Gambling serves as NCPG affiliate. Per NCPG State of the States surveys, North Dakota's per-capita problem-gambling funding has grown with the e-pull-tab expansion but remains modest relative to gross gaming proceeds.
