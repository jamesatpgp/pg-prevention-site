# Kentucky — Gambling History

Kentucky in 2026 has no commercial casinos, but its gambling landscape is wide: pari-mutuel horse racing at Churchill Downs, Keeneland, Turfway Park, Ellis Park, Kentucky Downs, and The Red Mile; historical horse racing (HHR) machines — slot-like terminals — operating as a statutory extension of pari-mutuel wagering at roughly 43 parlors with around 2,486 machines and approximately $1.7 billion in handle in 2023; the Kentucky Lottery (since 1989) including online iLottery (since 2016); retail and mobile sports wagering since September 2023; comprehensive charitable gaming; and no IGRA tribal gaming (Kentucky has no federally recognized tribes with reservation land). The Kentucky Horse Racing and Gaming Corporation (KHRGC), created by 2024 consolidation, regulates racing, HHR, and sports wagering; the Kentucky Lottery Corporation operates the lottery; the Kentucky Department of Charitable Gaming licenses bingo, raffles, and pull-tabs.¹

Lexington raced horses on Race Street as early as 1789, three years before statehood. The bluegrass region's limestone soils and rainfall produced America's most important thoroughbred bloodlines, and the Kentucky Derby (1875) and Kentucky Oaks (1875) emerged at what became Churchill Downs as cultural institutions of equine wagering. Pari-mutuel wagering, invented in France, came into use at U.S. tracks in the 1870s-1880s; the 1891 Kentucky Constitution (Section 226) banned lotteries in the aftermath of the Louisiana Lottery Company scandal of 1868-1894, but the Court of Appeals upheld pari-mutuel betting on horse races as a permitted exception in early-20th-century litigation. The 1906 State Racing Commission (now KHRGC) formalized pari-mutuel as the exclusive legal form of race-track betting. Through the 1930s-1961, Newport and Covington — across the Ohio from Cincinnati — hosted open mob-run casinos (the "Sin City of the Midwest") that a 1961 reform campaign shut down.²

Voters approved a constitutional amendment authorizing a state lottery in 1988, and the Kentucky Lottery launched April 4, 1989. Net proceeds support KEES merit scholarships, College Access Program need-based grants, and the General Fund. Kentucky was an early iLottery adopter, launching online ticket sales in 2016. Charitable gaming was authorized by 1992 amendment, with bingo, charity game tickets/pull-tabs, raffles, and charity poker licensed by the Department of Charitable Gaming. Historical horse racing emerged in 2010 when the Kentucky Horse Racing Commission redefined "pari-mutuel" to include HHR — slot-style machines whose outcomes derive from anonymized historical race data. The Family Trust Foundation of Kentucky and others challenged the legality, and in 2020 the Kentucky Supreme Court ruled that at least one HHR product (Exacta Systems) was not true pari-mutuel wagering. The General Assembly responded in 2021 by amending the statutory definition of pari-mutuel to explicitly include HHR, putting the practice on firmer footing. Churchill Downs's Derby City Gaming (Louisville) opened 2018 with about 900 HHR machines; HHR parlors now operate statewide.³ Sports wagering — House Bill 551 (2023), signed by Gov. Andy Beshear — launched retail Sept. 7, 2023, and mobile Sept. 28, 2023, with major operators tethered to licensed tracks. iGaming and commercial casinos remain unauthorized.

## Regulatory framework today

The Kentucky Horse Racing and Gaming Corporation (https://khrc.ky.gov/) regulates pari-mutuel racing, historical horse racing, and sports wagering under KRS Chapter 230 and 810/811 KAR. The Kentucky Lottery Corporation (https://www.kylottery.com/) operates the lottery under KRS Chapter 154A. The Kentucky Department of Charitable Gaming, within the Public Protection Cabinet, licenses charitable gaming under KRS Chapter 238. The Cabinet for Health and Family Services, Department for Behavioral Health, Developmental and Intellectual Disabilities, administers the Problem Gambling Assistance Account created by the 2023 sports-wagering act.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Kentucky's voluntary self-exclusion architecture is distinctive: rather than a single statewide list administered by the regulator, **810 KAR 2:100** requires each sports-wagering licensee to establish its own self-exclusion policy, approved by the regulator, with the **KHRGC compiling and maintaining a comprehensive list** of all voluntarily self-excluded persons from all licensees, updated at least monthly and distributed to all licensees. This hybrid model — operator enrollment, state consolidation — is unusual.⁴

**Enrollment.** Persons may self-exclude from sports wagering at any licensed premises, online via a licensee-specific website or mobile app, or by phone with the operator. Each licensee must display a public notice describing the self-exclusion list's existence and the methods to self-identify (online, in person at retail, or by phone). The KHRGC publishes an online self-exclusion form for direct submission at https://khrc.ky.gov/Documents/OnlineSelfExclusion.pdf, allowing enrollment without the operator as intermediary. The KHRGC maintains the master list under 810 KAR 2:100.⁵

**Duration.** Kentucky's regulation does not fix uniform durations — durations are set by each licensee's KHRGC-approved policy, typically including one-year, five-year, and lifetime options. Operators apply the duration that the enrollee selects, and the KHRGC consolidates entries onto the master list.

**Scope.** The KHRGC-maintained list covers retail and mobile **sports wagering** with all Kentucky-licensed operators (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars, Fanatics, ESPN BET, bet365, and the track-tethered books). HHR-machine self-exclusion is administered separately at the individual licensed track/parlor under KHRGC oversight (no statewide consolidated HHR list exists comparable to a commercial-casino program). The Kentucky Lottery operates a separate iLottery account-level self-exclusion. Charitable gaming is not within the KHRGC list. There is no Kentucky online casino to compact.

**Removal process.** Each licensee's policy specifies removal procedures; the KHRGC's compiled list reflects the licensee-determined duration and end date. Lifetime-enrollment policies, where adopted, are not unilaterally revocable.

**Penalties for breach.** Self-exclusion information is confidential under KRS Chapter 230, with disclosure permitted only for enforcement of KAR Titles 809 and 810. An enrollee who places a wager with a Kentucky-licensed sportsbook forfeits winnings; licensees that knowingly accept wagers face administrative discipline by the KHRGC. KRS 230.215 (effective July 1, 2025) gives the KHRGC power to exclude undesirables and explicit jurisdiction over sports wagering and charitable gaming. The 2026 General Assembly's HB 904 further strengthened responsible-gaming provisions, including stronger requirements that the KHRGC maintain a comprehensive self-exclusion list for problem gamblers.⁶

**Multi-state participation.** Kentucky is not a member of MSIGA (no online casino). The KHRGC list is not formally reciprocal with neighboring state lists, but national operators apply enrollments on other state lists to a player's national identity record as a matter of internal compliance.

**State of the program in 2026.** Kentucky was a relatively late adopter of sports wagering, and the KHRGC's hybrid licensee-operator model is comparatively new — meaning consolidated enforcement data is still maturing. Birches Health analysis showed Kentucky problem-gambling helpline calls roughly quadrupling 2022-2024 in parallel with sports-wagering launch. The 2023 act and 2025-2026 legislative amendments significantly increased dedicated problem-gambling funding through the Problem Gambling Assistance Account, though per-capita allocation still trails Indiana, Ohio, West Virginia, and Mississippi. The Kentucky Council on Problem Gambling (KCPG) is the private nonprofit coordinating outreach with treatment providers.⁷
