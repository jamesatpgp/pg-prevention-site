# Colorado — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
Colorado is a permissive, multi-vertical state with full statewide mobile sports betting. Legal verticals: the **Colorado Lottery** (Powerball, Mega Millions, scratch, Lotto+; administered by the Colorado Department of Revenue); commercial **casino gaming** with no bet caps in **Central City, Black Hawk, and Cripple Creek** since 2020 (regulated by the **Colorado Department of Revenue, Division of Gaming** through the **Colorado Limited Gaming Control Commission**); **tribal Class III casinos** at Sky Ute (Southern Ute) and Ute Mountain (Ute Mountain Ute); **pari-mutuel horse racing** at Arapahoe Park; **retail and mobile sports betting** since May 1, 2020 (Proposition DD); and **daily fantasy sports** since 2016 (HB 16-1404). Live and simulcast greyhound racing are banned. There is no licensed online casino in Colorado.[^1][^2]

## How Colorado got here
Pre-statehood Colorado (territory 1861, state August 1, 1876) was a classic frontier gambling jurisdiction. The Pike's Peak Gold Rush of 1858-1859 and the silver booms at Leadville, Aspen, Cripple Creek, and Black Hawk produced saloon-gambling culture indistinguishable from California, Nevada, and Montana of the same era; Cripple Creek alone reportedly had over 70 saloons at its 1900 peak. The 1876 Constitution (Article XVIII § 2) prohibited lotteries, and the 1901 anti-gambling statute formally closed commercial gambling. **Pari-mutuel wagering on horse and greyhound racing** was authorized by constitutional amendment in 1948 (passing roughly 2-to-1). Greyhound tracks — Mile High Kennel Club (Commerce City), Cloverleaf (Loveland), Pueblo Greyhound Park, Rocky Mountain (Colorado Springs) — operated through 2008; the legislature formally banned live greyhound racing in 2014 (HB 14-1052) and simulcast wagering in 2023 (HB 23-1041). Thoroughbred racing operated at Centennial Race Track (Littleton, 1950-1983) and Arapahoe Park (Aurora, opened 1992).[^1][^3]

The **Colorado Lottery** was authorized by 1980 constitutional amendment with implementing 1982 legislation; ticket sales began in 1983, initially scratch-only; Powerball joined in 2001 and Mega Millions on May 16, 2010. Net proceeds are constitutionally dedicated principally to parks, recreation, and conservation through Great Outdoors Colorado (GOCO, 1992 Amendment 8). Commercial casino gaming arrived via **Amendment 4** (November 1990, 57.31%), which authorized "limited stakes gaming" with a $5 maximum bet modeled on Deadwood's 1989 South Dakota structure in Central City, Black Hawk, and Cripple Creek; gaming began October 1, 1991. **Amendment 50** (November 2008) raised the bet to $100, extended hours to 24/7, and added craps and roulette (implementation July 2, 2009). **Amendment 77** (November 2020) removed bet caps entirely once local voters approved — all three towns did so — yielding effectively unlimited-stakes commercial casino gaming. Tribal Class III gaming developed in parallel: **Sky Ute Casino Resort** (Southern Ute, Ignacio) opened 1992 as a Class II bingo hall and went Class III; **Ute Mountain Casino** (Towaoc) opened 1992.[^4][^5]

Sports betting arrived through **Proposition DD** (November 2019, narrowly approved ~51%), with revenue dedicated principally to the Colorado Water Plan. Statewide retail and mobile sports betting launched May 1, 2020, making Colorado one of the earliest post-*Murphy* states with full mobile-first authorization. The first year saw calls to the Colorado problem-gambling helpline rise 45%, prompting subsequent rulemaking (HB 24-1402 and related measures).[^6]

## Regulatory framework today
The **Colorado Department of Revenue, Division of Gaming** ([sbg.colorado.gov](https://sbg.colorado.gov/)) — operating under the seven-member **Colorado Limited Gaming Control Commission** — regulates casinos and sports betting. The **Colorado Lottery** ([coloradolottery.com](https://www.coloradolottery.com/)) is housed within the Department of Revenue. The **Division of Racing Events** (also DOR) regulates pari-mutuel horse racing. Charitable bingo and raffles are regulated by the **Colorado Secretary of State, Bingo-Raffle Licensing Program** ([sos.state.co.us/pubs/bingo_raffles](https://www.sos.state.co.us/pubs/bingo_raffles/)) under Title 24, Article 21, Part 6. Tribal Class III gaming operates under each tribe's gaming commission with NIGC oversight. Problem-gambling treatment is administered by the **Colorado Behavioral Health Administration** ([bha.colorado.gov](https://bha.colorado.gov/)) with funding from gaming-tax proceeds; the **Problem Gambling Coalition of Colorado (PGCC)** is the NCPG state affiliate.[^2][^7]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
Colorado operates a modern, regulator-centralized voluntary self-exclusion program — one of the most clearly structured in the United States — launched in **September 2023** by the **Colorado Division of Gaming**.

**Where to enroll**: enrollment is principally online via the Division's portal at **[betsmart.colorado.gov/self-exclusion](https://betsmart.colorado.gov/self-exclusion)** and on the Division of Gaming site at **[sbg.colorado.gov](https://sbg.colorado.gov/)**. The portal accepts the majority of sign-ups electronically; alternative methods include in-person visits to a Division of Gaming office (Golden or Central City field offices) or downloading the form, having it notarized, and mailing it to the Division. Before September 2023, Colorado's self-exclusion list had been administered by the **Problem Gambling Coalition of Colorado** as a private project; the Division of Gaming formally **took over administration in 2023**, transferring the list and consolidating regulatory authority.[^8]

**Duration options**: **one year, three years, or five years**. Colorado does **not** currently offer a lifetime option in published materials — a deliberate Division design choice intended to favor renewable, time-bounded exclusion with re-evaluation. The patron chooses the term at enrollment.[^8]

**Scope**: the Self-Exclusion list covers **all forms of Colorado-regulated gambling** — brick-and-mortar casinos in Black Hawk, Central City, and Cripple Creek (slots, table games, retail sportsbooks) plus all licensed retail and online/mobile sportsbooks operating in the state. The list does **not** automatically cover Colorado Lottery purchases at retail outlets (a structural gap noted by PGCC) and does **not** cover the two tribal casinos (Sky Ute and Ute Mountain), which administer their own property-level lists through their tribal gaming commissions.[^8][^9]

**Removal**: at term expiration the registrant must take affirmative steps to re-enter; the list does **not** auto-renew. There is no early-termination petition for the chosen term.

**Penalties for breach**: a self-excluded patron who places a wager forfeits winnings and is barred from recovering losses; the licensee that knowingly allows play faces administrative discipline by the Division of Gaming under licensee responsible-gaming rules adopted in HB 24-1402 and subsequent rulemaking.[^8]

**Multi-state participation**: Colorado is **not** a signatory to MSIGA (the Multi-State Internet Gaming Agreement) because Colorado has no licensed iGaming; the state does **not** publicly join the National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program at this writing. The Division of Gaming reported that as of September 2024 — the program's one-year anniversary — nearly 300 patrons had enrolled in the statewide Self-Exclusion list, with growth continuing into 2025-2026 as PSA campaigns and licensee in-product placement increased visibility. The state helpline is **1-800-522-4700** (the NCPG national line, used as Colorado's contact). PGCC ([cogamblerhelp.org](https://www.cogamblerhelp.org/self-exclusion)) provides navigation assistance and 24/7 referral support for prospective enrollees.[^8][^9][^10]
