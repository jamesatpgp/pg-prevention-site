# Mississippi — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

Mississippi operates roughly two dozen commercial casinos along the Gulf Coast (Biloxi/Gulfport/Bay St. Louis) and the Mississippi River (Tunica/Vicksburg/Natchez/Greenville), plus three tribal casinos run by the Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians at the Pearl River Resort and Bok Homa. The Mississippi Lottery — the 44th and one of the last in the nation — launched in 2019. Retail sports betting has been live since 2018, but **mobile sports betting remains illegal off-property**: wagers must be placed while physically present at a licensed casino. Pari-mutuel horse and dog racing have never been legalized in the state.¹

## A compressed history

Mississippi's gambling identity was forged on the river. Antebellum riverboats carried legendary "blackleg" professional gamblers running faro, three-card monte, and poker between New Orleans and St. Louis; Vicksburg and Natchez-under-the-Hill operated open gambling districts. The 1835 Vicksburg lynchings — five professional gamblers hanged by vigilantes on July 6, 1835 — chilled but did not eliminate the trade.² The 1869 and 1890 Mississippi Constitutions both expressly prohibited lotteries; Article IV § 98 of the 1890 Constitution remained one of the strictest anti-gambling provisions in the South for a century.

The Gulf Coast nonetheless ran open slot machines through the 1940s and 1950s — Biloxi and Gulfport were nicknamed "the Las Vegas of the South" — until Johnson Act enforcement after 1951 pushed slots underground.³

Legalization arrived in two stages. The Mississippi Gaming Control Act (House Bill 2 of the 1990 Regular Session, Miss. Code Ann. § 75-76-1 et seq.) was signed by Governor Ray Mabus on June 29, 1990 and authorized dockside casino gaming on cruise vessels and vessels in eligible Gulf Coast counties and Mississippi River counties that approved local-option referenda. **Mississippi's critical innovation was permitting dockside gaming without a cruising requirement** — a model that distinguished it from Iowa and Illinois and shaped the Midwestern/Southern casino industry. The Mississippi Gaming Commission was established as regulator. Isle of Capri Biloxi opened first, August 1, 1992. By the mid-1990s a dozen casinos ran along the coast and the river; Tunica County became a national case study in casino-driven rural economic transformation.

**Hurricane Katrina** on August 29, 2005 destroyed nearly every Gulf Coast casino. Emergency legislation (House Bill 45 of the 2005 First Extraordinary Session) repealed the dockside restriction, allowing casinos to rebuild within 800 feet of the mean high-water mark on land. The post-Katrina rebuild produced larger and more storm-resilient resorts, notably the rebuilt Beau Rivage (2006) and Hard Rock Biloxi (2007).

The Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians (federally recognized 1945, reorganized 1975) opened the Silver Star Hotel & Casino in Choctaw on July 1, 1994 under a Class III IGRA compact. Golden Moon was added in 2002 and the Bok Homa Casino in Sandersville in 2010.

Lottery legalization came 26 years after the 1992 constitutional repeal of the prohibition. The Alyce G. Clarke Mississippi Lottery Law (HB 967 of the 2018 Regular Session, codified at Miss. Code Ann. §§ 27-115-1 et seq.) was signed by Governor Phil Bryant on August 31, 2018. The first scratch ticket was sold November 25, 2019; draw games (Powerball, Mega Millions) launched January 30, 2020. Net proceeds through fiscal 2029 are dedicated to the State Highway Fund up to $80 million annually with the remainder to the Education Enhancement Fund; after 2029, all net proceeds flow to education.⁴

Mississippi was an **early sports-betting mover**: the 2017 fantasy-sports HB 967 had removed the explicit casino prohibition on sports wagering, and after *Murphy v. NCAA* (May 2018) the MGC promulgated rules quickly. The Beau Rivage and Gold Strike Tunica opened books August 1, 2018, making Mississippi the third state to launch post-PASPA retail betting. Multiple attempts at statewide mobile (HB 774 of 2022, HB 271 of 2023, HB 1989 of 2024, and successor bills in 2025–2026) have failed, opposed by casino operators worried about cannibalization.

Defunct or never-legalized: pari-mutuel horse and greyhound racing (multiple failed attempts in 1928, 1968, 1990, 2003); antebellum riverboat gambling (ended with the Civil War); pre-1990 illegal Gulf Coast slot machines.

## Regulatory framework today

The Mississippi Gaming Commission (https://www.msgamingcommission.com) licenses and regulates all commercial casinos, retail sports books, and charitable bingo under Miss. Code Ann. § 75-76-1 et seq. The Mississippi Lottery Corporation (https://www.mslottery.com) operates the state lottery under §§ 27-115-1 et seq. The Mississippi Band of Choctaw Indians' three casinos operate under federal IGRA oversight by the National Indian Gaming Commission (https://www.nigc.gov) with state coordination through the MGC. The Mississippi Department of Mental Health (https://www.dmh.ms.gov) administers state problem-gambling treatment under Miss. Code Ann. § 41-4-1 et seq., and the Mississippi Council on Problem and Compulsive Gambling (https://msgambler.org) operates the 1-888-777-9696 helpline.

## Voluntary self-exclusion

The Mississippi Gaming Commission operates a single statewide Self-Exclusion Program under MGC Regulation IV (4.4). Enrollment is **online only** through the MGC portal: applicants create an account, complete an intake questionnaire, and upload photos of government ID and of themselves. The MGC provides walk-in access to a computer at any of its offices for applicants without internet access.⁵

The exclusion **term floor is three (3) years** — Mississippi is unusual in not offering shorter durations. Enrollees may choose 3 years, longer fixed terms, or lifetime, but **lifetime exclusion is not available until the enrollee has already completed at least three years** on the list. Once the chosen term expires, the participant is automatically flagged expired in the MGC database; no application for removal is required, but individual casinos may take up to 10 days to update their on-property systems.

Scope is broad. Enrollment covers **every Mississippi licensed casino** — the gaming floor, retail sports book, restaurants, hotels, events, marketing lists, and check-cashing privileges — at all commercial properties on the Gulf Coast and the river. **Retail sports wagering at casinos is covered** because Mississippi sports books exist only as casino amenities; mobile sports betting is illegal statewide so there is no separate mobile exclusion. The MGC list does **not** cover the Mississippi Band of Choctaw's three tribal casinos (Silver Star, Golden Moon, Bok Homa) — those properties run independent tribal self-exclusion programs under Choctaw gaming authority — and does not cover Mississippi Lottery products, which use a separate Mississippi Lottery Corporation responsible-gaming framework.

Breach carries serious consequences: an excluded person on a casino property is trespassing under Miss. Code Ann. § 75-76-65, all winnings are forfeited to the MGC, and the casino is required to remove the individual and report the contact. The casino itself faces regulatory penalties if it knowingly permits an excluded person to gamble or extends marketing materials.⁶

Mississippi is **not** a participant in the National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP at nvsep.org) and, lacking legal mobile sports betting or online casino, has no interstate digital-platform footprint. Tribal casinos are also outside any MSIGA or NVSEP arrangement. The MGC publishes summary statistics intermittently; enrollment was reported in the low thousands cumulatively through the 2020s [precise current enrollment count unconfirmed].
