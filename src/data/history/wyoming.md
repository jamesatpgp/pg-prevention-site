# Wyoming — Gambling History

## Present-day posture

Wyoming in 2026 operates a small but diverse gambling market regulated principally by the Wyoming Gaming Commission (reconstituted from the former Pari-Mutuel Commission in 2020). The legal market includes (1) mobile statewide online sports betting (live since September 1, 2021); (2) live pari-mutuel horse racing at small county-fair-style tracks (Wyoming Downs in Evanston, Energy Downs in Gillette, Sweetwater Downs in Rock Springs); (3) Historic Horse Racing (HHR) terminals at off-track wagering facilities in Cheyenne, Laramie, Casper, Evanston, Gillette, and Rock Springs (since December 2013); (4) tribal Class III gaming at four casinos on the Wind River Indian Reservation (Northern Arapaho and Eastern Shoshone tribes); (5) the Wyoming Lottery (WyoLotto), which uniquely prohibits scratch-off instant tickets and video lottery terminals; and (6) narrowly defined charitable bingo, pull-tabs, raffles, and calcutta auction pools under Wyo. Stat. Ann. § 6-7-101 et seq.¹

## Compressed history

Wyoming Territory was organized in 1868 and admitted as the 44th state on July 10, 1890. Cheyenne in the late 1860s and early 1870s rivaled Dodge City and Deadwood for open-saloon gambling along the Union Pacific main line. The 1889 constitution (effective at statehood 1890) included Article III § 20: "The legislature shall not authorize any game of chance, lottery, or gift enterprise, under any pretense or for any purpose" — language nearly identical to Utah's, and dispositive of Wyoming gambling policy for 75 years.²

The 1965 constitutional amendment first permitted the legislature to authorize pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing. The Wyoming Pari-Mutuel Commission was created by HB 130 of 1967 and operated under that name until reconstituted as the Wyoming Gaming Commission in 2020. The 2013 General Session passed HB 25, redefining "simulcast" to include historic pari-mutuel events and effectively authorizing HHR terminals; HHR went live in December 2013, and the introduction of HHR coincided with revival of Wyoming's live thoroughbred and quarter-horse racing industry — by 2022 Wyoming hosted 50 days of live racing involving 3,388 starters across 464 races, with HHR-derived breeders' funds substantially subsidizing live purses.³ WyoLotto was authorized by 2013 legislation (signed by Governor Matt Mead on March 13); operations began July 1, 2013 with first ticket sales August 24, 2014, initially limited to Powerball and Mega Millions and adding the Cowboy Draw and Lucky for Life thereafter. Wyoming and North Dakota are uniquely the only U.S. lottery jurisdictions that prohibit instant scratch games. Lottery net proceeds distribute to counties and municipalities for local-government use, rather than to education.

Tribal gaming developed only after a decade-long federal-court fight: the Northern Arapaho Tribe sought a Class III compact under IGRA; Wyoming initially refused; in 2005 the state was compelled to permit the tribe to operate a casino on tribal lands, and Wind River Hotel and Casino opened in Riverton that year.⁴ The Eastern Shoshone subsequently negotiated a compact. Four tribal casinos now operate (Wind River, Little Wind, 789 Casino, Shoshone Rose). Sports betting was legalized by HB 133 in April 2021; the Wyoming Gaming Commission was assigned regulatory authority and the online market launched September 1, 2021 with DraftKings and BetMGM. The tax rate is 10 percent of online sports-wagering revenue, among the lowest in the country.⁵

## Regulatory framework today

The **Wyoming Gaming Commission** (https://gaming.wyo.gov/) is the consolidated regulator for online sports wagering, pari-mutuel horse racing (live and off-track), Historic Horse Racing (HHR) terminals, skill-based amusement games, and charitable gaming. Tribal Class III gaming is regulated by the Northern Arapaho Gaming Agency and Eastern Shoshone Gaming Commission under federal IGRA oversight, with state-tribal compact compliance verified by the Wyoming Gaming Commission. **WyoLotto** (https://wyolotto.com/), the Wyoming Lottery Corporation, operates draw lottery games. The **Wyoming Department of Health, Behavioral Health Division** funds problem-gambling treatment and prevention, with statutory transfers from sports-wagering tax revenue and from lottery proceeds. The **Wyoming Council on Problem Gambling** is the state-affiliated organization coordinating helpline access through 1-800-GAMBLER.⁶

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Wyoming has the distinctive feature of being one of the first U.S. states to **fully adopt and route its self-exclusion program through the National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP)**, the National Council on Problem Gambling's national online platform. This is unusual: most states operate a stand-alone state-administered list, with NVSEP available as an optional add-on. Wyoming instead made NVSEP the principal pathway for sports-wagering and pari-mutuel/HHR exclusion.⁷

The Wyoming Gaming Commission's self-exclusion program is described at https://gaming.wyo.gov/resources/responsible-gaming/self-exclusion. Players can enroll either:

1. **Online at NVSEP.org**, which exposes Wyoming enrollment to the same national platform used by an expanding group of state regulators and operators (including, as of 2026, jurisdictions in Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, and others), or
2. **Via direct operator-app self-exclusion** at any of the licensed Wyoming online sports-wagering operators (DraftKings, BetMGM, Caesars, FanDuel, Fanatics, etc.), which then propagates to the Commission and to the licensee-specific list.

Scope of Wyoming enrollment is broad: under the WGC framework, NVSEP enrollment covers **online sports betting, pari-mutuel wagering (including HHR — Wyoming law treats HHR as pari-mutuel because the prize payouts come from a wagering pool, not from a house bank), skill-based amusement games**, and online casino play (though Wyoming has not legalized online casinos as of 2026). HHR coverage is significant — HHR is a substantial component of the Wyoming gaming market, and many states with HHR (Kentucky, Virginia) do not include HHR in their unified VSE lists.⁸

Duration options through NVSEP are typically **1 year, 3 years, 5 years, or lifetime**, with online enrollment and online reinstatement processes — Wyoming's adoption of NVSEP was explicitly motivated by elimination of paperwork hassles that had previously deterred enrollment. Removal at the end of a fixed-duration enrollment is automatic; lifetime enrollees may request reinstatement through NVSEP's review process. The Northern Arapaho Tribe's compact framework also includes sports-betting self-exclusion at the Wind River Hotel and Casino retail sportsbook; **tribal casinos in Wyoming operate their own VSE programs separate from the WGC/NVSEP list**, although the tribal commissions coordinate informally on cross-referrals.⁹

Breach penalties: an individual who attempts to wager through an online operator or at an HHR facility while enrolled forfeits any deposit and winnings; the operator is required to identify and reject the attempted wager and report the breach. Licensees that knowingly accept wagers from listed individuals face administrative penalties under WGC regulations. WyoLotto draw lottery products are not covered by NVSEP exclusion (no ID-verification at retail), but WyoLotto includes responsible-gaming messaging on tickets.

Charitable bingo and pull-tabs are not covered by NVSEP; exclusion at qualified charitable operations is handled at the operator level. The Wyoming Council on Problem Gambling provides referral support via 1-800-GAMBLER and helps enrollees navigate both NVSEP and tribal-casino VSE processes.¹⁰
