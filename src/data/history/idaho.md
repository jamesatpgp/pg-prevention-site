# Idaho — Gambling History

Idaho's legal gambling in 2026 is narrow: the Idaho Lottery (1989), pari-mutuel horse racing under the Idaho State Police Racing Commission, charitable bingo and raffles, and tribal Class III gaming at four reservation casinos. There is no commercial casino, no racino, no historical horse racing, no commercial sports wagering, and no online casino. Article III, §20 of the Idaho Constitution — amended in 1992 to declare gambling "contrary to public policy" — limits legal forms to a state lottery, pari-mutuel horse racing, and charitable bingo/raffles, and any further expansion would require constitutional amendment.¹

Gambling arrived with the fur trade and the gold rushes of the 1860s — Pierce, Idaho City, Silver City, and Murray were home to faro banks, three-card monte, keno, chuck-a-luck, and poker — overlaid on Nez Perce, Coeur d'Alene, Shoshone-Bannock, and Kootenai stick-game and hand-game traditions that long predated contact. Idaho Territory (1863) and the new state (1890) had largely outlawed commercial gambling by 1899, but slot machines were widely tolerated in northern Idaho from 1947 to 1953 — "one-armed bandits" operated openly on the outskirts of Idaho Falls, Pocatello, and Garden City — until a federal crackdown and state legislation ended the practice and shaped a durable political consensus that mechanical gambling devices were corrupting.² Idaho legalized pari-mutuel betting on horse racing in 1963 through the Idaho Horse Racing Act; Les Bois Park in Boise opened in 1970 and was for decades the state's flagship thoroughbred and quarter-horse track, ceasing live racing in 2009. The state authorized advance-deposit wagering in 2003 and simulcast wagering in 2011, and briefly experimented with "historical horse racing" (HHR) terminals at Les Bois, Sandy Downs, and the Greyhound Park & Event Center in Post Falls under House Bill 220 (2013), only to repeal the authorization in 2015 (SB 1011) and see voters defeat reauthorization Proposition 1 in 2018 — outcomes shaped heavily by tribal political opposition.³

The modern Idaho Lottery was authorized by a citizen-initiated constitutional amendment in 1986, with ticket sales beginning in 1989; net proceeds support public schools and the permanent building fund.⁴ Casino-style gambling exists only on tribal lands under IGRA: the Coeur d'Alene Tribe opened a bingo hall in 1993 and the Coeur d'Alene Casino at Worley shortly after; in 1994 the Tribe launched the National Indian Lottery, the first multi-state Internet lottery, which ended in 1998 after federal litigation. The Nez Perce (compact approved Nov. 14, 1995), Kootenai, and Shoshone-Bannock (compact approved 2000) operate Clearwater River Casino, the Kootenai River Inn & Casino in Bonners Ferry, and Fort Hall Casino respectively. Idaho compacts limit Class III machines to bingo-based "tribal video gaming" devices rather than traditional slots. Idaho has not legalized commercial sports wagering: the 1992 amendment is widely understood to require a constitutional amendment first, and none has been placed on the ballot through 2026.⁵

## Regulatory framework today

The Idaho State Lottery Commission (https://www.idaholottery.com/) administers the lottery under Idaho Code Title 67, Chapter 74, and also oversees charitable bingo and raffles under Title 67, Chapter 77. The Idaho State Police Racing Commission (https://isp.idaho.gov/racing/) regulates pari-mutuel horse racing under Idaho Code Title 54, Chapter 25. The four federally recognized tribes — Coeur d'Alene, Kootenai, Nez Perce, Shoshone-Bannock — regulate their own gaming through tribal gaming commissions and the National Indian Gaming Commission (https://www.nigc.gov/) under federal oversight; they are represented collectively by the Idaho Indian Gaming Association.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Idaho has no statewide voluntary self-exclusion program. This is structurally unusual but consistent with Idaho's narrow legal-gambling footprint and its compact framework: no commercial casino exists to be excluded from; pari-mutuel handle is low and concentrated in simulcast; and the Idaho Lottery operates a retailer model with no player-account infrastructure of the type that supports enrollment.

**Tribal self-exclusion** is the operative VSE option in Idaho. Each of the four compacted tribes operates its own responsible-gaming and self-exclusion program at the property level, with no statutory uniformity and no statewide reciprocity. Coeur d'Alene Casino Resort Hotel (Worley) accepts in-person enrollment at the casino through the player-services desk and at the security office; durations historically include one-year and five-year options, with lifetime available on request. Clearwater River Casino (Nez Perce, near Lewiston), the Kootenai River Inn & Casino (Bonners Ferry), and Fort Hall Casino (Shoshone-Bannock, near Pocatello) operate analogous tribal exclusion lists. A Hawaii or Idaho resident self-excluded at one tribal property is not automatically excluded at the other three: there is no shared tribal database in Idaho comparable to the cross-tribal lists maintained in some Pacific Northwest states. Tribal self-exclusion is enforceable on tribal land under tribal law; consequences for breach are set by each tribe's gaming ordinance and typically include trespass, forfeiture of winnings, and potential prosecution in tribal court.

**Idaho Lottery responsible-play** programming relies on the lottery's "GameSense" partnership with the National Council on Problem Gambling, in-store responsible-gambling messaging, and retailer training. There is no lottery-administered self-exclusion list because the lottery sells through retailers rather than player accounts; iLottery has not been authorized in Idaho.

**Cross-border and digital tools.** Idaho residents seeking exclusion from online gambling — typically offshore sportsbooks, sweepstakes casinos, and daily fantasy sports — rely on third-party blocking software (Gamban, GamBlock, BetBlocker) and on the operator-side voluntary exclusion offered by DraftKings and FanDuel even outside states where they hold a license. Idaho does not participate in MSIGA (no internet gaming exists to compact). There is no Idaho-administered cross-tribal database; informal coordination among tribal gaming commissions does occur but is not codified.

There is no Idaho statute imposing civil or criminal penalties for breach of a state-level VSE list because none exists. Breach of a tribal exclusion is enforced on tribal land. The state of the program in 2026 is sparse: Idaho is among the U.S. states with the smallest formal voluntary-exclusion infrastructure, a function of the constitutional cap on legal gambling. Reform proposals to consolidate a statewide list under the Lottery or the Racing Commission have surfaced periodically but have not advanced.⁶

The Idaho Council on Problem Gambling (https://idahoproblemgambling.org/) is a private nonprofit that operates a state helpline and coordinates clinician referrals; the Idaho Department of Health and Welfare does not run a dedicated problem-gambling program at the level seen in states with commercial casinos. Statewide prevalence has not been studied with the rigor of states with commercial gaming (Iowa, Nevada, New Jersey), and per-capita problem-gambling funding remains among the lowest in the country.
