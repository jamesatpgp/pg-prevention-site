# Oregon — Gambling History

## The present

Oregon in 2026 has an idiosyncratic gambling market shaped by its early Lottery-as-everything posture. Legal forms are: the Oregon State Lottery (since 1985); ubiquitous video lottery terminals at OLCC-licensed bars and restaurants (the largest single revenue source for the Lottery); pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing through the Oregon Racing Commission with limited live meets and broad simulcast/ADW; nine tribal Class III casinos under IGRA compacts; charitable bingo and raffles regulated by the Oregon Department of Justice; statewide mobile sports betting operated by **DraftKings as the Lottery's sole vendor** under a multi-year contract (replacing the in-house "Scoreboard" platform in January 2022); and tribal retail and (some) mobile sportsbooks under compact amendments. Commercial casinos remain constitutionally prohibited (Art. XV § 4). iGaming has not been authorized.¹ ²

## How it got here

Oregon's 1859 constitution included a flat lottery prohibition at Article XV, § 4 — "Lotteries and the sale of lottery tickets, for any purpose whatever, are prohibited." That ban survived until the 1933 amendment authorizing pari-mutuel wagering on horse races and the 1976 amendment authorizing charitable bingo and raffles.³ Portland Meadows opened as Oregon's flagship Thoroughbred track in 1946 (and finally ceased live racing in 2019); Multnomah Greyhound Park (Fairview/Wood Village) opened greyhound racing in 1944 and ran until 2004 when live greyhound racing ended in the state.

The **November 1984 Measure 4** authorized the State Lottery; first scratch tickets sold April 25, 1985. Two distinctive Oregon Lottery products: **video lottery terminals at retail liquor licensees** (authorized 1991, rolled out from 1992 forward — now the largest revenue producer for the Lottery), and **Sports Action** (1989-2007), an NFL parlay-card game that was the only legal sports wagering in the United States outside Nevada during the PASPA era (28 U.S.C. §§ 3701-3704, repealed by *Murphy* in 2018). Oregon discontinued Sports Action in 2007 under NCAA pressure tied to NCAA tournament hosting. Oregon Lottery proceeds are dedicated by constitutional amendment to the Education Stability Fund, economic development, **parks and watershed protection** (Measure 76, 2010, 15% set-aside), **veterans' services** (Measure 96, 2016, 1.5%), and outdoor school (Measure 99, 2016).⁴

Nine federally recognized tribes opened Class III casinos under IGRA compacts beginning in 1992-1995: Cow Creek's Seven Feathers (1992 bingo, Class III 1995), Chinook Winds (Siletz, 1995), Spirit Mountain (Grand Ronde, 1995), Wildhorse (Umatilla, 1995), The Mill (Coquille, 1995), Kla-Mo-Ya (Klamath, 1997), Old Camp (Burns Paiute, 1998), Three Rivers (Coos/Lower Umpqua/Siuslaw, 2004), and Indian Head (Warm Springs, 2012 replacement for Kah-Nee-Ta).⁵

After *Murphy v. NCAA*, 138 S. Ct. 1461 (2018), the Oregon Lottery launched **Scoreboard** — its own mobile-only sports-betting platform powered by SBTech — on October 16, 2019, without new legislation, citing its 1985 statutory mandate. In January 2022 the Lottery transitioned to **DraftKings** as its replacement sole operator. The Lottery prohibits wagering on Oregon college teams. Tribal sportsbooks operate at several casinos under compact amendment.

## Regulatory framework today

- **Oregon State Lottery (administered by the Oregon Lottery Commission)** — Lottery games, VLTs at retail liquor licensees, **DraftKings mobile sports betting** (the only legal mobile sportsbook in Oregon), and the Lottery's player-account responsible-gaming tools. https://www.oregonlottery.org/
- **Oregon Racing Commission** — pari-mutuel horse racing, simulcast, ADW. https://www.oregon.gov/racing
- **Oregon Department of Justice, Charitable Activities Section** — bingo, raffles, "Monte Carlo" events. https://www.doj.state.or.us/charitable-activities/
- **Oregon Health Authority (OHA) — Problem Gambling Services** — state lead for treatment, prevention, helpline. https://www.oregon.gov/oha/HSD/AMH-PG/Pages/index.aspx
- **Tribal gaming commissions** of the nine federally recognized gaming tribes; NIGC publishes filings. https://www.nigc.gov/

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Oregon's self-exclusion landscape is **fragmented by operator and not unified statewide** — a meaningful contrast with neighboring Washington and most casino-heavy states.

**Oregon Lottery / DraftKings mobile sports betting and DraftKings iCasino-style products.** Because DraftKings is the Lottery's sole mobile sports-betting operator, self-exclusion happens **inside the DraftKings Oregon Lottery app** under Responsible Gaming tools. Players can:

- self-exclude entirely for **up to 5 years** (no lifetime option as of 2026 within the DraftKings Oregon product);
- set deposit limits, time limits, wager limits, and cooling-off "time outs" without enrolling in full self-exclusion.

Once enrolled, the player's DraftKings Oregon account is closed and they cannot create a new one for the chosen term. Removal at term end is not automatic.⁶

**Oregon Lottery VLTs and traditional Lottery products.** The Lottery does not maintain a comprehensive in-store self-exclusion list enforced at point-of-sale because retailers do not check ID against a list. The Lottery offers a **Voluntary Self-Exclusion** for Lottery-iLottery / Players Club accounts (online subscription channel), but VLT play at retailers cannot be reliably blocked by a state list.

**Tribal casinos.** Each of the nine gaming tribes operates its own internal exclusion program — Oregon does not regulate tribal casinos and so does not maintain a statewide tribal exclusion list. **There is no statewide list covering all tribal venues**; a person seeking exclusion must contact each tribal gaming commission separately. Some tribes coordinate informally.

**Multi-state.** Oregon does not participate in a formal multi-state compact. DraftKings as an operator may apply corporate cross-state exclusion to a self-excluded customer's accounts in other states — that is a corporate policy choice.

**Companion tools.** The Oregon Council on Problem Gambling Resources (OPGR) recommends **Gamban** and **BetBlocker** for users who want platform-agnostic device-level blocking covering legal, gray-market, and illegal operators.

**Help.** **1-877-MYLIMIT** (1-877-695-4648), 24/7 call/text/chat through OPGR.

**2026 status.** Multiple advocacy groups (OPGR, NCPG) have called for a unified statewide self-exclusion list as part of any future iGaming or legislative reauthorization of sports betting. As of 2026 none has advanced.

Source: Oregon Health Authority Problem Gambling Services; Oregon Council on Problem Gambling Resources (https://www.opgr.org/); DraftKings Oregon Lottery Responsible Gaming tools (https://www.oregonlottery.org/sports/).

## Problem gambling response

Oregon's dedicated problem-gambling funding mechanism is one of the oldest in the country: a statutory **1% set-aside of Oregon Lottery net proceeds for gambling-treatment services** (Or. Rev. Stat. § 461.549), in place since 1992. The Oregon Health Authority's Problem Gambling Services program coordinates a statewide network of outpatient and residential treatment providers — Oregon has long operated **one of the only state-funded residential gambling-treatment facilities** in the U.S. The OHA contracts with county behavioral-health authorities for prevention and with academic researchers (OHSU, Portland State) for evaluation. The **Oregon Council on Problem Gambling** functions as the NCPG state affiliate, operates the 1-877-MYLIMIT helpline, and runs OPGR.org outreach.⁷
