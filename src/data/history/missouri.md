# Missouri — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

Missouri operates thirteen commercial riverboat-style casinos along the Mississippi and Missouri Rivers — all functionally land-based and tethered to bodies of water — under a 1992-era statutory framework that has been progressively relaxed. The Missouri State Lottery runs draw and instant games (no iLottery as of 2026). Mobile and retail sports betting launched **December 1, 2025** following voter approval of Amendment 2 in November 2024. There are no tribal casinos and no operational pari-mutuel horse or greyhound racing. The Missouri Voluntary Exclusion Program — established in 1996 — was one of the earliest state-administered casino exclusion programs in the United States and remains a national reference point.¹

## A compressed history

St. Louis and Kansas City sit at the confluence of nineteenth-century river-gambling cultures. Mark Twain's *Life on the Mississippi* (1883) documented faro, poker, and three-card monte on St. Louis-bound steamers. The Drake Constitution of 1865 (Article XI § 9) banned legislative authorization of any lottery; the 1875 Constitution carried the prohibition forward at Article IV § 45 — a ban that held for 109 years.²

Tom Pendergast's Kansas City political machine (1925–1939) ran one of the most extensive open-gambling operations in the country, effectively franchising slot machines, dice rooms, and the numbers (policy) game to specific operators in exchange for political support; St. Louis had its own structures via Egan's Rats and the Cuckoo Gang. Federal indictments in 1939 and the 1951 Kefauver hearings curtailed but did not end the operations; the 1951 Johnson Act finally cleared most clubhouse slot machines.

Constitutional reform came in stages. Charitable bingo was authorized by amendment in 1980; the state lottery by amendment ratified November 6, 1984 (amending Article III § 39(d)), with the Missouri Lottery Commission established under Mo. Rev. Stat. § 313.200 et seq. The first lottery ticket sold January 20, 1986. In 1992 voters approved Proposition A, amending Article III § 39 to authorize "games of chance" on excursion gambling boats. The Missouri Gaming Commission was established by 1993 enabling legislation codified at Mo. Rev. Stat. Chapter 313.³

The original Missouri statute imposed three distinctive restrictions that were progressively relaxed: a **$500 loss limit per two-hour boarding session** (a uniquely Missouri feature, repealed by Proposition A in November 2008); a **cruising requirement** (repealed in stages in the early 2000s); and a **local-option referendum** requirement for each host city or county (still in force). The first riverboat casino — the Argosy in Riverside — opened June 21, 1994, followed by the President in St. Louis (May 1994), Station Casino St. Charles (1994), and Harrah's North Kansas City (1994). The 2008 ballot measure capped commercial licenses at thirteen.

Pari-mutuel racing was authorized by 1986 constitutional amendment but never sustained a commercial race meet; the Missouri Horse Racing Commission has been dormant since the mid-1990s. Greyhound racing was never legalized.

Sports betting was the long-running unfinished business. From 2018 through 2023, repeated legislative attempts failed over disputes about video lottery terminals. In November 2024 voters approved Amendment 2 by a 7,500-vote margin (50.05% yes), authorizing both retail and statewide mobile sports wagering and dedicating a portion of revenue to a Compulsive Gambling Prevention Fund and to K-12 education. The MGC promulgated rules through 2025 and **launched both retail and online sports betting at 12:00 a.m. on December 1, 2025** with six day-one mobile operators: DraftKings, BetMGM, FanDuel, Fanatics, Caesars, and bet365, with DraftKings and Circa Sports receiving the two "untethered" licenses not requiring casino partnership.⁴

Defunct: antebellum riverboat gambling (ended with the Civil War); Pendergast-era open gambling; the $500 loss limit (repealed 2008); the cruising requirement.

## Regulatory framework today

The Missouri Gaming Commission (https://www.mgc.dps.mo.gov), within the Department of Public Safety, licenses and regulates the thirteen commercial casinos, all sports wagering operators, and charitable bingo under Mo. Rev. Stat. Chapter 313. The Missouri Lottery Commission (https://www.molottery.com) operates the state lottery under §§ 313.200 et seq. The Missouri Department of Mental Health, Division of Behavioral Health (https://dmh.mo.gov/behavioral-health/problem-gambling) administers problem-gambling treatment funded through the Compulsive Gamblers Fund under § 313.842, which receives $1 per casino admission plus a 2024-Amendment-2 sports betting allocation. The 1-888-BETSOFF (1-888-238-7633) helpline is operated under DMH contract.

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Missouri's Voluntary Exclusion Program (sometimes labeled the Disassociated Persons Program) was **established in 1996 — among the very first state-administered casino self-exclusion programs in the United States** — and is administered by the Missouri Gaming Commission under MGC Regulation 11 CSR 45-17.⁵

The program offers a single duration: **lifetime exclusion**, with a one-time opportunity to apply for removal after **five years** on the list. There are no shorter-term options. Enrollment requires the applicant to appear in person at any of the MGC offices located inside each of the thirteen licensed Missouri casinos (during casino operating hours) or at the MGC's Jefferson City headquarters (standard business hours, Monday–Friday). Online application is also accepted through the MGC's Disassociated Persons Online Application portal. Applicants must present state or government-issued photo ID, must complete the application of their own free will, and must not be under the influence of alcohol, controlled substances, or prescription medication that impairs judgment.⁶

Scope is comprehensive across Missouri-regulated gambling. Enrollment covers all thirteen Missouri commercial casinos (gaming floors, sports books, hotels, restaurants, marketing lists, and check-cashing privileges) and — following the 2025 sports-betting launch — **all licensed Missouri mobile and retail sports wagering operators statewide**. The program also covers video gaming on the casino floor. The Missouri Lottery operates a **separate** Self-Exclusion Program (SEP) under Lottery administrative rules, accessible through 888betsoff.org, for individuals seeking to exclude from Missouri Lottery products including retail and digital subscription games.⁷

Breach is a misdemeanor offense and carries severe consequences. An enrolled individual who enters a Missouri casino or wagers on a Missouri sports book is trespassing under Mo. Rev. Stat. § 313.812.14; all winnings are forfeited to the Compulsive Gamblers Fund; and the casino or operator is required to remove the individual and may face regulatory action for failing to detect a known excluded person. Missouri has been particularly aggressive in enforcement, with hundreds of trespass charges filed against breachers over the program's history.

Missouri is **not** a participant in the National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP) at nvsep.org. The state does not currently offer online casino (iGaming), so there is no shared-liquidity online poker concern. A peer-reviewed 2009 follow-up study of Missouri self-excluders (Nelson et al., *Journal of Gambling Studies*, 2010) became one of the most-cited evaluations of casino self-exclusion in the published literature and helped shape national NCPG best-practice guidance.⁸ The MGC publishes the active and removed exclusion list size periodically; cumulative enrollment exceeded 25,000 individuals by the early 2020s [precise 2026 enrollment count unconfirmed].
