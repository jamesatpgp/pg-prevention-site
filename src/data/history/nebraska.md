# Nebraska — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

Nebraska runs a **racino-only** commercial casino model — slots, table games, and retail sports betting are authorized only inside licensed racetrack enclosures — with WarHorse Casino Lincoln, WarHorse Omaha (Horsemen's Park), Grand Island Casino (Fonner Park), Harrah's Columbus, and additional facilities in South Sioux City and Hastings now operating or in development. The Nebraska Lottery, three tribal Class II/III casinos (Lucky 77, Ohiya, and the Winnebago's WinnaVegas just across the Iowa line on tribal land), and live pari-mutuel horse racing at Fonner Park, Horsemen's Park, Lincoln Race Course, Columbus, and Atokad round out the regulated market. **Mobile sports betting remains illegal** — sports wagering is retail-only at racino casinos pending a constitutional amendment that has been on multiple ballot drafts but has not yet passed.¹

## A compressed history

Frontier-era Omaha, North Platte, and Sidney developed extensive saloon and gambling cultures along the Union Pacific corridor. The Pawnee, Ponca, Omaha, Otoe-Missouria, and Lakota peoples played hand games, plum-stone, and horse-race wagering long before statehood in 1867. The 1875 Nebraska Constitution at Article III § 24 barred any lottery authorization — a ban that persisted in part until 1992. Tom Dennison's Omaha political machine (c. 1903–1933) protected extensive illegal gambling in the city's Third Ward (the "Burnt District") in exchange for political support, ending after Dennison's 1933 federal indictment.²

The first significant legal-gambling expansion came in 1934 when voters approved pari-mutuel racing and bingo by constitutional amendment. The State Racing Commission was created in 1935. Ak-Sar-Ben (Omaha, 1920–1995), owned by the civic Knights of Ak-Sar-Ben organization, hosted the Cornhusker Handicap and was one of the most beloved Midwestern thoroughbred venues; the track closed in 1995 and the site became the University of Nebraska Omaha's Aksarben Village campus. Fonner Park (Grand Island, opened 1954) remains the flagship live thoroughbred and quarter-horse track. Horsemen's Park (Omaha, 1998), Lincoln Race Course (1989), Columbus, and Atokad complete the live circuit. Greyhound racing was never legalized in Nebraska.

The state lottery was authorized by Initiative 415 in November 1992, replacing the 1875 prohibition. LB 138 of 1991 and follow-up acts (codified at Neb. Rev. Stat. § 9-801 et seq.) established the State Lottery Division within the Department of Revenue; the first ticket sold September 11, 1993. Net proceeds are constitutionally allocated to the Education Innovation Fund (44.5%), the Nebraska Environmental Trust Fund (44.5%), the State Fair (10%), and the **Compulsive Gamblers Assistance Fund (1% statutory floor)**.³

**Commercial casino expansion** is the most recent and most consequential change. In November 2020 voters approved three companion ballot measures sponsored by "Keep the Money in Nebraska": **Initiative 429** (constitutional amendment authorizing games of chance at licensed racetrack enclosures), **Initiative 430** (statutory authorization), and **Initiative 431** (20% GGR tax). The Nebraska Racing and Gaming Commission was rebranded from the Racing Commission as regulator. Casinos opened in phases at existing tracks: WarHorse Lincoln (temporary opening September 24, 2022; permanent 2024), WarHorse Omaha at Horsemen's Park (2023), Grand Island Casino at Fonner Park (2022), Harrah's Columbus (under development), with additional facilities at South Sioux City and Hastings.⁴

Retail sports betting launched at WarHorse Lincoln in June 2023 under the NRGC's interpretation of Initiative 430's "any game of chance" language. Mobile sports betting requires a further constitutional amendment; the 2024 Initiative 411 effort failed to qualify for the ballot, and 2025's LR 20CA / LR 22CA legislative resolutions to refer mobile to voters have advanced but not finally passed as of May 2026.

Defunct or never-legalized: Ak-Sar-Ben (closed 1995); greyhound racing (never legalized); Tom Dennison-era Third Ward gambling.

## Regulatory framework today

The Nebraska Racing and Gaming Commission (https://nrgc.nebraska.gov), formerly the Racing Commission, licenses and regulates pari-mutuel horse racing, the new racino casino operations, and retail sports betting under Neb. Rev. Stat. § 2-1201 et seq. The Nebraska Lottery (https://nelottery.com), within the Department of Revenue, operates under § 9-801 et seq. The Department of Revenue Charitable Gaming Division (https://revenue.nebraska.gov/gaming) regulates bingo, pickle cards, raffles, and small lotteries under § 9-201 et seq. Tribal Class II and III gaming operates under federal NIGC oversight. The Nebraska Council on Problem Gambling (https://problemgambling.nebraska.gov) and DHHS Division of Behavioral Health (https://dhhs.ne.gov) administer problem-gambling treatment and the 1-833-238-6837 helpline under the **Compulsive Gamblers Assistance Fund** (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 9-1001).

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Nebraska made a major upgrade to its self-exclusion infrastructure in **December 2025**: the Nebraska Racing and Gaming Commission activated **idPair's National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP at nvsep.org)** for regulated gambling in Nebraska, effective immediately. Nebraska became the **21st NVSEP participating jurisdiction** and the first racino-model state to fully integrate with the multi-state framework.⁵

Enrollment is free and runs through the NVSEP digital portal at https://www.nvsep.org. The process is online-first: applicants create an account, complete an identity-verification step (uploading government-issued photo ID and biometric photo), select duration and scope, and the enrollment is propagated to all participating Nebraska operators and to other participating NVSEP jurisdictions. In-person enrollment is also available through the NRGC's office in Lincoln. NVSEP supports multiple duration options — typical NVSEP terms run from **6 months to 5 years**, with some jurisdictions offering lifetime — and Nebraska's NRGC implementation honors the standard NVSEP options.

Scope under the Nebraska NVSEP activation covers **all licensed Nebraska racino casinos** (WarHorse Lincoln, WarHorse Omaha, Grand Island Casino, Harrah's Columbus, and additional properties as they open), **all pari-mutuel wagering facilities** (Fonner Park, Horsemen's Park, Lincoln Race Course, Columbus, Atokad), **retail sports betting** at racino sportsbooks, and various daily fantasy sports platforms participating in NVSEP. The WarHorse Sportsbook House Rules (revised March 19, 2026) explicitly prohibit any payment to a person currently on the Nebraska Self-Exclusion List.⁶ Because Nebraska does not yet have legal mobile sports betting or online casino, the NVSEP enrollment automatically covers the entire regulated commercial gaming market.

The state list does **not** cover the three tribal casinos (Lucky 77 in Walthill, Ohiya in Niobrara, and WinnaVegas on tribal land in Sloan, Iowa), which run their own tribal voluntary exclusion programs under their compacts. The Nebraska Lottery operates a **separate** responsible-gaming and self-exclusion framework for its retailer-network products.

Removal is handled through NVSEP at term expiration: short-term enrollments lapse automatically; lifetime enrollments are irrevocable. Breach of the Nebraska list triggers winnings forfeiture (deposited into the Compulsive Gamblers Assistance Fund), removal from the premises, and potential criminal trespass charges; operators are subject to regulatory sanction for failing to enforce.

Through NVSEP, Nebraska enrollees who travel to other participating NVSEP jurisdictions — currently 21 states including Colorado, Iowa, Michigan, Tennessee, Wyoming, and others — are automatically excluded from participating operators in those states as well, a substantial benefit for a border state with significant cross-state gambling traffic.⁷ Nebraska is not part of MSIGA (no online poker).
