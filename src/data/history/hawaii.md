# Hawaii — Gambling History

Hawaii is one of only two U.S. states — Utah being the other — that has authorized no form of state-regulated commercial gambling. As of 2026 there is no state lottery, no commercial or tribal casino (Hawaii has no federally recognized tribes), no pari-mutuel racing, no sports wagering, no daily fantasy sports authorization, no online casino, and no charitable bingo or raffle authorization beyond a narrow "social gambling" affirmative defense. Hawaii has no gaming regulator. Gambling offenses are codified in Hawaii Revised Statutes Chapter 712, Part III (HRS §§712-1220 to 712-1231) and enforced by county prosecutors and the Department of the Attorney General.¹

Wagering long predates Western contact. Native Hawaiians bet on footraces, boxing, holua sled racing, surfing, and the board game konane, particularly during the four-month Makahiki festival honoring Lono. Captain Cook's 1778 arrival introduced cards and dice; Protestant missionaries arriving after 1820 pressed for prohibition, and in August 1825 the chiefs at Honolulu issued a kapu against gambling, drunkenness, theft, and Sabbath-breaking — among the earliest formal gambling bans in the islands.² Through the Kingdom, Republic, and Territorial periods (1900 Organic Act onward), gambling remained a misdemeanor or, for organized operators, a more serious offense. Statehood in 1959 carried that prohibition forward intact.

The 1973 enactment of the modern penal code (Act 201) consolidated the gambling offenses in HRS Chapter 712. The "social gambling" exception in §712-1231 — modeled on a Michigan proposal and refined in New York — permits home games among players on equal terms, with no house cut, no minors, and no bookmaking, and not conducted in hotels, bars, schools, parks, or other listed locations. It is an affirmative defense rather than an authorization. Act 111 of 2022 elevated promoting gambling in the first degree to a class B felony and the second-degree offense to a class C felony.³ Casino-legalization bills — including HB1308 in the 2025 session, which would have created a state gaming commission and licensed an integrated resort — have been introduced repeatedly without passage. The 2026 session again advanced study but not legalization; a 24-member Tourism and Gaming Working Group is examining costs and benefits.⁴

Hawaii's outbound gambling tourism is among the highest in the country: Las Vegas is widely known as "the Ninth Island," with pre-pandemic Hawaii-to-Las Vegas air traffic peaking at roughly 335,000 passengers in 2019 and rebounding strongly since. Residents also wager through illegal storefront "game rooms," sweepstakes-style internet cafes (periodically prosecuted as gambling devices), offshore online sportsbooks, and within-community informal play (mahjong, pai gow, hwatu/hanafuda, sakura) that has been a focus of community-based prevention work, particularly among Filipino-American, Korean-American, Chinese-American, and Japanese-American populations.

## Regulatory framework today

There is no Hawaii state regulator for gambling, lottery, racing, or charitable gaming. The Department of the Attorney General (https://ag.hawaii.gov/) and the four county prosecuting attorneys (Honolulu, Hawaii, Maui, Kauai) handle gambling prosecutions. The Hawaii State Department of Health, Behavioral Health Administration (https://health.hawaii.gov/amhd/) houses substance-use and behavioral-health services that absorb most gambling-related clinical contact, though without a dedicated problem-gambling program.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

Hawaii operates no state voluntary self-exclusion program — a direct consequence of the comprehensive prohibition. There is no regulator to maintain a list, no licensed operator from which to be excluded, and no statutory mechanism for breach penalties beyond the existing gambling and trespassing offenses. This is structurally unusual: most U.S. states established their VSE program as a corollary of casino or sports wagering legalization, and Hawaii has authorized neither.

Hawaii residents who recognize a gambling problem have three practical paths. First, **operator-side enrollment in mainland or offshore programs** — Hawaii residents who travel to Las Vegas are eligible to enroll in Nevada's voluntary self-exclusion list maintained by the Nevada Gaming Control Board (https://gaming.nv.gov/about/self-exclusion-program/), which covers all Nevada non-restricted licensees; a Hawaii resident enrolled in Nevada VSE remains excluded on all subsequent trips. Many mainland states' programs are also available to non-residents who present in person. Second, **third-party blocking software** — Gamban, GamBlock, and BetBlocker are widely recommended by the Hawaii Department of Health's behavioral-health staff and by the National Council on Problem Gambling for residents whose primary exposure is online offshore sportsbooks, sweepstakes casinos, or daily fantasy apps. These tools install on phones and computers and restrict access to thousands of gambling URLs and apps. Third, **financial self-exclusion** — banks and several major credit-card issuers (notably Monzo-style transaction blocks and, in the U.S., Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and others by request) will block gambling-merchant codes (MCC 7995) at the cardholder's request.

There is no Hawaii statute imposing penalties for VSE breach because no Hawaii VSE list exists. A Hawaii resident on the Nevada list who attempts to gamble at a Nevada casino is subject to Nevada criminal trespass and forfeiture rules; a Hawaii resident bypassing third-party software has no civil or criminal exposure under Hawaii law beyond the underlying gambling-offense statute. Hawaii does not participate in MSIGA (it has no internet gaming to compact) and is not connected to any tribal self-exclusion database.

For acute help, calls from Hawaii area codes to 1-800-GAMBLER are answered by the National Council on Problem Gambling national helpline (since Hawaii operates no state-routed line), and the Hawaii State Department of Health funds no dedicated gambling-specific clinical program, leaving most clinical capacity inside generalist substance-use providers, the federally qualified health center network, and a small number of private practitioners trained in CBT for gambling disorder.⁵ The 2026 state of the program is, candidly, that there is no program: Hawaii is the U.S. jurisdiction with the largest known per-capita gambling-tourism flow and no dedicated problem-gambling appropriation, helpline, treatment network, or self-exclusion list — a gap repeatedly documented by Honolulu Civil Beat and by the National Council on Problem Gambling's state-level scorecards.
