# Nevada — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

Nevada remains the country's largest commercial gaming market by absolute revenue and the only state with continuous legal commercial casino gambling since 1931. Approximately 270 nonrestricted casinos and 1,800 restricted slot locations operate statewide under the **two-tier licensing system** (restricted = up to 15 slots as an incidental amenity; nonrestricted = full casino). Retail and mobile sports betting is widely available statewide. Online poker (via WSOP.com) is legal as the only authorized form of interactive casino-style gaming; **online casino slots and table games are not authorized**. Several federally recognized tribes operate small Class II/III facilities. **Nevada is the only U.S. state that has never legalized any form of state lottery** — Article IV § 24 of the 1864 Constitution still bars it.¹

## A compressed history

The Comstock Lode strike in 1859 made mining-camp gambling instantaneous and ubiquitous. The Territorial Legislature's first anti-gambling statute (November 28, 1861) was unenforced almost from passage. The 1864 Constitution barred lotteries but not other gaming; the 1869 statute "An Act concerning Gaming" legalized banking games under state and county licensing, making Nevada one of the first U.S. jurisdictions to fully license casino-style commercial gambling. Virginia City had over 100 licensed gaming venues by the 1870s.²

A Progressive Era prohibition (1909 Act, effective October 1, 1910) banned banking games and most non-banking games; the statute was selectively enforced, with slot machines often paying off in "trade stimulator" merchandise tokens. The decisive re-legalization came with **Assembly Bill 98 of 1931**, the "Wide Open Gambling Bill" introduced by Assemblyman Phil Tobin, signed by Governor Fred Balzar on **March 19, 1931** — the same session that adopted six-week-residency divorce, completing Nevada's transformation into a tourism destination founded on activities unavailable elsewhere.³

The mob era took shape after Bugsy Siegel's Flamingo opened December 26, 1946 and accelerated through the 1950s under Meyer Lansky, Frank Costello, and Teamsters Pension Fund financing. Kefauver Committee hearings (1950–1951, with a Las Vegas session November 15, 1950) forced reform. The 1955 Gaming Control Act created the Nevada Gaming Control Board (GCB), and the 1959 Nevada Gaming Control Act (Chapter 459, now codified at NRS Chapter 463) created the three-member Nevada Gaming Commission (NGC) — establishing the bifurcated GCB-investigates / NGC-decides structure copied worldwide. The Black Book ("List of Excluded Persons") was created in 1960 to bar individuals of "notorious or unsavory reputation" and remains in force at NRS 463.151.⁴

Howard Hughes's 1966–1970 acquisition campaign forced corporate-ownership reform: the Corporate Gaming Act of 1969 (SB 1, signed by Governor Paul Laxalt on April 22, 1969) permitted publicly traded companies to hold gaming licenses without individual licensing of every shareholder, opening Las Vegas to Wall Street capital. Hilton's 1971 acquisitions of the International (renamed Las Vegas Hilton) and the Flamingo were the first publicly traded entries.

The modern mega-resort era began November 22, 1989 with Steve Wynn's Mirage. Treasure Island (1993), Bellagio (1998), Wynn (2005), and Encore (2008) followed. Parallel openings — Excalibur, Luxor, MGM Grand, NY-NY, Mandalay Bay, Paris, Venetian, Palazzo, Aria/CityCenter, Cosmopolitan — built out the Strip through 2010. T-Mobile Arena (2016) and the arrival of the Golden Knights (NHL, 2017), Raiders (NFL, 2020), and Aces (WNBA) diversified the economic base beyond gaming. The October 1, 2017 Route 91 mass shooting reshaped Strip security; the March 2020 COVID-19 closure shut Nevada casinos for 78 days — the first sustained statewide closure since 1931.

Nevada is the cradle of legal U.S. sports betting (federal 10% excise tax 1951–1974 nearly killed it; 1974–1983 reductions revived it). Nevada was the lone major PASPA exception (1992–2018) until *Murphy v. NCAA* opened the field. Online poker was authorized by AB 114 of 2011; Ultimate Poker launched April 30, 2013 as the first regulated U.S. online poker site. Nevada and Delaware signed the first interstate online poker compact in 2014, joined by New Jersey (2017), Michigan (2022), Pennsylvania (2025), and West Virginia under the Multi-State Internet Gaming Agreement (MSIGA).⁵

Defunct: 1910–1931 prohibition era; mob-era hidden ownership (formally ended 1967/1969 Corporate Acts); Las Vegas Race Track midget auto pari-mutuel (1948–1949) [unconfirmed precise dates]; in-person mobile-betting registration requirement (repealed 2020).

## Regulatory framework today

The Nevada Gaming Commission and the Nevada Gaming Control Board (https://gaming.nv.gov) operate the bifurcated regulatory structure under NRS Chapters 462, 463, 463A–D, 464, 465, 466, 466A and the Nevada Gaming Regulations. Tribal Class III gaming operates under federal NIGC oversight (https://www.nigc.gov) and state compacts. The Nevada Department of Health and Human Services Division of Public and Behavioral Health Problem Gambling Program (https://dpbh.nv.gov) administers state-funded treatment under NRS 458A. The **Nevada Council on Problem Gambling** (https://www.nevadacouncil.org), founded 1984, is the NCPG affiliate and uses the national 1-800-GAMBLER number. The International Center for Responsible Gaming (https://www.icrg.org) and the UNLV International Gaming Institute conduct research.

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Nevada does **not** have a unified state-administered statewide self-exclusion program for in-person casino gambling. This is a long-standing critique. The actual self-exclusion picture in Nevada is fragmented into three layers.⁶

**1. Property-by-property in-person exclusion.** For in-person casino gambling at Nevada's roughly 270 nonrestricted casinos and 1,800 restricted locations, a person seeking exclusion **must complete a separate form at each individual casino** where they wish to be excluded. There is no centralized state list for brick-and-mortar gaming. The Nevada Council on Problem Gambling explicitly states, "There is no mandated exclusion program in Nevada" for land-based casinos. Duration and removal terms vary by property: some casinos offer 6-month, 1-year, 5-year, or lifetime; others lifetime only; some honor the player's stated term, others maintain the exclusion in perpetuity unless affirmatively rescinded. This patchwork allows a person self-excluded from one Strip property to walk down the Boulevard to another property without legal barrier.⁷

**2. Statewide Interactive Gaming Self-Exclusion List.** Under Nevada Gaming Commission Regulation 5.170 (and subsequent regulation amendments through the 2010s and 2020s), the Gaming Control Board maintains a **statewide self-exclusion list for interactive (online) gaming**, currently limited to online poker (WSOP.com is the only licensed Nevada online operator). Enrollment is by application to the GCB. The list is updated daily and shared with all licensed Nevada interactive operators; an enrolled player is blocked from playing on any Nevada-regulated online site. Duration is selected by the applicant at enrollment, and removal requires an affirmative request to GCB.⁸ This list does **not** cover land-based casinos or sports books; it covers only online interactive gaming as defined in the regulation.

**3. The Black Book (NRS 463.151).** This is **not** voluntary — it is a regulatory exclusion list maintained by the GCB and adopted by the NGC for individuals deemed of "notorious or unsavory reputation," dating to 1960. Listed individuals are barred from all Nevada licensed gaming venues. Approximately 30+ individuals are currently listed.

Despite the user instruction referencing "NVSEP at nvsep.org" as Nevada's program, **NVSEP is in fact the multi-state National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program**, not a Nevada-only state program. NVSEP was launched in August 2024 covering Colorado, Iowa, Michigan, and Tennessee, and as of December 2025 covers 21 participating jurisdictions. **Nevada is not currently an NVSEP participating jurisdiction.** Nevada residents may still access NVSEP-coordinated exclusion when traveling to participating states.⁹

**MSIGA shared liquidity:** Because Nevada is an MSIGA member alongside New Jersey, Michigan, Delaware, Pennsylvania, and West Virginia, players self-excluded under Nevada's online poker list are also blocked from WSOP.com shared-liquidity tables, which means in practical effect from all MSIGA states' online poker products.¹⁰

Cumulative Nevada interactive self-exclusion enrollment exceeded 1,500 individuals by 2024 GCB reporting. There are no published aggregate counts of property-by-property in-person exclusions across the state's 2,000+ licensed gambling locations. Nevada's per-capita problem-gambling funding has been the subject of sustained NCPG critique relative to states like Massachusetts and Pennsylvania.
