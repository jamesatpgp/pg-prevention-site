Russian colonial Alaska (1733-1867) and the U.S. District of Alaska (1867-1912) hosted no organized commercial gambling. Gold-rush mining camps at Nome (1898), Skagway, Wrangell, and Fairbanks (1902) saw saloon faro and dice play typical of frontier boomtowns. The most consequential rush of the era — the Klondike of 1896-1899 — channeled most commercial gambling north into Dawson City in Canada's Yukon. The Alaska Constitution took effect with statehood on January 3, 1959. Article XII § 15 prohibits lotteries except as authorized by the Legislature. The Legislature has never authorized a statewide lottery, and Alaska does not participate in Powerball or Mega Millions. Bills HB 246 of 2020 and SB 110 of 2022 both attempted to create one and failed.

The state's gambling regime instead pivoted around charitable gaming. The Legislature enacted the first authorizing statutes in 1960; the Alaska Gaming Reform Act took effect May 26, 2016. Permitted activities — bingo, pull-tabs, raffles, lotteries, and Calcutta pools — generate most of Alaska's regulated gambling revenue, with pull-tabs the dominant category. Qualified permittees include civic, religious, and fraternal entities plus uniquely Alaskan ones: dog mushers' associations, outboard motor associations, and fishing-derby associations. Two tribal Class II electronic-bingo halls operate under IGRA: the Klawock Cooperative Association on Prince of Wales Island, and the Native Village of Eklutna's Chin'an Gaming Hall outside Anchorage, which opened in 2024 and remains in legal dispute. Class III gaming has never been authorized anywhere in Alaska. Alaska has not legalized sports betting following Murphy v. NCAA.
