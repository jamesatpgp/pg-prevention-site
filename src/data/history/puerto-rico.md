# Puerto Rico — Gambling History

## Current landscape

Puerto Rico operates one of the broadest gambling markets in the U.S. system: a **traditional paper lottery (Lotería Tradicional)** and **Lotería Electrónica** (both under the Treasury Department), **hotel casinos** authorized since 1948, **thoroughbred horse racing** at Camarero Racetrack, **slot routes** at hotels and bars, **mobile sports wagering** since 2023, and **daily fantasy contests**. Cockfighting — historically a regulated cultural practice — was preempted by federal law in 2018. The principal regulator is the **Gaming Commission of the Government of Puerto Rico** (Comisión de Juegos del Gobierno de Puerto Rico), created in 2019 to consolidate functions previously housed in the Tourism Company.¹

## Compressed history

The colonial lottery was established in **1814** at the initiative of Don Alejandro Ramírez, first Intendant of Puerto Rico, with the first drawing held September 1, 1814, in the main plaza of San Juan — making it one of the oldest continuously documented lottery operations in the Americas.² The lottery struggled and lapsed; an 1829 Municipal Lottery for San Juan was joined by later municipal lotteries in Ponce and Mayagüez. Cockfighting (galleras) was widespread throughout the Spanish colonial period and carried over after the 1898 Treaty of Paris brought the island under U.S. sovereignty.

The modern framework rests on three statutes. **Act No. 11 of August 22, 1933** (the "Games of Chance Act") governs the traditional lottery and games of chance.³ **Act No. 221 of May 15, 1948** legalized hotel casinos as a tourism-development measure, requiring operation inside qualified hotels and licensing through the Commissioner of Financial Institutions.⁴ Modern lottery operations were re-established by **Law 465 of May 15, 1947**, which created the Lottery Bureau within Hacienda; Lotería Electrónica was added later as the electronic/instant product line.

Following *Murphy v. NCAA* (2018), **Act No. 81-2019** (the "Government of Puerto Rico Gaming Commission Act") consolidated regulation in a new seven-member **Gaming Commission** — including five ex officio members (Secretary of DDEC, Executive Director of Tourism, Secretary of Sports and Recreation, Administrator of ASSMCA, the Government CIO) and two private-sector appointees of the Governor — and authorized sports betting and fantasy contests.⁵ The Commission promulgated **Sports Betting Regulation No. 9316** and **Fantasy Contest Regulation No. 9317**; Act 168-2020 made technical amendments. Mobile sports wagering launched in 2023, with Caesars among the first operators live on island.

**Hurricane Maria** (September 19–20, 2017) devastated infrastructure and disrupted casinos and tourism for years, compounded by PROMESA fiscal oversight (2016) and the COVID-19 pandemic. Cockfighting was effectively prohibited when the **2018 Farm Bill** (Pub. L. 115-334) extended the federal Animal Welfare Act to U.S. territories.

## Regulatory framework today

The **Gaming Commission of the Government of Puerto Rico** — https://www.comisiondejuegos.pr.gov — licenses and regulates casinos, slot routes, sports betting, and fantasy contests under Act 81-2019. The **Loterías de Puerto Rico** unit of the **Department of the Treasury (Hacienda)** — https://loteriasdepuertorico.pr.gov — operates Lotería Tradicional and Lotería Electrónica. Thoroughbred racing falls under the **Horse Racing Industry and Sport Administration** pursuant to Act No. 83 of 1987. Problem-gambling treatment is delivered by the **Administración de Servicios de Salud Mental y Contra la Adicción (ASSMCA)** — https://www.assmca.pr.gov — whose Administrator sits ex officio on the Gaming Commission.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

**Administering agency:** The Gaming Commission administers the self-exclusion registry for casinos, sports wagering, fantasy contests, slot routes, and horse-racing wagers under **Sports Betting Regulation No. 9316** and parallel casino rules.⁶ Clinical assessment and treatment are referred to **ASSMCA's Programa de Ayuda al Jugador Compulsivo (PAJC)** — the Compulsive Gamblers Assistance Program created under Act 67 of 1993 — which provides free assessment and treatment across all product lines.

**Where to enroll:** Through the Gaming Commission's online portal and through licensed sports-wagering operators, which are required to honor and integrate the Commission's exclusion list. Enrollment forms and resources are linked from the Commission's website and from operator responsible-gaming pages.

**Duration options:** Tiered terms ranging from **one year up to indefinite (lifetime)** self-exclusion, per Regulation 9316. The participant selects the term at enrollment.

**Scope:** Unified across all licensed forms — sports betting (retail and mobile), online sportsbook, horse-racing wagering, daily fantasy contests, casino gaming, and slot routes. The traditional and electronic lottery products operated by Hacienda fall outside the Gaming Commission's portfolio and are not covered by the Commission's registry; players seeking lottery exclusion must coordinate separately through Hacienda.

**Removal process:** Term self-exclusion expires automatically; indefinite/lifetime enrollment requires a formal removal petition reviewed by the Commission with documentation of treatment, following the procedure set out in Regulation 9316.

**Penalties:** Self-excluded persons are barred from collecting winnings, recovering losses, and receiving promotional offers. Licensed operators must refuse service and confiscate winnings; failure to enforce can support disciplinary action against the licensee. Puerto Rico law also protects participants in compulsive-gambling treatment from employment discrimination.

**2026 program state:** Active. ASSMCA's PAJC continues to operate as the clinical arm, with the Administrator's ex officio Gaming Commission seat building problem-gambling representation directly into licensing governance.
