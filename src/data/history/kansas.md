The cattle-trail era of the 1860s-1880s made Wichita, Abilene, Ellsworth, and Dodge City famous for faro, monte, poker, keno, and dice. Wyatt Earp and Bat Masterson worked as both peace officers and faro dealers. The 1859 Wyandotte Constitution banned lotteries, and a wave of Methodist-Baptist temperance reformers — including Carry Nation's saloon-smashing — pushed Kansas into one of the most restrictive anti-gambling regimes in the country. Bingo was authorized by 1974 constitutional amendment. Pari-mutuel wagering was approved by voters in November 1986. The Woodlands in Kansas City, Camptown Greyhound Park in Frontenac, and Wichita Greyhound Park opened, but the racing industry struggled commercially; the Woodlands closed in 2008. Raffles by qualified charities were authorized by 2014 amendment.

The modern Kansas Lottery was authorized by November 1986 amendment, and the Kansas Lottery Act passed in 1987. Tribal casinos opened in 1996-1998: Casino White Cloud (Iowa Tribe), Golden Eagle Casino (Kickapoo Tribe), Sac and Fox Casino, and Prairie Band Casino and Resort. The Kansas Expanded Lottery Act of 2007, signed by Governor Kathleen Sebelius, created a structurally unusual state-owned casino model. The Kansas Lottery technically owns all gaming devices at four state-owned casinos, while private managers build and run the facilities. Boot Hill Casino in Dodge City opened December 15, 2009; Kansas Star, Hollywood Casino at Kansas Speedway, and Kansas Crossing Casino followed by 2017. Senate Bill 84 of 2022 legalized sports wagering at the four state-owned casinos and via mobile platforms; launch was September 1, 2022.
