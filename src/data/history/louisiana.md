Louisiana's gambling culture predates the United States. Under French and Spanish rule, colonial Louisiana hosted bouillotte, faro, and craps. Antebellum New Orleans had more than thirty licensed gaming houses by the 1820s, and steamboat gambling produced the Mississippi-river card sharp. The Fair Grounds racetrack, founded in 1852, is the second-oldest continuously running U.S. racetrack. The defining moment in 19th-century American gambling was the Louisiana State Lottery Company (1868-1894). The post-Civil War legislature granted Charles T. Howard's company a 25-year exclusive monopoly. Within years it was the largest lottery in the United States, operating nationwide by mail order and bribing legislators, judges, and federal postal officials on a breathtaking scale.

Federal action followed. The Anti-Lottery Act of 1890 banned the use of the U.S. mail for lottery tickets, and the Louisiana State Lottery Company ended in 1894. No U.S. state authorized a legal lottery from 1894 until New Hampshire revived the form in 1964 — a 70-year prohibition traceable directly to the Louisiana Lottery scandal. Pari-mutuel horse racing was re-authorized in 1908. Voters approved a modern state lottery in October 1990; tickets went on sale September 6, 1991. The Riverboat Economic Development and Gaming Control Act of 1991 authorized fifteen riverboat casino licenses. Caesars New Orleans, the single land-based casino, opened in 1999. Voters approved sports wagering by parish referendum in November 2020; retail launched October 2021 and mobile January 28, 2022. Three tribal casinos opened in the 1990s under IGRA compacts.
