# Montana — Gambling History

## Where things stand in 2026

Montana operates one of the most distinctive gambling regimes in the United States: **statewide distributed video gambling machines (VGMs) in tavern and on-sale beer/wine licensee locations** — up to 20 machines per qualifying premises — instead of concentrated casino floors. Approximately 1,400+ licensed locations host video poker, video keno, and video bingo under statutory caps ($2 max wager per play, $1,000 max single-game payout). The Montana Lottery runs draw and instant games and is the **monopoly operator of sports wagering** through "Sports Bet Montana," available only at kiosks and mobile apps geofenced to licensed retailer premises. Several federally recognized tribes operate Class III casinos under IGRA compacts. Live pari-mutuel racing survives as a county-fair phenomenon at a handful of summer meets.¹

## A compressed history

Montana's gaming culture grew out of the 1862 Bannack gold strike and the 1863 Virginia City rush. Faro, poker, and roulette ran openly in Butte, Anaconda, Helena, and Virginia City through the late nineteenth century. Indigenous nations — Salish, Kootenai, Pend d'Oreille, Blackfeet, Crow, Northern Cheyenne, Assiniboine, Gros Ventre — practiced stick games, hand games, and horse racing on extensive pre-contact networks; stick games are still played at intertribal celebrations today.² Montana was unusual: territorial codes after 1864 licensed commercial gambling, and Article XIX § 2 of the 1889 Constitution barred only lotteries and gift enterprises, not ordinary commercial games. Cities continued to license slot machines and card rooms into the early twentieth century.

The narrowing came with the Montana Anti-Gambling Act of 1949 and the 1951 federal Johnson Act. Pari-mutuel horse racing had been authorized earlier than most — Chapter 184 of the Laws of 1929 created the Racing Commission, now codified at Title 23, Chapter 4 of the Montana Code Annotated. Yellowstone Downs, Great Falls Race Park, Helena Downs, the Western Montana Fair, and Big Sky Downs hosted summer fairground meets; by the 2020s only Yellowstone Downs (Billings) and the Western Montana Fair (Missoula) remained as live venues, with simulcast and ADW wagering carrying most of the racing footprint.

The lottery prohibition fell by voter initiative in November 1986 (I-105, 69.4% yes), and enabling legislation (Chapter 466 of the Laws of 1987, Title 23 Chapter 7 MCA) launched the Montana Lottery on June 24, 1987. Montana joined Powerball in 1990 as a founding state of the modern game.

The **distributed VGM model** that defines modern Montana gambling was built statute-by-statute through the 1980s and 1990s, codified at Title 23, Chapter 5, Parts 6 and 7 MCA. The Department of Justice Gambling Control Division regulates VGMs, card rooms (live poker and similar), live keno, charitable bingo and raffles, and pull-tabs. The "Montana model" produces statewide gambling revenue spread across more than 1,400 small licensee locations rather than concentrated casino properties — there are no Las Vegas-style commercial destinations in the state.

Tribal Class III gaming operates at several federally recognized tribes: the Confederated Salish and Kootenai (Gray Wolf Peak, KwaTaqNuk Resort), the Blackfeet Nation (Glacier Peaks, Northern Winz), the Northern Cheyenne (Charging Horse), the Crow Tribe (Apsaalooke Nights), the Fort Peck Assiniboine and Sioux (Silver Wolf), the Fort Belknap Indian Community (Fort Belknap Casino), the Chippewa Cree, and the Little Shell Tribe of Chippewa (federally recognized 2019, gaming under development).

Sports betting arrived in May 2019 through HB 725, signed by Governor Steve Bullock, with the **Montana Lottery as state monopoly operator** under contract with Intralot. Kiosks launched in November 2019 and a Sports Bet Montana mobile app followed; wagering is geofenced to licensed retailer premises, making Montana one of the only sports-betting states without true statewide mobile.³ Tribal sports-book compact negotiations continue.

Defunct: open licensed faro and poker in Butte/Anaconda (curtailed 1949); mining-camp card rooms in Virginia City and Helena; greyhound racing (never legalized); large-scale live thoroughbred racing (largely gone since 2000).

## Regulatory framework today

The Montana Department of Justice Gambling Control Division (https://dojmt.gov/gaming), established 1987, regulates all gambling except sports betting, the lottery, and horse racing under Title 23 Chapter 5 MCA. The Montana Board of Horse Racing (within the Department of Livestock, https://liv.mt.gov) oversees pari-mutuel racing under Title 23 Chapter 4. The Montana Lottery (https://www.montanalottery.com) operates draw and instant games and Sports Bet Montana (https://www.sportsbetmontana.com) under Title 23 Chapter 7. Tribal Class III gaming is federally overseen by the National Indian Gaming Commission (https://www.nigc.gov). The Montana Department of Public Health and Human Services Behavioral Health Division (https://dphhs.mt.gov) contracts for problem-gambling treatment, and the Montana Council on Problem Gambling (https://montanacouncilonproblemgambling.org) runs the 1-888-900-9979 helpline.

## Voluntary self-exclusion

Montana operates a state Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program through the Gambling Control Division under MCA § 23-5-706 and Administrative Rules of Montana 23.16. The program is **fragmented across the state's distributed gambling regime** and is generally considered modest in scope relative to the size of the VGM industry.⁴

Enrollment is in person at any GCD office (Helena headquarters or regional field offices) or by appointment with GCD investigators; the GCD does not currently operate a full online enrollment portal for VGM exclusion. Applicants present photo ID, complete a sworn application, and select an exclusion term. Durations are typically **1 year, 5 years, or lifetime**, varying somewhat by licensee category.

Scope is narrower than in most concentrated-casino states. The state VSE list covers **tavern and on-sale beer/wine licensees with VGMs** statewide, card rooms, live keno establishments, and licensed bingo halls — operators receive the exclusion list and are required to refuse service to enrolled persons. However, the distributed model has historically made enforcement difficult: a tavern with five VGMs in a small Montana town does not have the surveillance infrastructure of a destination casino, and breach detection often depends on operator vigilance. The state VSE list does **not** cover Montana tribal casinos (which run independent tribal exclusion programs under each tribe's gaming authority) and is **separate from the Montana Lottery's Sports Bet Montana** responsible-gaming and self-exclusion mechanisms, which a player must request directly through the Sports Bet Montana app and account-management system.

Breach: An enrolled person who gambles at a covered licensee may have winnings forfeited (returned to the licensee or to the Montana Lottery and Charitable Gambling Account per MCA), and the licensee that knowingly permits the play may face administrative sanctions including license suspension. The state's enforcement record has been criticized by NCPG affiliates and the Montana Council on Problem Gambling, with calls for higher dedicated VGM-revenue funding and a unified statewide enrollment portal.

Montana is **not** a participant in the National Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program (NVSEP at nvsep.org). It has no MSIGA exposure (no online poker or online casino), and tribal casinos operate separate compact-based programs. Aggregate VSE enrollment is not centrally published by GCD; the program is widely regarded as under-enrolled relative to the state's gambling-disorder prevalence, which NCPG estimates at among the higher rates per capita in the United States given the density of VGM availability [precise enrollment counts unconfirmed].
