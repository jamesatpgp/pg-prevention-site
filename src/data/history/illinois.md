# Illinois — Gambling History

Illinois in 2026 is one of the broadest legal-gambling jurisdictions in the country: riverboat and land-based commercial casinos (now ten, including Bally's Chicago), racetracks operating as racinos under the 2019 expansion, roughly 8,000 Video Gaming Terminal (VGT) establishments across the state, retail and mobile sports wagering, the Illinois Lottery (the first U.S. state lottery to sell tickets online, in 2012), pari-mutuel horse racing, and comprehensive charitable gaming. The Illinois Gaming Board (IGB) regulates casinos, VGTs, and sports wagering; the Illinois Racing Board regulates pari-mutuel racing; the Illinois Department of the Lottery operates the lottery; and the Illinois Department of Revenue licenses charitable gaming. Illinois has tiered sports-wagering taxes that reach 40% on top-tier operators — the highest in the country at adoption.¹

The Illinois Country's gambling culture traces to French colonial card and dice games along the Mississippi and Illinois rivers; after statehood in 1818, Chicago — particularly after the 1848 opening of the Illinois & Michigan Canal — grew into a regional center of faro banks, policy/numbers operations, and commercial gambling houses tightly woven into municipal politics. The 1893 Columbian Exposition catalyzed a reform movement; the Civic Federation of Chicago, the Illinois Crime Commission, and successive grand juries through the 1900s, 1910s, and 1920s exposed organized gambling, and Prohibition (1920-1933) entrenched Al Capone's Outfit as the dominant operator across Cook County and downstate. The 1927 Illinois Horse Racing Act legalized pari-mutuel wagering — a compromise that taxed the most popular form while continuing to prohibit others.² Through the mid-20th century illegal handbooks, policy wheels, and slot machines persisted in "wide open" downstate towns and in Cicero and Berwyn; the 1950 Kefauver Senate hearings documented the scale.

Modern expansion unfolded in waves. The Illinois Lottery was authorized by the General Assembly and launched July 1, 1974, with first-year sales of about $129 million; it joined Powerball in 2010 and became the first U.S. lottery to sell tickets online in 2012.³ The Riverboat Gambling Act (Feb. 7, 1990) made Illinois the second state after Iowa to authorize riverboat casinos, with up to ten licenses originally required to "cruise" while gambling occurred; the Argosy Casino Alton opened first on Sept. 11, 1991, and the cruising requirement was repealed in 1999. The Video Gaming Act (July 13, 2009) authorized VGTs in bars, restaurants, truck stops, and fraternal/veterans' clubs with liquor licenses; the first machines went live Sept. 10, 2012, originally capped at five per location (raised to six in 2019). Public Act 101-0031 (June 28, 2019) authorized six new casino licenses including the Chicago casino, converted racetracks into racinos, and legalized sports wagering. The first legal Illinois sports wager was placed March 9, 2020; the COVID-19 pandemic prompted Gov. Pritzker to suspend the in-person sportsbook-registration requirement, made permanent in 2022. Arlington Park's last live racing meet was held in 2021 and the property was sold; Hawthorne, Fairmount, Bally's temporary Chicago casino (2023), and new builds in Waukegan, Danville, Walker's Bluff, and Rockford reflect the post-2019 landscape.

## Regulatory framework today

The Illinois Gaming Board (https://igb.illinois.gov/) regulates casinos, video gaming, and sports wagering under 230 ILCS 10, 230 ILCS 40, and 230 ILCS 45. The Illinois Racing Board regulates pari-mutuel horse racing under 230 ILCS 5. The Illinois Department of the Lottery (https://www.illinoislottery.com/) operates the lottery under 20 ILCS 1605, with private management under Camelot since 2018 (the original Northstar contract was terminated in 2017). The Illinois Department of Revenue licenses charitable bingo (230 ILCS 25), raffles (230 ILCS 15), pull-tabs (230 ILCS 20), and charitable games (230 ILCS 30). The Illinois Department of Human Services, Division of Substance Use Prevention and Recovery, administers the Statewide Compulsive Gambling Program.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

The Illinois Gaming Board operates the Self-Exclusion Program (SEP) under 11 Ill. Admin. Code §1770.240, established in 2002 for casinos and expanded under the 2019 Sports Wagering Act to cover retail and mobile sportsbooks. The SEP currently does **not** include video gaming terminals — a long-standing gap that drew sharp public-health criticism. In October 2024, IGB Administrator Marcus Fruchter opened a public-comment process to extend the SEP to VGTs; rulemaking has been active through 2025-2026 and is expected to take effect.⁴

**Duration.** Illinois SEP enrollment is functionally a lifetime ban. Enrollees may apply to be removed only after five years on the list and only with a notarized affidavit from a state-licensed mental-health professional certifying the person can gamble safely again. There are no one-year or three-year tiers.

**Enrollment.** Persons must enroll in person at an IGB office located inside any Illinois casino (no appointment required) or at one of several sanctioned community enrollment sites that include IDHS-contracted treatment providers throughout the state. The process takes 30-45 minutes and requires a valid government-issued photo ID. The Illinois Department of Human Services publishes formal SEP enrollment guidelines for counselors at https://www.dhs.state.il.us/OneNetLibrary/27896/documents/SUPR/Guidelines/SEPGuidelines0822A11Y_ADA.pdf.

**Scope.** The SEP covers all Illinois land-based casinos and racinos, all retail sportsbooks, and all mobile sports wagering platforms (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM, Caesars, Fanatics, ESPN BET, and casino-aligned books). It does **not** cover VGTs (pending rulemaking), the Illinois Lottery (which operates a separate, narrower retailer-side bar program), pari-mutuel racing wagers placed at off-track facilities, or charitable bingo. The Illinois Lottery's iLottery online platform applies its own account-level self-exclusion separate from the SEP.

**Removal process.** Removal is by application to the IGB after five years on the list, with the affidavit requirement above. The IGB Administrator may deny removal applications.

**Penalties for breach.** A person on the SEP who enters a licensed casino or wagers with a sportsbook is subject to criminal trespass charges and forfeits all winnings to the State, which transfers them to the Compulsive Gambling Fund administered by IDHS. Sportsbook operators that knowingly accept wagers from SEP enrollees face administrative discipline.

**Multi-state participation.** Illinois does **not** participate in MSIGA (no Illinois online casino exists to compact), and the SEP is not formally reciprocal with neighboring state lists (Indiana VEP, Iowa SEP, Michigan, Wisconsin tribal lists). Operators voluntarily honor enrollments on other states' lists for accounts the operator already maintains under a single national identity-verification spine, but Illinois law does not require cross-state recognition.

**State of the program in 2026.** Illinois has consistently been among the top states by SEP enrollments — reflecting both its market size and its broad coverage — and the open question is whether 2025-2026 rulemaking to extend the SEP to VGTs takes effect on the projected timeline. The VGT gap is widely understood as the largest structural weakness in Illinois's responsible-gambling framework, since neighborhood VGT exposure has been linked in University of Illinois at Chicago and other public-health research to elevated problem-gambling prevalence. For SEP information, the IGB page is: https://igb.illinois.gov/help-for-problem-gamblers/self-exclusion-program.html. Helpline: 1-800-GAMBLER. SEP Director: (312) 814-8832 or 1-877-968-7848.⁵
