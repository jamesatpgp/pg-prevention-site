The 1850 Michigan Constitution at Article IV § 33 barred any lottery authorization — a provision that held for 122 years. Detroit's proximity to Windsor, Ontario — where racetracks opened after Canada legalized pari-mutuel betting in 1910 — and the Purple Gang's Prohibition-era vice operations produced a robust illegal-gambling shadow economy through the 1930s. Voters approved pari-mutuel racing by constitutional amendment in 1933. The lottery prohibition fell in 1972 when voters approved Proposal 1; the Michigan Lottery sold its first ticket on November 13, 1972, with net proceeds dedicated to the School Aid Fund. Michigan's iLottery launched in August 2014, anticipating broader iGaming a decade later. Live pari-mutuel horse racing effectively ended on February 3, 2024, when Northville Downs ran its final card.

Tribal gaming came next. Bay Mills Indian Community opened bingo in 1984; Michigan signed Class III compacts with seven tribes in August 1993. Soaring Eagle Casino opened December 17, 1993. Commercial casinos followed a 1996 Detroit referendum and the Michigan Gaming Control and Revenue Act of 1997. MGM Grand Detroit opened July 29, 1999; MotorCity and Greektown followed by November 2000. A 2004 ballot measure constitutionalized voter approval for any new non-tribal casino, freezing the commercial footprint at three Detroit properties. The decisive expansion came December 20, 2019, when Governor Gretchen Whitmer signed the Lawful Sports Betting Act and the Lawful Internet Gaming Act. Both went live January 22, 2021. Michigan joined the Multi-State Internet Gaming Agreement in 2022 for shared online poker liquidity.
