# Alabama — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
Alabama is one of the most restrictive gambling jurisdictions in the United States. The state has **no lottery, no commercial casinos, no pari-mutuel live racing, no legal sports betting, and no licensed online gaming.** The only lawful casino-style gaming is Class II electronic bingo and pull-tabs operated by the **Poarch Band of Creek Indians** at three Wind Creek properties (Atmore, Wetumpka, Montgomery), regulated tribally by the **Poarch Creek Gaming Commission** with federal oversight by the **National Indian Gaming Commission**. Pari-mutuel licensing remains nominally with county racing commissions under the Class 1 Municipalities racing statute, but all four former greyhound tracks have ended live racing. Charitable bingo exists only by local constitutional amendment in specific counties. The 1901 Alabama Constitution is the controlling reason for this posture.[^1][^2]

## How Alabama got here
The 1819, 1861, 1865, 1868, and 1875 constitutions all contained anti-lottery language consistent with the broader post-Louisiana-Lottery Southern reform wave, and the **Constitution of Alabama of 1901** — still in force, heavily amended — entrenched a comprehensive ban on lotteries and "any scheme in the nature of a lottery." That provision, Article IV § 65, is the load-bearing reason Alabama still has no state lottery in 2026; every modern attempt to replace it (most prominently Governor Don Siegelman's October 1999 education-lottery referendum, which voters rejected) has failed.[^1][^2]

Pari-mutuel dog racing was authorized only county-by-county through local constitutional amendments beginning with Mobile County (enabling 1971, ratified January 1972). Four greyhound tracks operated and all have since closed live racing: **Greenetrack** (Greene County) ended live racing in 1996, **VictoryLand** (Macon County, Shorter) on June 1, 2011, **Mobile Greyhound Park** on August 19, 2017, and **Birmingham Race Course** (Jefferson County) on March 18, 2020. The Birmingham Turf Club opened thoroughbred racing in March 1987 under Ala. Code §§ 11-65-1 to 11-65-47 but converted to greyhound within a few years. Repeated Alabama Supreme Court decisions — most prominently *State v. $223,405.86* (2016) and *Houston Cnty. Econ. Dev. Auth. v. State* (2013) — have held that electronic gaming machines marketed as "bingo" at Greenetrack, VictoryLand, and other non-tribal sites did not satisfy the constitutional definition of bingo.[^2][^3][^4]

Tribal gaming arrived with federal recognition of the Poarch Band on August 11, 1984, and the opening of the Creek Bingo Palace at Atmore on April 13, 1985 — one of the earliest tribal gaming facilities post-IGRA. All Poarch Band gaming remains **Class II only** (electronic bingo and pull-tabs); Alabama has never entered a Class III tribal-state compact. Comprehensive 2024 lottery/sports-betting legislation (HB 151 and HB 152) passed the House but died in the Senate, and the *Murphy* decision has produced no sports-wagering authorization in Alabama.[^5]

## Regulatory framework today
There is no statewide gaming regulator. **Poarch Band tribal gaming** is regulated by the **Poarch Creek Gaming Commission** under tribal ordinance with NIGC oversight ([nigc.gov](https://www.nigc.gov)). County **racing commissions** (Birmingham, Mobile, Greene, Macon) retain nominal jurisdiction over the moribund pari-mutuel sector. **Charitable bingo** is enforced county-by-county by sheriffs under local constitutional amendments, with Attorney General backstop. The **Alabama Council on Compulsive Gambling** (501(c)(3), EIN 27-2030535, Montgomery) is the non-governmental hub for problem-gambling helpline service, counselor training, and referrals; it is not a regulator.[^6][^7]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
**Alabama has no statewide voluntary self-exclusion (VSE) program.** Because the state operates no lottery, regulates no commercial casino, and has no licensed sports-wagering vertical, there is no state agency that maintains an exclusion list and no enrollment portal on any Alabama .gov site. The only self-exclusion option available to Alabama residents who gamble inside the state is **tribal-property-specific exclusion at the three Wind Creek casinos**, administered by Wind Creek Hospitality security and the Poarch Creek Gaming Commission. The Poarch Band does not publish enrollment statistics, duration menus, or removal procedures publicly; players seeking exclusion from a specific Wind Creek property must request it on site through casino security or guest services, and the enrollment is generally treated as property-specific (not state-wide and not cross-property in published form). The Poarch Creek Gaming Commission has not published a model self-exclusion form on its website, and the duration options, scope (whether enrollment at Wind Creek Atmore also covers Wetumpka and Montgomery), penalties for breach, and removal/petition rules are **[unconfirmed]** absent direct contact with tribal gaming staff.

Alabama also does **not participate** in any multi-state self-exclusion arrangement. The state is not a signatory to the Multi-State Internet Gaming Agreement (MSIGA) — which is irrelevant because Alabama has no licensed iGaming — and is not part of any tribal multi-state self-exclusion compact. The National Council on Problem Gambling's voluntary national exclusion program (a private initiative) is accessible to Alabama residents but operates outside any state authority. For Alabama residents who self-exclude from out-of-state online sportsbooks or casinos, that exclusion is administered by the home-state regulator of the operator (e.g., Mississippi, Tennessee, Florida via the Seminole compact, or any state where the operator is licensed) and does not affect Alabama land-based access.

Practically, Alabama-based prevention work routes self-exclusion conversations through the **Alabama Council on Compulsive Gambling** helpline (1-800-GAMBLER for national calls, with Alabama-specific intake through ACCG's voice line) and into clinical referrals rather than into a regulatory exclusion list. The 2026 state of the program is **no statewide list exists**; this is the honest gap that distinguishes Alabama from neighboring Mississippi, Tennessee, and Florida, all of which maintain regulator-administered VSE rosters.[^7][^8]
