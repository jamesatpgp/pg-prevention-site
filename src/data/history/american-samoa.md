# American Samoa — Gambling History

## Current landscape

American Samoa is a **comprehensive prohibition jurisdiction**: it has no government lottery, no commercial casino, no parimutuel racing, no licensed sports-wagering market, no slot or video-gambling devices, and no commercial gaming regulatory agency. The only legal exception is **occasional charitable bingo and raffles** where proceeds support religious, educational, or charitable purposes. The prohibition framework sits at **A.S.C.A. Title 46, Chapter 43** (Gambling), reinforced by federal preemption of gambling devices in U.S. possessions under the **Johnson Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 1171–1178)**.¹

## Compressed history

American Samoa came under U.S. sovereignty by the **1900 Deed of Cession** (Tutuila and Aunu'u) and the **1904 Deed of Cession** (Manu'a), with U.S. Naval administration through 1951 and civilian administration thereafter. Traditional Samoan governance — *fa'amatai*, the chiefly system — emphasizes communal land tenure and deference to *matai* authority, layered with a strongly Christian moral framework introduced by London Missionary Society missionaries in the 1830s. The resulting cultural and religious matrix (predominantly Congregational, Catholic, and Methodist) has been the defining force in shaping a prohibitionist gambling policy.²

The modern prohibition sits at **A.S.C.A. § 46.4301** (criminalizing gambling and keeping gaming facilities) and **A.S.C.A. § 46.4302**, which carves the sole meaningful exception: "Nothing contained in this chapter prohibits the occasional playing of bingo or the selling of chances for the raffling of an item of value when the profits from the bingo game or raffle are used for religious, educational or charitable purposes." This exception traces to **Public Law 16-43 § 2 (1979)**.³ A separate territorial poker-machine licensing scheme was held preempted by the federal **Johnson Act** in *American Samoa Government v. Muavaefa'atasi* and is thus inoperative, meaning the practical legal landscape has been comprehensive prohibition since well before *Murphy v. NCAA*.⁴

The 2018 *Murphy v. NCAA* ruling did not produce territorial sports-wagering legislation; no bill has been enacted by the Fono (American Samoa Legislature) authorizing a sports-wagering framework. Multi-state lottery products, daily fantasy sports, online sportsbooks, and internet casino products are functionally unavailable as legal commercial activity in the territory. The population (approximately 45,000–50,000 per U.S. Census Bureau territorial profiles) and concentrated tuna-cannery and federal-transfer economy do not present the demographic predicate for a casino-tourism revenue model.

## Regulatory framework today

There is **no commercial gaming regulatory agency** in American Samoa. Charitable bingo and raffles operate under the **A.S.C.A. § 46.4302** exception with **no centralized licensing commission**; oversight is handled through general criminal-law enforcement by the **Office of the Attorney General** — https://www.americansamoa.gov/attorney-general — and by the **Department of Public Safety**. Behavioral-health services are provided through the **American Samoa Department of Health** — https://americansamoa.gov/health-2. The **Fono** (Legislature) — https://www.americansamoa.gov/fono — would be the body to enact any future gambling legislation.

## Voluntary self-exclusion (VSE)

**Administering agency:** **None.** American Samoa has **no licensed gambling industry and consequently no formal voluntary self-exclusion program**. There is no statutory authority charging any territorial agency with operating a self-exclusion registry, and there is no gaming licensee from which a participant could be formally excluded. This is not a gap in implementation — it is the direct consequence of the prohibition framework: with no commercial casino, no lottery, no sports book, and no parimutuel racing, there is no licensed venue or platform within the territory from which to self-exclude.

**Where to enroll:** No enrollment pathway exists.

**Duration options:** Not applicable.

**Scope:** Not applicable.

**Removal process:** Not applicable.

**Penalties:** Not applicable.

**2026 program state:** No formal program in the territory. Concerns occasionally cited in American Samoa government and media discussions focus on illegal pockets, cross-border online gambling reachable from devices on island, and spillover risk of problem-gambling behavior originating from gambling activity during travel off-island (Hawaii, U.S. mainland, or U.S. military bases in the Pacific). Residents experiencing gambling-related distress can access behavioral-health services through the American Samoa Department of Health alongside substance-use and mental-health programming, and the **National Council on Problem Gambling** national helpline (1-800-GAMBLER) is available.⁵ The most consequential gambling-policy choice American Samoa has made is not to make one — by keeping the prohibition in A.S.C.A. § 46.4301 intact and declining post-*Murphy* sports-wagering legislation, the territory has preserved the smallest legal-gaming footprint of any U.S. jurisdiction.
