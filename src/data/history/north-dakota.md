The 1889 North Dakota Constitution barred lotteries — paralleling the state's alcohol prohibition, which ran from 1889 to 1933. Despite the ban, illegal slot machines and punchboards proliferated through the 1930s to 1950s. The Legislative Assembly authorized charitable gaming by 1976 constitutional amendment and 1977 implementing legislation — the start of what would become a multibillion-dollar charitable sector. Pari-mutuel horse racing came in 1987. Tribal casinos opened between 1992 and 1993 after IGRA compacts: Spirit Lake at St. Michael, 4 Bears (MHA Nation, New Town), Prairie Knights (Standing Rock, Fort Yates), Sky Dancer (Turtle Mountain, Belcourt), and Dakota Magic (Sisseton-Wahpeton, Hankinson). Greyhound racing was never legalized.

Voters approved a state Lottery in November 2002; the North Dakota Lottery sold its first ticket on March 25, 2004. The lottery is uniquely structured: it sells only multistate draw games (Powerball, Mega Millions, Lotto America, Lucky for Life, and 2by2) and no scratch products. The transformative 2017 change was the authorization of electronic pull-tab devices. Electronic pull-tab proceeds have grown sharply since, ranking North Dakota near the top of all states in per-capita charitable gaming. Tribal sports betting was added by 2021 compact amendment; Standing Rock opened the first sportsbook at Prairie Knights on November 1, 2021. Mobile sports betting is permitted only while the bettor is physically present on tribal land. Statewide commercial sports-betting amendments have failed in 2021, 2023, and 2025.
