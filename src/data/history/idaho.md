Gambling arrived in Idaho with the fur trade and the gold rushes of the 1860s. Frontier camps such as Pierce, Idaho City, and Silver City were home to faro, keno, and poker — overlaid on stick-game and hand-game traditions among the Nez Perce, Coeur d'Alene, Shoshone-Bannock, and Kootenai. Idaho Territory (1863) and the new state (1890) had largely outlawed commercial gambling by 1899. From 1947 to 1953, however, slot machines were widely tolerated in northern Idaho — "one-armed bandits" operated openly on the outskirts of Idaho Falls, Pocatello, and Garden City. A federal crackdown and state legislation ended the practice and shaped a durable political consensus that mechanical gambling devices were corrupting. Idaho legalized pari-mutuel betting on horse racing in 1963.

The modern Idaho Lottery was authorized by a citizen-initiated constitutional amendment in 1986, with ticket sales beginning in 1989. Casino-style gambling exists only on tribal lands under IGRA. The Coeur d'Alene Tribe opened a bingo hall in 1993, and in 1994 launched the National Indian Lottery — the first multi-state Internet lottery — which ended in 1998 after federal litigation. Three other tribes — Nez Perce, Kootenai, and Shoshone-Bannock — opened casinos by 2000. Idaho compacts limit Class III machines to bingo-based "tribal video gaming" devices rather than traditional slots. A 1992 constitutional amendment declared gambling "contrary to public policy," limiting legal forms to the lottery, pari-mutuel racing, and charitable bingo and raffles. Idaho has not legalized commercial sports wagering as of 2026.
