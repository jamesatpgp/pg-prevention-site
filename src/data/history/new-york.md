Horse racing in what became New York predates the Republic. The Newmarket Course on Long Island's Hempstead Plain, established in 1665, is generally cited as North America's first organized racetrack. Saratoga Race Course opened in 1863 and is still the oldest continuously operating major sports venue in the country. Before 1894, the colonial legislature and the early Republic chartered lotteries to fund King's College (now Columbia, 1746), Union College (1805), roads, bridges, and prisons. On November 6, 1894, voters ratified a sweeping constitutional ban on "lotteries or the sale of lottery tickets, pool-selling, book-making, or any other kind of gambling." That clause remains the baseline today, modified only by enumerated exceptions added through later amendments.

Every modern legal form came as a carve-out from the 1894 ban. The 1939 amendment reopened pari-mutuel wagering on horse races. Voters approved charitable bingo in 1957, then the state lottery in 1966 — the New York Lottery began sales on June 1, 1967, the second modern U.S. lottery after New Hampshire. The 1970 off-track betting law created the country's first legal OTB system. Tribal Class III gaming opened with Oneida's Turning Stone in July 1993. Voters approved commercial casinos by 2013 amendment; four upstate casinos opened by February 2018. After Murphy v. NCAA, retail sports betting launched in July 2019; mobile sports betting went live January 8, 2022. Within a year, New York was the largest sports-betting market in the country.
