Gambling in the Dakota Territory was a fixture of Black Hills frontier life. Deadwood's saloons ran open faro, poker, and roulette from the 1876 gold rush onward. Wild Bill Hickok died holding aces and eights — the "dead man's hand" — in Nuttall and Mann's No. 10 Saloon on August 2, 1876. Statehood in 1889 brought a constitutional anti-lottery clause, but enforcement was lax until 1947, when state authorities formally shuttered Deadwood's open gambling halls. Deadwood's economy then declined for four decades. The catalytic event was the 1987 fire that destroyed the Syndicate Block, exposing the depth of the preservation crisis and energizing the Deadwood You Bet Committee. Voters approved limited gaming on November 8, 1988.

Casinos opened on November 1, 1989 — making South Dakota the second state, after Nevada, with state-licensed commercial casinos. Initial $5 bet limits rose to $100 in 2000 and $1,000 in 2012. The state lottery launched September 30, 1987. South Dakota's video lottery, the country's first lottery-operated VLT network, began in October 1989; the state Supreme Court briefly struck it down in 1994, and voters reauthorized it that November. Tribal Class III gaming followed under IGRA: the Flandreau Santee Sioux opened Royal River Casino in 1990, with five other tribes compacting by 1992. Sports wagering arrived through Amendment B in November 2020, permitting wagering only "within the city of Deadwood"; retail sportsbooks opened September 9, 2021. Statewide mobile remains unauthorized as of 2026.
