Ohio banned commercial gambling in its 1851 constitution and held that line for more than a century. The first legal opening came in 1933, when the legislature legalized pari-mutuel betting on horse racing. The ban did not stop illegal play. The Cleveland Syndicate and Mayfield Road Mob ran some of the largest illegal gambling networks in the Midwest, with casinos and numbers rackets in Cleveland and Cincinnati. In 1934, Al Capone's Chicago syndicate financed a new track in Northfield Village. The legislature blocked dog betting, so the track pivoted to midget car racing. That same parcel is now a state-licensed racino.

Modern legal gambling came mostly through the voters. They approved the state lottery in May 1973 (Issue 1), and tickets went on sale the next year. A 1975 amendment allowed charities to run bingo and raffles. In 1987, voters dedicated all lottery profits to public education. Casinos took four tries — voters rejected proposals in 1990, 1996, 2006, and 2008 before Issue 3 narrowly passed in November 2009. Four casinos opened in Cleveland, Toledo, Columbus, and Cincinnati between 2012 and 2013. The Ohio Lottery later installed video lottery terminals at the seven existing racetracks, creating the "racinos." Sports betting came through the legislature in late 2021 and launched on January 1, 2023.
