Virginia is the original cradle of state-sanctioned lotteries in North America. King James I authorized the Virginia Company of London in 1612 to operate lotteries to fund the struggling Jamestown colony — the first English-language public lotteries conducted on the continent. Thomas Jefferson personally promoted an 1826 private lottery to pay down his Monticello debts. The 1902 Virginia Constitution outlawed lotteries after the discredited Louisiana Lottery model. Virginia maintained one of the South's most restrictive gambling regimes through the first three-quarters of the 20th century. Voters approved pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing in 1988, and Colonial Downs opened in 1997. Voters approved a state lottery in November 1987; the Virginia Lottery began ticket sales September 20, 1988. Charitable bingo and raffles operate under VDACS oversight.

The watershed expansion came in 2020. Senate Bill 36 authorized commercial casino licenses in five specifically named cities, subject to local-option referendums. On November 3, 2020, voters in Bristol, Danville, Norfolk, and Portsmouth approved. Richmond voted no in 2021 and again in 2023, and the General Assembly reassigned Richmond's authorization to Petersburg in 2024. Rivers Casino Portsmouth opened January 23, 2023 as Virginia's first permanent commercial casino, with Hard Rock Bristol, Caesars Virginia in Danville, and HeadWaters in Norfolk following. Sports wagering launched January 21, 2021, after legislative authorization the prior year. iLottery launched July 1, 2020. The Virginia Racing Commission authorized historical horse racing (HHR) machines in 2018; Churchill Downs acquired Colonial Downs and the Rosie's Gaming Emporium HHR chain in 2022.
