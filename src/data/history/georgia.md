# Georgia — Gambling History

## Present-day posture (2026)
Georgia is a restrictive state with a single-vertical lottery monopoly plus a sprawling, regulatorily distinct **Coin-Operated Amusement Machine (COAM)** sector. Legal verticals: the **Georgia Lottery** (Powerball, Mega Millions, scratch, daily numbers, online iLottery (iHOPE accounts); administered by the **Georgia Lottery Corporation**, a public-private state-owned corporation); **Class B COAMs** — skill-based redemption gaming machines at convenience stores and gas stations awarding non-cash prizes (lottery tickets, gas, merchandise, additional plays) — regulated by the **Georgia Lottery Corporation, COAM Division** with criminal enforcement by the **Georgia Bureau of Investigation, Commercial Gambling Unit**; **charitable bingo and raffles** under O.C.G.A. §§ 16-12-50 to 16-12-58, enforced by the **GBI Charitable Gaming Section**. **No commercial casinos**, **no tribal gaming**, **no pari-mutuel horse or greyhound racing**, **no legal sports betting**, and **no licensed online casino**. Daily fantasy sports operates without specific authorization. The 1877 constitutional lottery prohibition (still in force) is the structural reason for this restrictive posture.[^1][^2]

## How Georgia got here
Colonial Georgia (founded 1733, the last of the original thirteen) was established under James Oglethorpe's charter that explicitly prohibited many vices including organized gambling. The **Constitution of Georgia of 1877** explicitly prohibited lotteries and the sale of lottery tickets — the controlling constitutional fact in Georgia gambling history. The 1877 prohibition has barred a state lottery without constitutional amendment, foreclosed pari-mutuel wagering on horse racing, and prevented commercial casino gaming. Subsequent constitutional revisions (1945, 1976, 1983) have not removed the underlying anti-lottery posture; only narrow exceptions have been carved out. In 1947 Governor Herman Talmadge signed legislation explicitly prohibiting wagering on sports, and illegal numbers ("bug" or "policy") games operated in Atlanta, Savannah, and other urban centers through the mid-20th century, sometimes linked to the "Dixie Mafia" organized-crime network.[^1][^2]

The first major exception came in **1976**, when constitutional amendment legalized **charitable bingo**, with implementing legislation creating a Charitable Gaming Section (eventually housed at the GBI). The second and dominant exception came in **November 1992**, when Governor Zell Miller's signature initiative — a constitutional amendment authorizing a state-run lottery dedicated to education funding — passed by ballot. The **Georgia Lottery Corporation** sold its first ticket on **June 29, 1993** under the Georgia Lottery for Education Act (O.C.G.A. § 50-27-1 et seq.). Lottery proceeds fund the **HOPE Scholarship** (Helping Outstanding Pupils Educationally, first awarded September 1993) and the **Pre-K Program** (free voluntary pre-kindergarten for all Georgia 4-year-olds). Georgia joined Powerball on April 8, 1995 and Mega Millions on March 15, 2010.[^3]

The third and most economically consequential exception is the **COAM sector**. Class A COAMs (traditional amusement — pinball, video games) were under Department of Revenue oversight; **Class B COAMs** (skill-based redemption awarding non-cash prizes) were nominally regulated but effectively unenforced prior to 2013. **HB 487** (signed by Governor Nathan Deal on April 10, 2013) transferred regulatory authority over Class A and Class B COAMs to the **Georgia Lottery Corporation**. In **August 2013**, the **Georgia Bureau of Investigation** created a **Commercial Gambling Unit** at the request of the Governor and the Lottery to enforce COAM laws. Class B COAMs may award **non-cash prizes** — typically lottery tickets, gas, store merchandise, or additional plays — but **not cash redemption**. The sector generates several hundred million dollars annually and is the principal pseudo-gambling industry in Georgia. Repeated efforts to authorize commercial casino "destination resorts" in Atlanta and Savannah (2017-2019) failed to clear the constitutional-amendment threshold; sports-betting bills (SB 386 / SR 538 in 2024 and parallel 2022, 2023, 2025 packages) have all failed.[^4][^5]

## Regulatory framework today
The **Georgia Lottery Corporation** ([galottery.com](https://www.galottery.com/)) — created by 1992 amendment and operated as a public-private state-owned corporation — administers the state lottery, the iLottery iHOPE account platform, and the COAM Division ([galottery.com/en-us/coam.html](https://www.galottery.com/en-us/coam.html)). The **Georgia Bureau of Investigation, Commercial Gambling Unit** ([investigative-gbi.georgia.gov](https://investigative-gbi.georgia.gov/investigative-offices-and-services/specialized-units/commercial-gambling-unit)) enforces COAM laws and criminal commercial gambling. **Charitable bingo and raffles** are regulated by the **GBI Charitable Gaming Section** under O.C.G.A. §§ 16-12-50 to 16-12-58. Problem-gambling treatment is administered by the **Georgia Department of Behavioral Health and Developmental Disabilities (DBHDD)** ([dbhdd.georgia.gov](https://dbhdd.georgia.gov/)), funded by an approximately **$400,000 annual contribution** from the Georgia Lottery Corporation. The contact line is the **Georgia Crisis and Access Line at 1-800-715-4225** (also accessible via 988). The **Georgia Council on Problem Gambling** is the NCPG state affiliate.[^6]

## Voluntary self-exclusion
Georgia's voluntary self-exclusion architecture is **narrow and lottery-product-specific** — a function of the state's narrow legal-gambling footprint. There is no statewide list covering casinos (none exist), no list covering sports betting (none exists), and no centralized list covering COAMs.

**Where to enroll**: the **Georgia Lottery Corporation Voluntary Self-Exclusion Program** is administered through the Lottery's iHOPE account system at **[galottery.com/en-us/myaccount/self-exclusion.html](https://www.galottery.com/en-us/myaccount/self-exclusion.html)**. The Lottery's published agreement document is at **[d1gszp1bmamha.cloudfront.net/content/dam/portal/pdfs/about-us/Voluntary_Self-Exclusion_Program.pdf](https://d1gszp1bmamha.cloudfront.net/content/dam/portal/pdfs/about-us/Voluntary_Self-Exclusion_Program.pdf)**. The iHOPE-account self-exclusion or time-out controls are available within the iLottery responsible-gaming hub at **[galottery.com/content/interactive/igaming/en_US/responsible-gaming/self-exclusion-and-timeout.html](https://galottery.com/content/interactive/igaming/en_US/responsible-gaming/self-exclusion-and-timeout.html)**. Enrollment is online through the iHOPE account.[^7]

**Duration options**: the Georgia Lottery offers **time-bounded suspension periods** through the iHOPE platform (typical menu — short timeout and longer self-exclusion periods of varying length); the published agreement notes that at the end of a selected time period for self-exclusion or suspending an iHOPE account, the player will not be able to access the account without taking further action. The specific menu (and whether a permanent option is available) is documented in the linked program agreement; exact duration tiers in 2026 are **[unconfirmed]** in published GLC materials reviewed for this brief. The Lottery treats account suspension and self-exclusion as separate but related controls.[^7]

**Scope**: the Lottery's VSE program covers **iHOPE-account activity** — meaning iLottery online play, second-chance promotions, marketing communications, and account-based deposits/withdrawals. Critically, the **playing of GLC games at physical retailer locations** (convenience stores, gas stations selling tickets) is **beyond the GLC's control and beyond the scope of the program** — the GLC explicitly disclaims this in the program agreement. Likewise, the VSE program does **not** cover **COAM gameplay** at convenience stores; the Lottery's COAM Division has not implemented a player-level exclusion list for COAM machines (a structural gap, given that COAMs are the most addictive-pattern-prone gambling product in widespread Georgia distribution). The VSE program does **not** cover charitable bingo (which has no centralized exclusion mechanism) and does not cover out-of-state online sportsbooks accessed by Georgia residents.[^7][^8]

**Removal**: at the end of the chosen suspension or self-exclusion period, the player must take affirmative action to re-enable the iHOPE account; access is not automatic. **Penalties for breach**: a self-excluded player cannot deposit, withdraw, purchase, or claim winnings on the iHOPE account, and cannot enter second-chance promotions. Lottery retail enforcement is limited by the disclaimer.[^7]

**Multi-state participation**: Georgia is **not** a signatory to MSIGA (no licensed iGaming poker). The **2026 state of the program** is **narrow and digital-only**, reflecting Georgia's restrictive legal footprint. The most significant gap — and a meaningful prevention-practice flag — is that **COAM players have no centralized regulator-administered self-exclusion option**; the GLC's $400,000/year contribution to DBHDD funds treatment and education through the Georgia Council on Problem Gambling rather than a COAM-specific exclusion mechanism. Helpline routing is through **1-800-715-4225** (Georgia Crisis and Access Line) and **1-800-GAMBLER**.[^6][^7][^8]
